## TL;DR

If you're experiencing homelessness, you are not disqualified from federal benefits, in fact, several programs have rules specifically designed to reach people without stable housing. Emergency SNAP can be issued within **7 days**. Medicaid can be approved the same day through presumptive eligibility. Veterans have a dedicated housing system separate from the civilian queue. This guide covers every major program available to people experiencing homelessness, how to apply without an address or ID, and what to do if you're in crisis right now.

> **Crisis resources first:** if you need emergency shelter tonight, call **211** (available 24/7 in most U.S. cities). For veterans: call the **VA National Call Center for Homeless Veterans** at 1-877-4AID-VET (1-877-424-3838). For the Benefind screener, go to [benefind.app/screening](/screening).

## Who this guide is for

This guide covers people who are:

- Sleeping outside, in a car, in a shelter, or in temporary housing
- "Couch surfing" or staying temporarily with others due to loss of housing
- Recently displaced from a home and staying in a hotel/motel temporarily
- Facing imminent eviction or housing loss within the next 14 days

All of these situations count as "homeless" or "at risk of homelessness" under federal program definitions. You don't need to be sleeping on the street to qualify for emergency benefits.

## Emergency SNAP: food within 7 days

SNAP (Supplemental Nutrition Assistance Program) has an emergency track called **Expedited SNAP** specifically for people in acute crisis. The rules are simple:

You qualify for expedited SNAP (issued within 7 calendar days) if:

- Your household's gross monthly income is under $150 **and** your liquid assets (cash, bank accounts) are under $100, **OR**
- Your monthly rent/utility costs exceed your monthly income and liquid assets combined, **OR**
- You're a migrant or seasonal farmworker with under $100 in liquid assets

People experiencing homelessness almost always meet criterion 1 or 2.

### What "no fixed address" means for SNAP

You do not need a permanent address to receive SNAP. When applying:

- Use the address of a shelter, friend's home, or your caseworker's office as your mailing address
- Many states allow you to pick up your EBT card in person at the SNAP office
- An EBT card works at any participating retailer regardless of where you applied

### What happens with the standard income test

The gross income limit for standard SNAP is 130% of the federal poverty level ($1,696/month for an individual in 2026). Most people experiencing homelessness have income well below this. The net income limit (100% FPL = $1,255/month for an individual) applies after deductions for a disabled or elderly household member.

### Maximum SNAP benefits in 2026

| Household size | Maximum monthly benefit |
| --- | --- |
| 1 | $292 |
| 2 | $536 |
| 3 | $768 |
| 4 | $975 |
| Each additional | +$220 |

**Restaurant Meals Program:** in some states (California, Arizona, Rhode Island, and others), homeless SNAP recipients can use their EBT card at participating restaurants. This accommodates people who can't cook. Ask your SNAP caseworker whether your state participates.

Apply at your local SNAP office or online through your state's benefits portal. For emergency expedited SNAP, visit in person and say explicitly: "I need expedited SNAP. I have no income and no food." That phrase triggers the 7-day processing track.

## Medicaid: same-day coverage through presumptive eligibility

Medicaid provides free healthcare (doctor visits, emergency care, prescriptions, mental health treatment, and substance use treatment) for people with low income. For people experiencing homelessness; it's often the most urgent need alongside food.

### Presumptive eligibility

Many states allow certain providers, hospitals, community health centers, shelters, and social service agencies, to make a preliminary Medicaid eligibility determination on the spot. This gives you **temporary Medicaid coverage immediately**, while your full application is processed.

Under presumptive eligibility, you can get care the same day you walk in. The temporary coverage lasts until your full application is processed (usually 30–60 days), at which point you're either enrolled permanently or your coverage ends.

### Income limits for full Medicaid (2026)

| State type | Monthly income limit (individual) |
| --- | --- |
| Medicaid expansion states (40 states + DC) | 138% FPL = $1,732/month |
| Non-expansion states | Varies significantly; often very restrictive |

People experiencing homelessness typically have income far below these limits and qualify for full Medicaid. Even in non-expansion states, emergency Medicaid is available for life-threatening conditions.

### Applying without an address

Like SNAP, Medicaid does not require a permanent address. Use:

- A shelter address
- The address of a community health center or social services agency
- A P.O. Box in some states

Community Health Centers (federally qualified health centers, or FQHCs) serve anyone regardless of ability to pay and are often the fastest path to care for unhoused people. Find one at findahealthcenter.hrsa.gov.

### Street outreach Medicaid enrollment

In many cities, Medicaid outreach workers will come to encampments, soup kitchens, and shelters to enroll people on-site. Ask a shelter or your local 211 whether this service is available.

## Emergency shelter programs

### How the shelter system works

Emergency shelters are operated by local nonprofits, faith organizations, and governments, funded largely through HUD's Emergency Solutions Grant (ESG) program. To access them:

1. **Call 211.** This is the universal access point. 211 dispatches to available shelter beds in your area in real-time.
2. **Go to your local Continuum of Care (CoC).** Every U.S. community has a CoC, a coordinated network of homeless service providers. The CoC coordinates the Coordinated Entry System (CES), which assesses need and prioritizes placement.
3. **Visit a warming or cooling center** in extreme weather. These are operated separately from the shelter system and are usually available walk-in, no assessment required.

### Coordinated Entry System

The Coordinated Entry System (CES) is the intake process for most HUD-funded housing programs. It typically involves:

1. A vulnerability or needs assessment (often the VI-SPDAT tool)
2. Placement on a prioritized list based on need
3. Referral to the appropriate housing intervention (shelter, transitional housing, rapid rehousing, or permanent supportive housing)

Priority is given to people with the longest history of homelessness and the most severe needs. Chronically homeless individuals, defined as those with a disability who have been homeless for 12+ months, or 4 or more occasions in the last 3 years totaling at least 12 months, receive the highest priority for permanent supportive housing.

## HUD homeless assistance programs

HUD funds several distinct programs through the Continuum of Care (CoC) program:

### Emergency Solutions Grant (ESG)

ESG funds emergency shelter, rapid rehousing, and homelessness prevention. Rapid rehousing provides short-term rental assistance (typically 3–12 months) plus case management to help people move from streets or shelters into housing quickly.

### Permanent Supportive Housing (PSH)

PSH provides long-term housing with wraparound services for chronically homeless individuals with disabilities. There's no time limit; it's designed to be a permanent solution. It's the most effective intervention for people with serious mental illness, substance use disorders, or other chronic conditions.

### HUD-VASH (Veterans Affairs Supportive Housing)

HUD-VASH is a joint HUD and VA program that provides Housing Choice Vouchers specifically for homeless veterans, combined with VA case management services. Unlike the general Section 8 waitlist (which can take years), HUD-VASH has dedicated vouchers and is often accessible faster for eligible veterans. See the veteran-specific section below for more.

### Continuum of Care (CoC) program grants

CoC grants fund locally coordinated housing and services. CoC programs in your area are listed at hudexchange.info/grantees. Your local CoC coordinator can connect you with resources you might not find by searching independently.

## VA homeless programs (for veterans)

If you are a veteran experiencing homelessness, you have access to an entirely separate, better-funded system than the civilian homeless system.

### VA programs for homeless veterans

| Program | What it provides |
| --- | --- |
| HUD-VASH | Housing Choice Vouchers + case management; no general Section 8 waitlist |
| SSVF (Supportive Services for Veteran Families) | Rapid rehousing, security deposits, utilities, case management |
| Grant and Per Diem (GPD) | Transitional housing up to 2 years for veterans working toward permanent housing |
| Healthcare for Homeless Veterans (HCHV) | Outreach, case management, and VA healthcare |
| VA Domiciliary (Dom) | Residential rehabilitation for veterans with mental illness or substance use disorders |
| National Call Center | 24/7 hotline: 1-877-4AID-VET connects to local VA homeless program |

**How to access VA services without documentation:** VA outreach workers are trained to work with veterans who have no ID, no discharge papers, and no fixed address. Call 1-877-4AID-VET and identify yourself as a veteran needing housing help. They will send an outreach worker or refer you to the nearest VA facility's Homeless Veterans coordinator.

Veterans do not need an honorable discharge to receive most VA homeless services. Veterans with other-than-honorable (OTH) discharges may still be eligible, contact the VA directly to determine your status.

## How to apply without an address or ID

This is the single biggest barrier for people experiencing homelessness, and it's often a solvable one.

### Applying without a permanent address

- **SNAP, Medicaid, SSI, SSDI:** All allow you to use a shelter, outreach organization, community health center, or even a caseworker's mailing address
- **Section 8:** Apply using any address you currently use for mail
- **VA programs:** No mailing address required to initiate contact; VA outreach workers can initiate enrollment

### Getting ID without an ID

Many states have special processes for obtaining identification for homeless individuals:

1. **Birth certificate:** Contact the vital records office in your birth state. Many states offer free or reduced-cost birth certificates for people experiencing homelessness. Some homeless service organizations will order it on your behalf.
2. **Social Security card:** Social Security offices can issue a replacement card (up to 3 per year, 10 lifetime) with alternative verification documents. Call 1-800-772-1213.
3. **State ID:** Once you have a birth certificate and Social Security card, you can apply for a state ID. Many states offer free state IDs for homeless individuals, ask your shelter or a case manager.
4. **ID clinics:** Many cities have nonprofit organizations that run free ID clinics specifically for homeless individuals, handling all of the paperwork at once. Ask 211 about ID assistance in your area.

### Voicing your needs to caseworkers

When you interact with a SNAP or Medicaid worker, explicitly state: "I am experiencing homelessness. I don't have a permanent address or ID. What documentation alternatives are available to me?" Workers are trained to handle this, but they may not volunteer the options; you need to ask.

## Other programs and resources

### LIHEAP (utility assistance)

If you're in transitional housing or a shelter, you may be able to receive LIHEAP assistance paid directly to the housing provider to cover utility costs. Ask your shelter staff or caseworker if they receive LIHEAP funds and how they're applied.

### SSI/SSDI for unhoused individuals with disabilities

If you have a disability and are experiencing homelessness, you may qualify for SSI or SSDI. SSA's Homeless Outreach Projects and Partnerships (HOPP) program deploys outreach workers to shelters, soup kitchens, and encampments to assist with applications. Ask your shelter about SSA outreach services.

### Substance use and mental health treatment

SAMHSA's National Helpline (1-800-662-4357) provides free, confidential referrals to local treatment programs, including those that serve homeless individuals. Medicaid covers substance use and mental health treatment in most states.

## Step-by-step: what to do right now

### Step 1: Call 211 for immediate shelter

If you need a place to sleep tonight, call 211 or text "211" in some states. This is the fastest path to a shelter bed.

### Step 2: Go to your nearest community health center

You can receive healthcare without insurance or identification at a federally qualified health center (FQHC). Find one at findahealthcenter.hrsa.gov. Staff at FQHCs can often help you apply for Medicaid same-day.

### Step 3: Apply for emergency SNAP in person

Go to your local SNAP office and ask for expedited SNAP. Bring any documentation you have, but you can apply with minimal documentation in a crisis. Benefits can be issued within 7 days.

### Step 4: Get connected to your Continuum of Care

Ask any shelter, community health center, or 211 operator to connect you with your local Continuum of Care (CoC) and get a Coordinated Entry System (CES) assessment. This is the entry point for most longer-term housing programs.

### Step 5: Start the ID process

Even if you can receive benefits without ID right now, having ID will make every future interaction easier. Ask your shelter or 211 about local ID clinics or organizations that assist with document recovery.

## Frequently asked questions

### Can I get SNAP if I have no income at all?

Yes. People with zero income are eligible for the maximum SNAP benefit for their household size ($292/month for an individual in 2026) and qualify for expedited SNAP within 7 days.

### Do I need to prove I'm homeless to access homeless services?

Most programs require a self-certification or attestation that you're homeless, a simple statement that you sign. A shelter intake form often serves as documentation. You generally don't need to prove it with external documents.

### Can I get Medicaid if I'm undocumented?

Undocumented immigrants are eligible for emergency Medicaid in all states, which covers emergency room treatment and stabilization. Some states (including California, Illinois, Washington, and New York) have expanded Medicaid to cover all income-eligible residents regardless of immigration status.

### What if the shelter is full?

Ask 211 about overflow capacity, warming/cooling centers, and church-based programs. Many cities activate emergency overflow capacity during extreme weather. During non-emergency times, a 211 operator can help identify available beds across the full regional system.

### What do I do if I'm fleeing domestic violence?

Call the National Domestic Violence Hotline at 1-800-799-7233. Domestic violence shelters operate separately from the general homeless system and often have priority placement. SNAP and other benefits can be received confidentially without your address appearing in any database accessible to your abuser. VAWA (Violence Against Women Act) protections apply.

### Can I receive SNAP at a shelter that provides meals?

Generally yes, but your benefit may be reduced. If a shelter provides all meals, your SNAP benefit is calculated differently. Ask your SNAP caseworker about the rules for "residents of institutions", the answer depends on whether the shelter is considered an institution under SNAP rules.

## The bottom line

Homelessness creates barriers to benefits, but those barriers are smaller than most people think. Emergency SNAP is available within 7 days with minimal documentation. Medicaid can be activated same-day through presumptive eligibility. Veterans have a dedicated, better-funded housing system. And the Coordinated Entry System exists specifically to connect people with the right program at the right time.

The key is knowing to ask. Call 211, walk into a community health center, and say explicitly what you need.
