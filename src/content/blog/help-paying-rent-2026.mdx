## TL;DR

If you're struggling to make rent in 2026, you are not out of options. Federal, state, and local programs cover everything from short-term emergency cash to long-term subsidized housing. The big ones: **Section 8 Housing Choice Vouchers**, **Emergency Rental Assistance (ERA)**, **HUD public housing**, **USDA rural housing**, and a patchwork of state and local programs that most people never find. This guide explains each program, who qualifies, how to apply, and, critically, what to do while you're on a waitlist.

## Section 8 Housing Choice Vouchers

Section 8, officially the **Housing Choice Voucher (HCV) program**, is the largest federal rental assistance program in the country. About 5 million households receive vouchers, which pay the difference between 30% of your income and the fair market rent for your area.

### Who qualifies

Eligibility is based on:

- **Income:** You must earn at or below 50% of your area's median income (AMI). By law, 75% of new vouchers must go to households at or below 30% AMI.
- **Household composition:** Families, the elderly, and people with disabilities are prioritized.
- **Citizenship:** At least one household member must be a U.S. citizen or eligible non-citizen.
- **Rental history:** Serious lease violations or prior evictions from federally assisted housing can disqualify you.

### How it works

Once you receive a voucher, you find a private landlord willing to accept it. The landlord must pass an HUD inspection. You pay roughly 30% of your adjusted income; the voucher covers the rest, up to a payment standard set by your local Public Housing Authority (PHA).

### How to apply

1. Find your local Public Housing Authority at [HUD's PHA directory](https://www.hud.gov/program_offices/public_indian_housing/pha/contacts).
2. Submit an application when the waitlist is open. **Most waitlists open rarely**, some for only a few days per year.
3. Check waitlist status regularly by contacting your PHA or checking their website.

### The waitlist reality

Section 8 waitlists are notoriously long, 1 to 10 years in most major metro areas. Some PHAs have closed waitlists entirely. Apply everywhere that has an open waitlist, even if it's a different city or county. Some households have received vouchers in lower-demand areas and used them in areas with more opportunity.

## Emergency Rental Assistance (ERA)

Emergency Rental Assistance programs were massively expanded during COVID and, while federal ERA funding has largely wound down, many states and localities still have active ERA programs funded through their own budgets or remaining allocations.

### What ERA covers

Depending on the program:

- Past-due rent (up to 12 months in some programs)
- Future rent (up to 3 months forward in some programs)
- Utilities and energy costs
- Rental application fees, security deposits, and moving costs in some programs

### Who qualifies

ERA eligibility typically requires:

- Income at or below 80% of AMI (Area Median Income)
- A risk of homelessness or housing instability (past-due rent, eviction notice, unsafe conditions)
- Some documentation of financial hardship

### How to find active ERA programs

Search "[your state or city] emergency rental assistance 2026" or visit the [National Low Income Housing Coalition's ERA tracker](https://nlihc.org/era) for state-by-state program status. Many counties and cities run their own ERA funds, your local Community Action Agency often administers them.

## HUD Public Housing

Public housing is government-owned and operated housing, distinct from the voucher program. HUD funds local PHAs to maintain and operate the properties; tenants pay 30% of their adjusted income.

### Who qualifies

The same income and citizenship rules as Section 8 apply. PHAs also consider:

- Age (some complexes are seniors-only)
- Family size (matched to available unit sizes)
- Criminal history (conviction screening varies widely by PHA)

### Applying

Apply through your local PHA, often the same office that handles Section 8. In many cities, a single application covers both programs. Waitlists for public housing are often shorter than Section 8 in rural areas but just as long in major cities.

## USDA Rural Housing Assistance

If you live outside a major metropolitan area, the **USDA Rural Development** programs are worth knowing about. The main ones for renters:

### Section 515 Rural Rental Housing

USDA-funded apartment complexes in rural areas. Tenants pay 30% of income. To find a property, search the [USDA Multi-Family Housing Property Search](https://www.rd.usda.gov/programs-services/multi-family-housing-programs).

### Section 521 Rental Assistance

A voucher-like subsidy within USDA-financed properties. You don't need to find a landlord, you apply to specific properties that have this subsidy available.

### Who qualifies for USDA programs

- Must live in a rural area (USDA defines this broadly, many towns up to 35,000 population qualify)
- Income at or below 80% of AMI
- U.S. citizenship or eligible non-citizen status

## State and local rent assistance programs

Beyond federal programs, every state has its own housing assistance landscape. A few common types:

| Program type | Where to look |
| ------------ | ------------- |
| State housing trust funds | State housing finance agency website |
| County emergency assistance | County social services or community action agency |
| Community Development Block Grants (CDBG) | City housing department |
| Faith-based and nonprofit rental help | 211.org (by phone or online) |
| Legal aid for eviction defense | Your state bar association |

**211 is underused.** Dial 2-1-1 from any phone or visit 211.org. It's a nationwide social services hotline that connects you to local programs that aren't always findable on Google. They can often identify programs not available through any federal database.

## Utility assistance: LIHEAP

Rent isn't the only housing cost. The **Low Income Home Energy Assistance Program (LIHEAP)** helps low-income households pay energy bills, heating, cooling, and sometimes water. Reducing your utility costs frees up money for rent.

### Who qualifies

Income at or below 150% of the federal poverty level (roughly $21,300/year for a single person, $43,800 for a family of four). States set exact limits; many use 60% of state median income instead, which can be higher.

### What it covers

- Heating bill payments (main benefit; typically $200 to $1,000 depending on state and income)
- Cooling assistance in summer (not all states)
- Crisis assistance when utilities are about to be shut off
- Weatherization referrals (making your home more energy-efficient)

### How to apply

Apply through your state's LIHEAP office or your local Community Action Agency. Many states open applications in the fall when heating season begins. Crisis assistance is usually available year-round. Find your local office at [liheap.acf.hhs.gov](https://www.acf.hhs.gov/ocs/programs/liheap).

## How to apply: a practical step-by-step

### Step 1: Get organized before you start

Gather:

- Photo ID for all adult household members
- Social Security numbers or documentation for everyone in the household
- Proof of income (last 30-90 days of paystubs, benefit award letters, tax returns)
- Lease or rental agreement
- Utility bills
- Any eviction notices or past-due rent notices
- Bank statements (some programs require them)

### Step 2: Apply for emergency assistance first

If you're facing eviction or utility shutoff, skip the waitlists and go straight to ERA and LIHEAP. These programs are designed for crisis situations. Also contact a local eviction defense legal aid organization, many jurisdictions have mandatory mediation or additional protections that can buy you time.

### Step 3: Apply to Section 8 and public housing while you wait

These programs take time, so start the process immediately even if you're currently housed. Being on a waitlist is free. Apply to multiple PHAs if their jurisdiction allows it.

### Step 4: Check state and local programs

Contact your local Community Action Agency (find it at [communityactionpartnership.com](https://communityactionpartnership.com)) and call 211. Local programs often have faster turnaround and less competition than federal ones.

### Step 5: Apply for SNAP if you haven't

SNAP won't pay rent, but freeing up grocery money is real money for rent. See our [SNAP eligibility guide](/blog/snap-eligibility-2026) for details.

## Waitlist strategies: what actually works

Most federal housing programs have long waitlists. This isn't a reason not to apply; it's a reason to apply strategically.

**Apply everywhere that's open.** PHAs in suburban or rural areas often have shorter waitlists than urban ones. A voucher can sometimes be "ported", transferred to be used in a different jurisdiction. Ask the PHA about portability when you apply.

**Update your contact information.** The single most common reason people lose their position on a waitlist is that the PHA can't reach them. Call your PHA every 6 months to confirm your contact info is current.

**Report life changes.** Income drops, new household members, or disability status can move you up the priority list in some PHAs. Don't assume the PHA knows.

**Ask about preferences.** Many PHAs give priority to veterans, people experiencing homelessness, domestic violence survivors, and people with disabilities. Make sure the PHA has recorded every preference you qualify for.

**Look for new waitlists opening.** Bookmark your PHA's website or sign up for email alerts. Some PHAs open waitlists for a week or less; if you miss the window, you wait for the next one.

## What to do while you're waiting

Rental assistance programs are long-term plays. Here's how to manage the gap:

**Negotiate directly with your landlord.** Many landlords prefer a payment plan over an eviction, the eviction process is costly and time-consuming for them too. Put any agreement in writing.

**Get help with back rent.** If you're behind on rent, look for one-time emergency funds at local nonprofits, churches, and mutual aid organizations. These don't require long eligibility processes and often move within days.

**Look into state renter protections.** Many states have notice periods, just-cause eviction requirements, and right-to-counsel programs that give you more time and legal standing than you might realize.

**Build income.** Programs like WIOA (Workforce Innovation and Opportunity Act) offer free job training; some offer stipends during training. See our [guide for recently unemployed individuals](/benefits-for/recently-unemployed) for more.

**Check Section 8 mobility programs.** Several metros offer counseling and support to help voucher holders move to higher-opportunity neighborhoods. If you eventually receive a voucher; this can make a significant difference in long-term stability.

## Frequently asked questions

### Can I get rent help if I'm currently employed?

Yes. Most programs are income-based, not employment-status-based. LIHEAP, ERA, and state emergency programs all serve working households below the income thresholds.

### What if my landlord won't accept Section 8?

In a growing number of states and cities, landlords cannot refuse to rent to Section 8 holders based on voucher status alone. Check your state or city law. If discrimination occurred, file a complaint with HUD or your state housing agency.

### Do I need to be on the verge of homelessness to qualify for emergency assistance?

Not always. ERA programs typically require documentation of financial hardship or "risk" of housing instability, which can include a pay cut, unexpected medical bills, or even an informal warning from a landlord. Apply and let the program determine eligibility, don't self-screen out.

### Can undocumented household members get help?

Most federal housing programs require at least one citizen or eligible non-citizen in the household. Some state and local programs have no citizenship requirement. Mixed-status households can sometimes receive prorated benefits. A local housing counselor can help you navigate this.

### How long does emergency rental assistance take?

Many ERA programs process applications in 2 to 6 weeks. Some have expedited tracks for active eviction cases. Apply as soon as a problem arises, don't wait until the eviction notice is served.

## The bottom line

Rental assistance exists at every level, federal, state, local, and nonprofit. The system is fragmented, which makes it hard to navigate, but it's not empty. Section 8 and public housing are long-game plays; emergency rental assistance and LIHEAP are for right now. Apply for both tracks simultaneously, call 211, and talk to a local Community Action Agency.
