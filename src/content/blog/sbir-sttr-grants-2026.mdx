## TL;DR

SBIR and STTR grants are the federal government's primary mechanism for funding early-stage technology companies. Phase I awards go up to $314,363 and Phase II awards up to $2.1 million, spread across 11 federal agencies. The authorization lapsed September 30, 2025 (the Senate passed reauthorization through FY 2031 in March 2026, awaiting House action). Overall Phase I success rates run around 17%, but agency-specific rates and topic selection make a big difference. This guide covers how the phases work, every participating agency, eligibility, SBIR vs. STTR, and the tips that separate winning proposals from the pile.

## What SBIR and STTR actually are

SBIR (Small Business Innovation Research) and STTR (Small Business Technology Transfer) are federally mandated programs that require participating federal agencies to set aside a portion of their research and development budgets for small businesses.

**SBIR** sets aside at least 3.2% of an agency's extramural R&D budget. The research must be primarily performed by the small business's own employees.

**STTR** sets aside at least 0.45% of the agency's extramural R&D budget. It requires the small business to partner with a nonprofit research institution (university, federally funded research center, or nonprofit research lab), which must perform at least 30% of the work.

Both programs originate from the Small Business Innovation Development Act of 1982 (15 USC Section 638) and have been reauthorized multiple times. They are not competitive grants in the traditional sense — each agency posts specific technology topics it wants to fund, and small businesses submit proposals directly responding to those topics. Funded companies retain full rights to commercialize the resulting intellectual property.

**These are not loans.** Awards are grants (Phase I and Phase II) with no equity dilution and no repayment obligation.

## Phase I, Phase II, and Phase III structure

### Phase I: Feasibility

Phase I is proof-of-concept funding. The agency wants to know if the proposed technology works and has commercial potential.

| Parameter | Details |
|---|---|
| Duration | 6-12 months |
| Award range | $50,000 - $314,363 (SBA maximum; agencies can exceed with justification) |
| NIH maximum | $314,363 |
| NSF maximum | $305,000 |
| DoD maximum | $50,000 - $250,000 (varies by component) |
| DOE maximum | $250,000 typical |
| DHS maximum | $175,000 |
| USDA maximum | $125,000 - $175,000 |
| Proposal requirements | 15-25 pages; technical approach, team qualifications, commercialization plan |
| Review timeline | 3-6 months after submission |

A strong Phase I application demonstrates: (1) the technical problem is real and significant, (2) your approach is feasible and novel, (3) you have the team to execute it, and (4) there is a real commercial market for the solution.

### Phase II: Full R&D

Phase II is full development funding. Agencies typically require a successful Phase I (though some agencies offer "Direct-to-Phase-II" options for companies with substantial prior work).

| Parameter | Details |
|---|---|
| Duration | 1-3 years |
| Award range | $500,000 - $2,095,748 (SBA maximum) |
| Phase II success rate from Phase I | ~50-60% overall; 30% at NIH |
| Commercialization weight | Heavily weighted in review |

Phase II applicants are evaluated on their Phase I results, technical progress, and most importantly, their commercialization plan. Agencies want funded technology to become real products, not academic papers.

### Phase III: Commercialization

Phase III receives no SBIR or STTR funds from the awarding agency. Instead, the company is expected to secure private investment, other federal contracts, or revenue. Some agencies have non-SBIR contracts available to Phase III companies, and the program has provisions requiring agencies to consider awarding sole-source or other-than-full-and-open-competition contracts to successful Phase II graduates.

Phase III is the goal: a company that can stand on its own.

First-time applicants often find NSF and NIH more accessible because their review criteria are more transparent and their program managers are more accessible.

## Eligibility requirements

To be eligible for SBIR, your company must:

- Be a **for-profit company** (sole proprietors, LLCs, C-corps, S-corps all qualify; nonprofits do not)
- Be **organized in the United States**
- Be **51% or more owned by U.S. citizens or permanent resident aliens** (or by another SBIR-eligible small business)
- Have **500 or fewer employees** (including affiliates)
- Have the **principal investigator (PI) primarily employed by the company** — the PI cannot be primarily employed by a university and moonlighting at your startup

For **STTR**, there are additional requirements:

- Must have a **formal cooperative R&D agreement** with a nonprofit research institution (university, national lab, or nonprofit research organization)
- The **research institution must perform at least 30%** of the work under the award
- The **small business must perform at least 40%** of the work
- The remaining 30% can go to either party or to additional subcontractors

## SBIR vs. STTR: which one should you apply for?

| Factor | SBIR | STTR |
|---|---|---|
| University partner required? | No | Yes (30%+ of work) |
| PI must be primarily employed by company? | Yes | No |
| Available agencies | All 11 | 6 (DoD, NIH, DOE, NSF, NASA, USDA) |
| IP rights | Company retains | Negotiated with research institution |
| Best for | Companies with in-house R&D capability | Early-stage companies with university spin-out technology or active academic collaborators |

SBIR is generally simpler and available through more agencies. STTR is the better path if you are commercializing technology from a university lab, if your PI is a faculty member who cannot leave their position to join the company full-time, or if partnering with a research institution gives you access to specialized equipment or expertise you would not otherwise have.

## How to apply

1. **Register on SAM.gov.** This is mandatory and can take 2-4 weeks if you do not already have an account. Do not wait until an application is due. Get your Unique Entity ID (UEI) while you are there.

2. **Create accounts on agency portals.** For NIH: eRA Commons. For NSF: Research.gov. For DoD: the Defense SBIR/STTR Innovation Portal (DSIP). For most others: Grants.gov and the agency-specific portal.

3. **Find open solicitations at SBIR.gov.** Each agency releases solicitations with specific topic areas they want funded. Read the topic descriptions carefully — proposals must directly respond to the agency's stated needs, not your preferred technology direction.

4. **Talk to the Program Manager.** This is the most underutilized step. Program managers are listed in solicitations and are explicitly available to answer pre-proposal questions. A 15-minute call can clarify whether your approach fits the topic and signal your seriousness.

5. **Write the proposal.** Technical approach, team qualifications, budget and budget justification, and commercialization plan. Format requirements vary by agency, but most Phase I proposals are 15-25 pages. Read the instructions exactly — reviewers check compliance before reading content.

6. **Submit before the deadline.** Agency portals have hard cutoffs. Submit at least 48 hours early to handle technical issues.

7. **Wait for review.** Phase I review cycles run 3-6 months. You will receive written review comments regardless of outcome — these are valuable for revision and resubmission.

8. **Phase II.** If awarded Phase I, agencies typically invite Phase II proposals from successful grantees. Some agencies (NIH, NSF) have structured Phase I-to-Phase II review processes; others are more discretionary.

## Tips for winning

**Match your proposal to the topic, not the technology.** Reviewers score proposals against specific evaluation criteria tied to the topic description. If your abstract reads like a product pitch without connecting to the agency's stated need, it will not score well.

**Lead with commercial potential.** Agencies are increasingly weighted toward commercialization. Describe the market size, your go-to-market plan, your initial customers, and your path to revenue. Reviewers are looking for a company, not a research lab.

**Have a credentialed PI.** A principal investigator with peer-reviewed publications in the relevant domain, relevant patents, or prior SBIR success dramatically improves your score. If your PI is a first-time applicant with limited credentials, consider bringing in an advisor who can demonstrate relevant expertise.

**Search prior award databases.** SBIR.gov lets you search all prior awards by agency, year, and keyword. Find successfully funded proposals in your topic area, read the titles and abstracts, and understand what the agency has funded before. Do not replicate a prior award — differentiate from it.

**Start with less-competitive agencies or topics.** NSF accepts more broadly across technology domains. Some DoD components (DHS, DOT) have smaller applicant pools. For a first-time applicant, a cleaner shot at a smaller award beats a competitive loss at a bigger program.

**Join a state SBIR assistance program.** At least 35 states offer free proposal review, coaching, or technical assistance to first-time SBIR applicants. These programs can dramatically improve your proposal quality at no cost.

**Do not miss registration deadlines.** SAM.gov registration lapses and delays have killed otherwise strong proposals. Keep your SAM.gov registration current.

## Authorization status (2026)

SBIR/STTR statutory authority expired September 30, 2025. Most agencies paused new solicitations during the lapse. DoD continued making awards under existing contracts.

On March 3, 2026, the Senate passed the Small Business Innovation and Economic Security Act, which would reauthorize SBIR/STTR through FY 2031 with strengthened reporting requirements and enhanced technology transfer provisions. The bill was awaiting House action as of April 2026.

Once reauthorized, agencies typically ramp back up within 60-90 days with new solicitations. Watch SBIR.gov for updates by agency.

## State-level supplements

Many states offer SBIR/STTR matching programs that provide additional funding when you receive a federal award:

- **Virginia:** Up to $100,000 in matching grants
- **Ohio:** Ohio Third Frontier SBIR/STTR Phase I Match up to $100,000
- **Maryland:** TEDCO SBIR/STTR Bridge funding
- **Indiana:** Phase I match up to $75,000
- **Kentucky, Kansas, Oklahoma, Michigan:** Matching grant programs (amounts vary)
- **North Carolina, New York:** Free proposal assistance and matching funds

These programs do not require a separate application cycle — you apply after receiving a federal SBIR/STTR award. Check your state's economic development agency for current availability.

## Frequently asked questions

### Is SBIR a grant or a contract?

It depends on the agency. NIH, NSF, USDA, and most civilian agencies issue grants. DoD typically issues contracts. Both involve no equity dilution and no repayment obligation, but DoD contracts carry different IP and reporting requirements than grants.

### Do I need a PhD to apply for SBIR?

No. The principal investigator must be primarily employed by the company and have relevant expertise, but there is no degree requirement. Industry experience and prior technical work can satisfy reviewers. That said, strong academic credentials on the team help your scores, especially at NIH and NSF.

### Can a startup with no revenue apply?

Yes. SBIR is designed for early-stage companies. Phase I is proof-of-concept funding — agencies expect applicants to be pre-revenue. You need a for-profit corporate entity, a registered PI, and a compelling technology approach.

### What is the Phase I to Phase II conversion rate?

On average, 50-60% of Phase I awardees go on to receive Phase II. NIH is lower (~30%). DoD varies by component. Conversion depends on Phase I technical results and, increasingly, on the quality of your commercialization plan.

### Can I apply to multiple agencies at once?

Yes, but only for different topics. Submitting substantially identical proposals to multiple agencies for the same work in the same period is not allowed. Different agencies have different topic areas, so it is possible (and common) to pursue SBIR funding from multiple agencies over time for different aspects of your technology.

### How long does it take from application to funding?

Phase I review cycles are 3-6 months after submission. Add 1-3 months for award processing. Plan on 6-9 months from submission to first dollar received.

### Does SBIR funding conflict with R&D tax credits?

Partially. You cannot claim the R&D tax credit on expenses funded by the SBIR grant. However, you can claim the credit on other qualified R&D expenses on the same project that are not covered by the grant. Work with a tax advisor to separate the two.

### What is the best agency for a first-time applicant?

NSF (America's Seed Fund) and NIH are commonly recommended for first-timers because their evaluation criteria are more transparent, their program managers are accessible, and their review comments are detailed. DHS and DOT have smaller applicant pools. DoD has the most money but also the most complex process and highly technical topic requirements.

## The bottom line

SBIR and STTR are the most accessible non-dilutive funding sources available to early-stage U.S. technology companies. Phase I awards in the $150,000-$314,000 range provide real runway; Phase II awards in the $1-2M range can fund a company's entire development cycle. The process demands rigorous proposal writing and direct engagement with agency program managers — but the rewards are substantial and the IP stays yours.
