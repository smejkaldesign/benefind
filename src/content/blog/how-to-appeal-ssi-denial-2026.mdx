## TL;DR

If the Social Security Administration denied your SSI claim, you have the right to appeal — and you should. About **65% of SSI appeals that reach an Administrative Law Judge hearing are won**. There are four levels of appeal, each with a 60-day deadline. Do not ignore a denial letter. This guide walks through every level, what to submit, common denial reasons, and when to bring in a disability lawyer.

## Why you should always appeal an SSI denial

The Social Security Administration denies roughly **65% of initial SSI applications**. That number sounds discouraging, but it reflects a broken process at the front end, not an accurate assessment of eligibility. Many denials are based on incomplete records, communication failures, or reviewers who never spoke with the applicant.

The appeals process exists precisely because the initial review is imperfect. At every level beyond the first, the approval rate climbs. If you stop at "denied," you're almost certainly leaving money on the table.

SSI (Supplemental Security Income) pays up to **$967/month in 2026** for individuals and **$1,450/month for couples**. Over the average claim period, giving up on a legitimate claim can mean tens of thousands of dollars lost.

## The 4 levels of SSI appeal

### Level 1: Reconsideration

**What it is:** A completely new review of your case by a different SSA examiner — not the one who originally denied you.

**Deadline:** 60 days from the date on your denial letter, plus 5 days for mail delivery (so effectively 65 days).

**Success rate:** Roughly 10–15%. Reconsideration has the lowest success rate of any level. Many advocates recommend treating it as a required step rather than a genuine chance for reversal, and focusing your energy on the ALJ hearing.

**How to request it:**
1. File Form SSA-561 (Request for Reconsideration) online at ssa.gov or at your local SSA office
2. Submit any new medical evidence or documentation you didn't include in your original application
3. Request a formal conference if you want to present your case in person (not available in all states)

**What to include:**
- A written statement explaining why you disagree with the decision
- Updated medical records, doctor's letters, or test results
- Any evidence the SSA missed or mischaracterized in the denial

**Tip:** Some states use a "disability hearing" model instead of paper-only reconsideration. If your state offers this, request an in-person hearing — it's a better format for presenting your case.

### Level 2: Administrative Law Judge (ALJ) Hearing

**What it is:** An in-person or video hearing before an independent Administrative Law Judge. This is where most successful appeals are won.

**Deadline:** 60 days from your Reconsideration denial letter (plus 5 days for mail).

**Success rate:** Approximately 50–55% for disability-related SSI cases. If you have strong medical evidence and a credible representative, rates climb to 65%+.

**Wait time:** ALJ hearings typically take 12–24 months to schedule. This is the biggest bottleneck in the system and why filing as early as possible matters.

**How to request it:**
1. File Form HA-501 (Request for Hearing by Administrative Law Judge) online, by mail, or in person
2. Request your hearing office location preference
3. Prepare your case or retain a disability attorney (see below)

**What happens at the hearing:**
- The ALJ reviews your complete file before the hearing
- You testify about your condition, daily limitations, and how your disability affects your ability to work (for SSI disability claims)
- The ALJ may call a vocational expert (VE) to testify about job availability — understanding how to counter VE testimony is where attorneys add major value
- Medical experts are sometimes called to review your records

**What to bring and submit:**
- All medical records from every treating physician for the past 12–24 months
- Statements from your doctors describing your functional limitations (RFC forms)
- Work history records
- Lay witness statements from family members or caregivers who can describe your daily limitations
- A personal statement about your typical day and how your condition affects you

**Critical tip:** Get your medical records to the ALJ **at least 5 business days before the hearing**. Evidence submitted after that point may not be considered.

### Level 3: Appeals Council Review

**What it is:** A review by the Social Security Appeals Council in Falls Church, Virginia. The council can approve your claim, send it back to an ALJ for a new hearing, or deny review entirely.

**Deadline:** 60 days from the ALJ denial letter (plus 5 days for mail).

**Success rate:** Low — roughly 5–10% of cases are reversed or remanded at this level. However, getting a "remand" (sent back to an ALJ) is itself a partial win because you get another hearing with a fresh look.

**What the Appeals Council reviews:**
- Whether the ALJ made a legal error in applying SSA rules
- Whether the decision was supported by substantial evidence
- New and material evidence that could not have been submitted earlier (with strict limits)

**What to include:**
- A brief statement identifying the specific legal or factual errors in the ALJ decision
- Any new evidence that is both new and material and relates to the period before the ALJ decision
- Reference to specific medical records or vocational expert responses that were mishandled

**Honest assessment:** If your ALJ hearing was lost on medical evidence grounds rather than legal error, the Appeals Council is not your best path. Federal court may offer more opportunity.

### Level 4: Federal District Court

**What it is:** A civil lawsuit filed in federal district court challenging the SSA's final decision.

**Deadline:** 60 days from the Appeals Council denial letter (plus 5 days for mail). This deadline is stricter — missing it almost always ends your case permanently.

**Success rate:** Approximately 40–45% of cases result in remand to the SSA. Outright reversals by federal courts are rare, but remands force a new hearing, which has its own success rate.

**What happens:**
- You (or your attorney) file a complaint in the federal district court in your jurisdiction
- The SSA files the administrative record with the court
- Both sides file briefs; there is no new testimony or evidence — the court reviews only what was in the SSA's record
- If you win, the court usually remands the case back to an ALJ with instructions

**When to pursue this level:** Only if you have a strong legal argument that the ALJ misapplied the law or that the decision is not supported by substantial evidence. Federal court is not a do-over — it's a legal review.

## The 60-day rule in plain language

Each level of appeal has the same core deadline structure:

| Appeal level | Deadline | Clock starts |
|---|---|---|
| Reconsideration | 60 days + 5 | From initial denial letter |
| ALJ Hearing | 60 days + 5 | From Reconsideration denial |
| Appeals Council | 60 days + 5 | From ALJ denial |
| Federal Court | 60 days + 5 | From Appeals Council denial |

The "+5 days" is for mail delivery. If you received the letter later than that, you can argue the date.

**Missing a deadline does not automatically end your case.** You can request an extension by showing "good cause" — serious illness, death in the family, lost mail, difficulty communicating with SSA. File the extension request as soon as you realize the deadline has passed. Don't wait.

If too much time has passed for an extension, you can file a new SSI application. You'll lose any potential back pay for the period between the original filing and the new one, but it preserves your claim going forward.

## Common reasons for SSI denial

Understanding why you were denied tells you what to fix on appeal.

### Medical/disability denials

| Reason | What it means | How to address it |
|---|---|---|
| "Not severe enough" | SSA doesn't believe your condition limits you enough | Submit function-specific records; get RFC forms from your doctors |
| "Ability to perform past work" | SSA says you can do jobs you've had before | Provide detailed evidence of how your condition prevents this |
| "Can adjust to other work" | A vocational expert identified jobs you could theoretically do | Counter with specific limitations your doctors have documented |
| "Insufficient medical evidence" | Not enough records to evaluate your claim | Get all treating physicians to submit records; request missing records |
| "Failed to follow prescribed treatment" | SSA says you didn't follow medical advice | Provide reasons: cost, side effects, religious objection |

### Financial/technical denials

| Reason | What it means | How to address it |
|---|---|---|
| Income over limits | Counted income incorrectly | Review what SSA counted; some income is excluded |
| Assets over limits | Counted resources that shouldn't count | Verify which assets are exempt in your situation |
| Not a U.S. citizen or eligible non-citizen | Citizenship/immigration status issue | Verify your status qualifies; some non-citizens are eligible |
| Living arrangement issue | SSA miscalculated your housing situation | Correct the record with documentation |

## Success rates by appeal level (2026 estimates)

| Level | Approval rate | Typical wait time |
|---|---|---|
| Initial application | ~35% | 3–6 months |
| Reconsideration | ~10–15% | 3–5 months |
| ALJ Hearing | ~50–55% | 12–24 months |
| Appeals Council | ~5–10% (remand or reversal) | 12–18 months |
| Federal Court | ~40–45% remand | 18–36 months |

The headline insight: if you're denied initially and denied at reconsideration, getting to an ALJ hearing is worth it. More than half of claimants who make it there win.

## When to get a disability lawyer

### You should consider a lawyer if:

- Your claim involves a complex medical condition or multiple conditions
- You've already been denied at Reconsideration
- You're heading into an ALJ hearing (this is the most attorney-effective level)
- A vocational expert has been scheduled for your hearing
- Your medical records are incomplete or your doctors haven't completed RFC forms
- You have a mental health condition as a primary disability
- You're applying for both SSDI and SSI at the same time

### How disability lawyers are paid

Disability lawyers work on **contingency** — meaning you pay nothing upfront. If you win, they receive **25% of your back pay, capped at $7,200** (the SSA-set maximum as of 2026). If you lose, you owe nothing.

This structure means a qualified attorney has no financial incentive to take on weak cases. If an attorney agrees to represent you, that's itself a signal your case has merit.

### Where to find a disability attorney

- **NOSSCR** (National Organization of Social Security Claimants' Representatives): nosscr.org
- **Legal aid organizations** in your area for free or sliding-scale help
- **Your state bar's lawyer referral service** for a free initial consultation

## Step-by-step: what to do right now

1. **Locate your denial letter** and find the date on it
2. **Count your 65-day window** from that date
3. **File the appeal request** (Form SSA-561 for Reconsideration, Form HA-501 for ALJ) online at ssa.gov — you do not need a lawyer to file
4. **Gather missing medical records** from every doctor who has treated your condition in the past 24 months
5. **Ask your treating physicians** to complete a Residual Functional Capacity (RFC) form describing what you can and cannot do
6. **Consider a disability attorney** especially if you're at the ALJ hearing level
7. **Document everything**: Keep copies of everything you send and receive

## Frequently asked questions

### Can I get SSI back pay if I win my appeal?

Yes. If you win on appeal, SSI back pay covers benefits from the date of your original application (or the established onset date of your disability) through the month before your approval. For ALJ hearings that take 18–24 months, this can be a significant lump sum.

### Should I re-apply while my appeal is pending?

Generally no — a new application may undermine your original filing date, which affects back pay. There are specific situations where re-applying makes sense (if your onset date has changed, or if you missed appeal deadlines), but discuss this with a representative first.

### What if I become unable to wait for the hearing?

Request a "critical case" designation if your situation has deteriorated — terminal illness, eviction, utility shutoff. The SSA has expedited review processes. Also check if you qualify for other emergency assistance through the [Benefind screener](/screening) while you wait.

### Can I work while appealing?

With SSI, income affects your benefit amount but there are earned income exclusions. During an appeal for a disability-based SSI denial, working can complicate things if the SSA argues it proves you can work. Talk to your attorney before accepting employment.

### What is a "fully favorable" vs. "partially favorable" decision?

A fully favorable decision gives you everything you asked for. A partially favorable decision approves your claim but with a different onset date (which reduces back pay) or different benefit amount. You can appeal a partially favorable decision if you believe the onset date is wrong.

## The bottom line

SSI denials are common and often wrong. The appeals process is designed for this. File your appeal before the 65-day window closes, gather strong medical evidence, and get an attorney for your ALJ hearing. The system rewards persistence.
