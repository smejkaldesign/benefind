## The funding landscape most business owners miss

When founders think about government money, they usually think "grants" and then assume they don't qualify. That's the wrong frame. The business funding landscape in 2026 includes:

- **Tax credits** — reduce what you owe dollar-for-dollar (R&D credit, WOTC, ITC)
- **Competitive grants** — direct funding for specific activities (SBIR, STEP, USDA)
- **Contracting set-asides** — reserved federal contracts for qualifying businesses (SBA 8(a))
- **Incentive zones** — preferential tax treatment for investing in specific locations (Opportunity Zones)
- **State workforce programs** — subsidized training costs when you hire or upskill employees

Most businesses qualify for at least two or three of these. A business doing any R&D, hiring from disadvantaged groups, or located in a rural or distressed area may qualify for five or more.

## R&D Tax Credit

The Research and Development Tax Credit is the most underused business tax credit in the U.S. — and also one of the most valuable.

**Who it's for:** Any business spending money on developing or improving products, software, processes, or formulas. You don't need a lab coat. If you're a software startup, a food company testing new recipes, or a manufacturer improving a production process, you likely qualify.

**What it's worth:** Up to 20% of qualifying research expenses above a base amount. For most businesses, the effective credit runs 6–8% of total R&D spend. That's $60,000–$80,000 on $1 million in qualifying expenses. Startups with no tax liability can offset up to $500,000 in payroll taxes per year through the Payroll Tax Election.

**How to claim it:** Filed with your federal tax return on Form 6765. Requires documentation of qualifying activities — typically time tracking, payroll records, and contractor invoices.

## SBIR and STTR grants

The Small Business Innovation Research (SBIR) and Small Business Technology Transfer (STTR) programs are the federal government's primary mechanism for funding early-stage R&D in small businesses.

**Who it's for:** U.S.-based small businesses (under 500 employees) doing technology-focused research and development. STTR requires a partnership with a research institution; SBIR does not.

**What it's worth:** Phase I awards run $50,000–$275,000 and fund 6–12 months of feasibility research. Phase II awards run $750,000–$2,000,000 and fund full R&D execution. There's no equity component — this is non-dilutive funding.

**How to apply:** Through specific federal agencies (NIH, DOD, DOE, NSF, and others). Each agency publishes solicitations. Deadlines are agency-specific and vary throughout the year.

Full guide: [SBIR/STTR Grants 2026](/blog/sbir-sttr-grants-2026)

## Work Opportunity Tax Credit (WOTC)

WOTC rewards employers for hiring workers from groups that face significant barriers to employment.

**Who it's for:** Any employer that hires from one of the ten WOTC target groups: veterans, TANF recipients, SNAP recipients, SSI recipients, long-term unemployed individuals, ex-felons, vocational rehab referrals, Empowerment Zone residents, and others.

**What it's worth:** $2,400–$9,600 per qualifying hire, depending on the target group and hours worked. Veterans with service-connected disabilities can generate up to $9,600 per hire.

**How to claim it:** Pre-certification required. File IRS Form 8850 within 28 days of the employee's start date. Credit claimed on your annual tax return.

Full guide: [WOTC Tax Credit 2026](/blog/wotc-tax-credit-2026)

## SBA 8(a) Business Development Program

The 8(a) program is a nine-year business development program for small businesses owned by socially and economically disadvantaged individuals. The main benefit: access to sole-source federal contracts and set-aside contract competitions worth billions of dollars.

**Who it's for:** Small businesses where at least 51% is owned and controlled by individuals who are socially disadvantaged (often racial or ethnic minority group members) and economically disadvantaged (net worth under $850,000, adjusted gross income under $400,000, assets under $6.5 million).

**What it's worth:** Access to over $9 billion in 8(a) set-aside contracts annually. Sole-source contracts can be awarded without competition for up to $4.5 million for services and $7 million for manufacturing.

**How to apply:** Through the SBA's MySBA portal. The application process is thorough — plan for 4–8 months. Certification lasts nine years.

Full guide: [SBA 8(a) Program 2026](/blog/sba-8a-program-2026)

## Clean Energy Investment Tax Credit (ITC)

The Inflation Reduction Act extended and expanded the Investment Tax Credit for clean energy installations — making it one of the most valuable credits available to businesses investing in energy infrastructure.

**Who it's for:** Businesses installing solar, wind, battery storage, geothermal, fuel cell, microgrid, or other qualifying clean energy systems. Bonus credits available for projects in low-income communities, energy communities (former fossil fuel areas), or using domestic content.

**What it's worth:** 30% base credit on the full cost of the qualifying system, including installation. With bonus adders (domestic content, energy community, low-income), total credits can reach 50–70% of project cost. On a $500,000 solar installation, that's $150,000–$350,000 in tax credits.

**How to claim it:** Claimed on Form 3468 with your federal tax return. Some credits can be transferred or sold to third parties under new IRA provisions.

Full guide: [Clean Energy ITC for Businesses 2026](/blog/clean-energy-itc-2026)

## Opportunity Zones

Opportunity Zones are designated low-income census tracts where investments receive preferential capital gains treatment under federal law.

**Who it's for:** Investors and businesses deploying capital into qualifying Opportunity Zone businesses or real estate. The investor must have realized capital gains to defer; the business receiving investment must operate substantially within a Qualified Opportunity Zone.

**What it's worth:** Deferral of original capital gains tax until 2026 or sale of the Opportunity Fund investment (whichever comes first), plus permanent exclusion of gains earned on the Opportunity Zone investment itself if held for 10+ years.

**How to structure it:** Investment must flow through a Qualified Opportunity Fund (QOF). Most businesses work with a tax attorney to establish the QOF and ensure the investment meets the Opportunity Zone business requirements.

Full guide: [Opportunity Zones 2026](/blog/opportunity-zones-2026)

## STEP Export Grants

The State Trade Expansion Program (STEP) helps small businesses enter or expand into export markets by covering eligible costs.

**Who it's for:** Small businesses (SBA size standards) looking to export goods or services. Specific eligibility varies by state — most require the business to be headquartered in the state and have been in operation for at least one year.

**What it's worth:** Most states award $5,000–$25,000 in matching or reimbursement grants per business per year. Eligible costs include trade show fees, foreign market sales trips, international website translation, product compliance certifications, and more.

**How to apply:** Through your state's STEP administering agency (often the state's economic development office or department of commerce). Grant amounts and eligible activities vary significantly by state.

Full guide: [STEP Export Grants 2026](/blog/step-export-grants-2026)

## USDA Rural Business Grants

The USDA runs several grant and loan programs specifically for businesses in rural areas — defined generally as communities with populations under 50,000.

**Who it's for:** Businesses, cooperatives, and nonprofit organizations in rural areas. Key programs include the Rural Business Development Grant (RBDG) and the Rural Energy for America Program (REAP), among others.

**What it's worth:** RBDG awards typically run $10,000–$500,000 for business development, training, and technical assistance. REAP provides grants covering up to 25% of eligible project costs for renewable energy and energy efficiency projects, plus loan guarantees.

**How to apply:** Through your local USDA Rural Development state office. Application cycles vary by program — most have one to two application windows per year.

Full guide: [USDA Rural Business Grants 2026](/blog/usda-rural-business-grants-2026)

## State Workforce Training Grants

Nearly every state runs employer-facing workforce training programs that reimburse or subsidize the cost of training new hires and incumbent workers. The programs go by different names — customized job training, incumbent worker grants, apprenticeship incentives — but the structure is similar.

**Who it's for:** Employers in manufacturing, healthcare, technology, construction, logistics, and other priority industries. Eligibility often requires the employer to be creating new jobs or upskilling existing workers, and to be located in the state.

**What it's worth:** Varies widely. Many states reimburse $1,000–$5,000 per employee trained. Some states offer much larger grants for major job creation events (typically $500K+ for large manufacturers committing to significant headcount growth).

**How to apply:** Through your state workforce development agency. The Benefind screener surfaces the specific programs available in your state.

## Frequently asked questions

### Do government grants for small businesses have to be repaid?

True grants do not have to be repaid. However, some programs (like USDA programs) are structured as loan guarantees or forgivable loans rather than pure grants — read the terms carefully. Tax credits reduce your tax liability but don't require repayment as long as you meet the program requirements during the credit period.

### Can I apply for multiple programs at the same time?

Yes. There's no restriction on stacking multiple programs, and in practice the highest-value outcomes come from combining credits (R&D + WOTC + ITC, for example). The only exception: some programs explicitly exclude businesses receiving other federal funding for the same project.

### My business is pre-revenue. Can I still qualify?

Yes, for several programs. SBIR/STTR is designed for pre-revenue R&D companies. The R&D Payroll Tax Election was specifically created so startups with no tax liability can still monetize the credit. STEP grants only require 12 months in operation in most states, not profitability.

### How long does it take to get approved?

Tax credits: available when you file your tax return (no pre-approval needed for most). SBIR: 3–9 months from application to award. SBA 8(a): 4–8 months for certification. USDA grants: typically 3–6 months. State programs vary.

### What documentation do I need?

It varies by program. For R&D credit: payroll records, time tracking, contractor agreements, and project documentation. For WOTC: Form 8850 filed within 28 days of hire.
