## TL;DR

College students are generally excluded from SNAP — but in 2026, that exclusion has more exceptions than ever. If you're working 20+ hours a week, receiving work-study, caring for a dependent, or participating in certain state job training programs, you may qualify. This guide explains exactly which students get in, why the exclusion exists, and what to do if you're on the edge.

## Why students are mostly excluded from SNAP (and why it matters)

SNAP was designed for working-age adults and families who are in a state of economic hardship. Congress created the student exclusion in 1977 based on the theory that college students have voluntarily chosen a path that temporarily reduces their income — and that they have resources (family support, student loans, future earning potential) that other low-income people don't have.

The practical reality in 2026 is more complicated. Many college students are financially independent, supporting families, working significant hours, and genuinely food insecure. The pandemic-era expansion of student eligibility rules partially addressed this, and while some of those expansions have expired, the remaining exemptions are broader than they were before 2020.

Here's what actually determines whether you can get SNAP as a student.

## The baseline: who the student exclusion applies to

If you are enrolled **at least half-time** in a college, university, or other institution of higher education, you are subject to the student eligibility rules. This includes:

- Four-year colleges and universities
- Community colleges
- Trade and vocational schools at the postsecondary level

**Half-time** is defined by the school — typically 6 credit hours per semester for undergraduates.

If you are enrolled less than half-time, the student exclusion does not apply. You would be evaluated under standard SNAP eligibility rules.

## The exemptions: when students DO qualify

Even if the exclusion applies to you, you can still get SNAP if you meet at least one of the following exemptions. These are federal rules — they apply in every state.

### Exemption 1: You work at least 20 hours per week

If you are employed and working an average of 20 or more hours per week, you're exempt from the student exclusion. Part-time jobs count as long as the hours are real and consistent.

**What counts:** W-2 employment, self-employment (with verifiable income), and in some states, gig economy work with documentation. Federal work-study does not count toward this 20-hour threshold (it has its own exemption — see below).

**Documentation needed:** Recent pay stubs or employer verification of hours. If you're self-employed, income records and a statement of typical hours.

### Exemption 2: You participate in federal work-study

If you've been awarded federal work-study as part of your financial aid package, you qualify for SNAP regardless of how many hours you actually work.

**What counts:** Federally-funded work-study positions. State-funded work-study varies — some states count it, some don't.

**Documentation needed:** Your financial aid award letter showing work-study, or a letter from your financial aid office confirming work-study eligibility (even if you haven't started working yet).

### Exemption 3: You have a dependent child under 12

If you are a student who is also the parent or caretaker of a dependent child under the age of 12, and you don't have adequate child care to enable you to work 20+ hours per week, you're exempt from the student exclusion.

**Important nuance:** You don't have to prove you can't find child care — the law presumes it if your child is under 6. For children 6–11, you need to show you can't find adequate child care.

### Exemption 4: You receive TANF

If you receive Temporary Assistance for Needy Families (TANF), you're exempt from the student exclusion. This most commonly applies to parents who are student recipients of TANF.

### Exemption 5: You're physically or mentally unfit for employment

Students who are incapacitated (as recognized by SNAP) are exempt. This applies to students with documented disabilities that prevent full-time work.

### Exemption 6: You're enrolled in a state-approved workforce training program

Some schools and programs are specifically designated by their state SNAP agency as job-skills training programs for low-income individuals. Students enrolled in these programs are exempt.

**What counts:** Programs specifically approved by your state SNAP agency. This is not the same as a regular college program. Ask your state SNAP agency whether your school or program is on the approved list.

## What happens if you meet an exemption

If you meet at least one exemption, you're evaluated under standard SNAP rules:

- **Income limit (gross):** 130% of the Federal Poverty Level. In 2026, that's $1,695/month for a household of 1, $2,290 for a household of 2.
- **Income limit (net):** 100% of the FPL after deductions for housing, dependent care, and other allowable expenses.
- **Asset limit:** Most states have eliminated or relaxed asset tests. A few still apply a $2,750 limit for households without elderly or disabled members.

See the full [SNAP eligibility guide for 2026](/blog/snap-eligibility-2026) for complete income tables, deductions, and benefit amounts.

## How much SNAP could you get?

If you qualify, benefit amounts are based on your net income and household size. In 2026, the maximum monthly SNAP benefit is:

| Household size | Maximum monthly benefit |
|---|---|
| 1 person | $292 |
| 2 people | $536 |
| 3 people | $768 |
| 4 people | $975 |

Most qualifying students receive somewhere between $100–$292 per month as a single-person household. Students with dependents can receive higher amounts.

## The household question: can you be your own household?

This is where student SNAP applications often get complicated.

If your parents claim you as a tax dependent, you may need to be included in their household for SNAP purposes — which means their income counts against your eligibility, even if they don't actually support you.

**The key question:** Do you purchase and prepare food separately from your parents? If you live in a dorm or separate apartment and buy your own groceries, you can generally be your own SNAP household regardless of your parents' tax filing. Document this with a separate lease agreement or utility bill if possible.

If you're living at home with your parents and sharing meals, you're likely part of their household.

## State-specific expansions to know about

Several states have added rules that expand student eligibility beyond the federal baseline. These are separate from federal law and vary significantly.

- **California** expanded student eligibility significantly after the pandemic. Students receiving financial aid who meet income limits may qualify under state rules even without meeting a federal exemption. Verify with your county SNAP office or run the Benefind screener.
- **Washington** and **New York** have similar expansions for students in approved training programs.

The [Benefind screener](/screening) accounts for state-specific expansions when evaluating your eligibility — the generic federal rules alone may not give you the full picture.

## How to apply

SNAP applications are processed by your state SNAP agency, not your school. Here's how to apply:

1. **Determine your household:** Are you an independent household or part of your parents' household?
2. **Identify your exemption:** Which of the six exemptions above do you meet? Gather documentation.
3. **Apply:** Go to your state's online SNAP portal (find it at benefits.gov or through the Benefind screener). Most states have online applications that take 15–30 minutes.
4. **Attend your interview:** Most states require a phone interview as part of the application. Have your documents ready: pay stubs, work-study award letter, lease agreement, school enrollment verification.
5. **Respond quickly to document requests:** The faster you send required documents, the faster your application is processed. Benefits are typically backdated to your application date.

If you're approved, benefits are loaded to an EBT card that works at most grocery stores and farmers' markets.

## Frequently asked questions

### I have a work-study award but haven't started working yet. Does that still count?

Yes. The federal rules say you need to be "eligible" for work-study, not that you need to have actually worked any hours. Get a letter from your financial aid office confirming your work-study eligibility as part of your aid package.

### I'm a graduate student. Does the student exemption apply to me?

Yes. The student exclusion applies to anyone enrolled at least half-time at a postsecondary institution — undergraduate and graduate alike. You need to meet one of the same exemptions.

### My parents still claim me as a tax dependent but I live in my own apartment. Am I their household?

Not necessarily. SNAP household rules are based on who purchases and prepares food together, not on tax dependency. If you live separately and buy your own food, you can apply as your own household. You'll need to document your separate living situation — a lease or sublease agreement in your name is the strongest evidence.

### What if I'm denied? Can I appeal?

Yes. Denials are common for student applicants because caseworkers sometimes apply exemptions incorrectly. You have the right to a fair hearing. Request it in writing within 90 days of the denial notice. Having documentation of your exemption clearly organized will significantly help your appeal.

### Does SNAP report to my school or affect my financial aid?

No. SNAP eligibility and usage is not reported to your school, and it does not affect your federal financial aid eligibility. It's a separate system.
