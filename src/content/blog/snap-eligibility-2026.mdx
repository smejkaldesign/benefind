## TL;DR

SNAP (the Supplemental Nutrition Assistance Program, formerly food stamps) pays for groceries for about 41 million people every month. To qualify in 2026, your household's gross income usually needs to be at or below **130% of the federal poverty level** — roughly **$1,696/month for a single person** or **$3,485/month for a family of four**. There are also asset tests in some states and work requirements for able-bodied adults. This guide walks through every rule, every exception, and exactly how to apply.

## What SNAP is (and what it isn't)

SNAP is a federal program administered by state agencies. You get a monthly benefit loaded onto an EBT card (Electronic Benefits Transfer), which works like a debit card at grocery stores, most farmers' markets, and many online grocery retailers. The average household benefit in 2026 is about **$230/month**, and the maximum for a family of four is **$975/month**.

SNAP **can** be used on:

- Fruits, vegetables, meat, dairy, bread, cereal
- Seeds and plants that produce food
- Snack foods, non-alcoholic drinks
- Online grocery orders from approved retailers (Walmart, Amazon, many regional chains)

SNAP **cannot** be used on:

- Alcohol, tobacco
- Hot prepared food (with exceptions for elderly/homeless recipients in participating states)
- Vitamins, supplements, medicines
- Pet food, paper goods, household supplies

## The income test — the single most important rule

Almost every household that qualifies for SNAP does so by meeting both the gross income test and the net income test. A few categories (households with an elderly or disabled member, categorically eligible households) skip the gross income test.

### Gross income limits for 2026

Gross income is your household's income before any deductions. Here are the 2026 limits, which run from October 2025 through September 2026.

| Household size         | Gross monthly income (130% FPL) | Gross annual income |
| ---------------------- | ------------------------------- | ------------------- |
| 1                      | $1,696                          | $20,352             |
| 2                      | $2,292                          | $27,504             |
| 3                      | $2,888                          | $34,656             |
| 4                      | $3,485                          | $41,820             |
| 5                      | $4,081                          | $48,972             |
| 6                      | $4,677                          | $56,124             |
| 7                      | $5,274                          | $63,288             |
| 8                      | $5,870                          | $70,440             |
| Each additional person | +$597                           | +$7,164             |

Alaska and Hawaii have higher limits because their cost of living is higher.

### Net income limits

If you pass gross, your net income also has to be at or below 100% of the federal poverty level. "Net" means after SNAP deducts things like:

- A standard deduction (varies by household size)
- 20% of earned income
- Dependent care costs
- Medical expenses over $35/month for elderly/disabled members
- Child support paid to someone outside the household
- Excess shelter costs (rent/mortgage + utilities above half your income, up to a cap)

These deductions often push households below the net limit even when they look over the gross limit at first glance.

### Categorical eligibility

Most states have something called **Broad-Based Categorical Eligibility (BBCE)**. If you qualify for TANF-funded services (even non-cash services), you're automatically over the gross income test. BBCE lets states raise the gross income limit to **200% FPL** in many cases. Check your state's SNAP website — it matters a lot.

## Asset limits

Here's where it gets complicated. The federal asset limit is $3,000 for most households and $4,500 for households with an elderly or disabled member. But most states have **dropped the asset test entirely** through BBCE. The ones that still enforce it tend to be the same states that didn't expand Medicaid.

States that **still apply asset tests as of 2026** include Georgia, Idaho, Kansas, Missouri, South Dakota, Tennessee, Texas, and Wyoming — though rules change frequently. A few things to know:

- Your primary home doesn't count
- One car is exempt in most states
- Retirement accounts (401k, IRA) are exempt in almost every state
- Checking, savings, CDs, and stocks count

## Work requirements

This is the rule most people get wrong. There are two different work requirements.

### The "general" SNAP work requirement

Anyone 16–59 must register for work, accept a suitable job if offered, and not voluntarily quit or cut hours without good reason. Almost everyone meets this — you don't have to actively look for work unless you're enrolled in a SNAP employment & training program.

### The ABAWD rule (Able-Bodied Adults Without Dependents)

This is the strict one. If you're between **18 and 54** (yes, the upper age got raised from 49 in the 2023 farm bill changes) and have **no dependents under 18**, you can only get SNAP for **3 months out of every 36** unless you're:

- Working at least 20 hours a week
- In a qualifying training program at least 20 hours a week
- Pregnant
- Medically unable to work
- Caring for a disabled household member
- A veteran, homeless person, or aging-out-of-foster-care youth up to age 24 (new exemptions)

States can also request waivers for areas with high unemployment.

**This is the rule that knocks a lot of people off SNAP.** If you're an ABAWD and you're not working or training enough hours, your state will start a 3-month clock.

## Special rules for students, seniors, and immigrants

### College students

College students enrolled at least half-time have extra rules. You generally don't qualify unless you meet an exception: working 20+ hours a week, caring for a dependent under 6, receiving TANF, participating in work-study, or enrolled in certain career and technical programs.

### Seniors and people with disabilities

Households with someone 60+ or receiving disability benefits have looser rules:

- Skip the gross income test
- Higher asset limit
- Special medical deduction
- Can use SNAP at participating restaurants (Restaurant Meals Program) in some states

### Immigrants

Lawful permanent residents generally qualify after 5 years in the country. Refugees, asylees, trafficking victims, and some others qualify immediately. U.S.-citizen children of undocumented parents are eligible regardless of their parents' status.

## How much will I actually get?

SNAP benefits are calculated as:

**Maximum benefit for your household size − (30% × net income) = your monthly benefit**

The 2026 maximum benefits:

| Household size         | Max monthly benefit |
| ---------------------- | ------------------- |
| 1                      | $292                |
| 2                      | $536                |
| 3                      | $768                |
| 4                      | $975                |
| 5                      | $1,158              |
| 6                      | $1,390              |
| 7                      | $1,536              |
| 8                      | $1,756              |
| Each additional person | +$220               |

So a family of 4 with $0 net income gets the full $975. A family of 4 with $1,500 net income gets $975 − (0.30 × $1,500) = $525/month.

Minimum benefit for 1- and 2-person households is $24/month.

## How to apply — step by step

### Step 1: Find your state's SNAP agency

Every state runs SNAP through a different agency. Search "SNAP [your state]" or go to usda.gov/snap/state-directory. You'll find an online application in almost every state now.

### Step 2: Gather your documents

You'll need:

- ID for every household member (driver's license, passport, birth certificate, or school ID)
- Social Security numbers (or ITINs) for everyone applying
- Proof of income (last 4 weeks of paystubs, self-employment records, unemployment letter)
- Proof of rent/mortgage (lease, mortgage statement)
- Proof of utilities (electric, gas, water, phone bills)
- Proof of child care costs if applicable
- Medical bills if household has elderly or disabled members

### Step 3: Submit the application

Most states let you apply online in 20–30 minutes. Paper applications still work. Some states let you apply in person same-day if you're in a crisis. You can submit the application with missing information and fill in gaps later.

### Step 4: Interview

After you apply, you'll be contacted for an interview — usually by phone, occasionally in person. This is where they verify the numbers on your application. Be honest, keep it short, and ask clarifying questions if you're unsure of anything.

### Step 5: Get your EBT card

If approved, you'll get your card in the mail within 7–10 days. Benefits hit monthly on a day that depends on your case number or last name. Emergency SNAP (for households with under $150/month in gross income or under $100 in assets) is issued within 7 days.

## Frequently asked questions

### How long does SNAP approval take?

Standard applications are processed within 30 days. Expedited (emergency) applications for households in crisis get approved within 7 days.

### Can I apply for SNAP if I have a job?

Yes. Most SNAP recipients are in working households. SNAP is designed to supplement low wages, not replace them.

### What if I'm denied?

Appeal. You have 90 days to request a fair hearing in most states. Denials based on income miscalculations or missing documents are common and often reversed on appeal.

### Do I have to recertify?

Yes. Most households recertify every 6 or 12 months. You'll get a notice in the mail a month before your case is up. Miss the recertification and you lose benefits until you reapply.

### What happens if my income changes mid-certification?

Report income changes to your state agency. If your income drops, your benefit usually goes up. If it increases significantly (often defined as exceeding 130% of FPL), your benefit decreases or ends.

### Does SNAP count as income for other benefits?

No. SNAP is not counted as income for Medicaid, housing assistance, SSI, or most other programs.

## The bottom line

If your household earns less than 130% of the federal poverty level, you probably qualify for SNAP. The application takes about 30 minutes, approval takes a month, and the average benefit is worth about $2,760 a year — more if you're a larger household.
