## TL;DR

WIC (the Special Supplemental Nutrition Program for Women, Infants, and Children) provides free nutritious food, nutrition counseling, and healthcare referrals to pregnant women, new mothers, infants, and children under 5. The income limit is **185% of the federal poverty level** — about **$61,050/year for a family of four**. WIC serves roughly **6.3 million people per month** and covers approximately half of all infants born in the U.S. Unlike most federal programs, WIC has **no citizenship requirement** — all immigration statuses are welcome.

## What WIC is

WIC stands for the Special Supplemental Nutrition Program for Women, Infants, and Children. It's run by the USDA's Food and Nutrition Service and administered through approximately **10,000 clinic sites** across all 50 states, D.C., 34 Indian Tribal Organizations, and 5 U.S. territories.

WIC is not SNAP (food stamps). Where SNAP provides broad food purchasing assistance to low-income households, WIC is targeted specifically to pregnant women, new mothers, infants, and young children, and provides **specific nutritious foods** selected for their role in healthy development. WIC also includes free nutrition counseling, breastfeeding support, and referrals to healthcare providers and social services.

About **half of all infants born in the United States** receive WIC benefits. For many families, WIC is the first government program they interact with.

One thing that sets WIC apart from almost every other federal benefit program: **WIC has no citizenship or immigration status requirement.** Undocumented immigrants, DACA recipients, visa holders, and all non-citizens are eligible if they meet income and categorical requirements. WIC is also not considered a "public charge" for immigration purposes.

## Who qualifies for WIC

WIC serves four categories of people:

| Category | Who |
|---|---|
| Pregnant women | Any pregnant woman, regardless of how far along |
| Postpartum women | Up to 6 months after the end of pregnancy (including miscarriage or stillbirth) |
| Breastfeeding women | Up to the infant's first birthday if breastfeeding |
| Infants | Birth to first birthday |
| Children | Age 1 through their fifth birthday (up to but not including age 5) |

Importantly: **fathers, foster parents, grandparents, and other caregivers** can apply for WIC on behalf of eligible infants and children. The program is about the child's nutrition, not who the primary caregiver is.

The WIC income limit is **185% of the federal poverty level**. Here are the 2026 thresholds:

### Automatic eligibility

If your household already receives **Medicaid, SNAP, or TANF**, you are **automatically income-eligible for WIC** — no additional income documentation required. This is called adjunctive eligibility. Given that Medicaid covers families up to 138% FPL in expansion states, and SNAP covers up to 130% FPL, there is significant overlap.

## WIC vs. SNAP — knowing the difference

| Feature | WIC | SNAP |
|---|---|---|
| Who it serves | Pregnant/postpartum women, infants, children under 5 | Any low-income household |
| What you can buy | Specific approved nutritious foods | Almost any food at grocery stores |
| Income limit | 185% FPL | 130% FPL (gross) |
| Citizenship requirement | None | Required (with exceptions) |
| Non-food services | Yes (nutrition counseling, breastfeeding support, referrals) | No |
| Benefit delivery | eWIC card (approved foods only) | EBT card (broad food purchases) |
| Monthly value | ~$40–$150+ depending on family composition | ~$230 average household |
| Enrollment period | Year-round | Year-round |

Many families qualify for both WIC and SNAP. The two programs complement each other — WIC focuses on specific high-nutrition foods for early childhood, while SNAP covers broader household grocery needs.

## WIC benefits — what you actually get

WIC benefits are loaded onto an **eWIC card**, which looks and works like a debit card at WIC-approved grocery stores, pharmacies, and farmers' markets. There's no cash involved.

### The food packages

The specific foods depend on which WIC category you're in (pregnant, breastfeeding, postpartum, infant, child), but WIC-approved items typically include:

- **Fruits and vegetables:** $43–$47/month Cash Value Benefit (CVB). This was permanently increased in the FY2025 appropriations after being temporarily raised during COVID. Fully breastfeeding women receive $47/month; children and other women receive $43/month.
- **Milk and dairy:** Whole milk for children 1–2; low-fat or nonfat for women and older children; cheese; yogurt (added as a stand-alone item in the 2024 food package update)
- **Iron-fortified breakfast cereal**
- **Whole grains:** Whole wheat bread, corn or flour tortillas, brown rice, oatmeal, barley
- **Eggs:** One dozen per month for women and children
- **Legumes and protein:** Canned beans, peanut butter, canned fish (tuna, salmon, sardines, mackerel — sardines and mackerel added in 2024)
- **100% fruit or vegetable juice** (limited quantities)
- **Infant formula:** For non-breastfeeding and partially breastfeeding infants; WIC negotiates contracts with formula manufacturers to keep costs low
- **Infant foods:** Iron-fortified infant cereal, jarred baby food fruits, vegetables, and meats
- **Dairy alternatives:** Tofu and soy beverages available

### Estimated monthly value

| Family Composition | Approximate Monthly WIC Value |
|---|---|
| Pregnant or postpartum woman only | ~$50–$60 |
| Breastfeeding woman only | ~$65–$75 |
| Infant (formula-fed) | ~$90–$120 (formula included) |
| Infant (breastfed) | ~$30–$50 |
| Child age 1–4 | ~$40–$55 |
| Breastfeeding mom + infant + toddler | $120–$160+ |

### Non-food services (also free)

Beyond food, WIC provides services that many families find just as valuable:

- **Personalized nutrition education and counseling** at every certification appointment
- **Breastfeeding support:** peer counselors, certified lactation consultants, and breast pump loans
- **Health screenings:** height, weight, and blood tests for anemia (low iron) at every appointment
- **Referrals** to immunizations, dental care, healthcare providers, substance abuse treatment, housing assistance, and other social services

## How to enroll in WIC — step by step

### Step 1: Check your eligibility

Use the USDA WIC Eligibility Tool at **fns.usda.gov/wic/eligibility-tool** to do a quick check before contacting an office. Or, if you're already on Medicaid or SNAP, you're automatically income-eligible — just call a WIC office.

### Step 2: Find your local WIC office

Search for your nearest WIC clinic at **fns.usda.gov/wic/locator** or call 1-800-4-HUNGRY (1-800-444-6867). WIC offices are located in health departments, community health centers, and clinics.

### Step 3: Schedule your certification appointment

Call or visit your local WIC office to schedule an in-person or virtual certification appointment. Many offices offer phone pre-screening to save time.

### Step 4: Attend your certification appointment

At your appointment, WIC staff will:
- Measure height and weight
- Draw a small blood sample to check for anemia
- Ask about your diet and eating habits
- Review proof of income (or confirm Medicaid/SNAP participation)
- Verify residency
- Provide a brief nutrition education session
- Issue your eWIC card if you're approved — often same day

### Step 5: Receive your eWIC card

Your monthly food benefits are loaded onto your eWIC card at the start of each certification period. You shop like normal at approved stores — the WIC items are automatically separated at checkout.

### Step 6: Recertify regularly

Recertification periods vary by category:
- Pregnant women: certify when first eligible; re-certify for postpartum
- Infants: every 6 months
- Children: every 6–12 months depending on age and state

You'll receive a reminder before your certification expires. Missing recertification means losing benefits until you re-enroll.

## The citizenship rule — WIC is unique

WIC is unusual among federal benefit programs because it has **no citizenship or immigration status requirement**. Undocumented immigrants, DACA recipients, visa holders, people on temporary protected status, and all non-citizens are welcome to apply if they meet income and categorical requirements.

WIC is also **not included in the public charge rule** — applying for or receiving WIC cannot be used against someone applying for a green card or other immigration benefits. This distinction is important and frequently misunderstood.

## Common mistakes to avoid

1. **"Only mothers can apply."** Fathers, grandparents, foster parents, and other caregivers can apply for WIC on behalf of eligible infants and children.
2. **"I need SNAP to get WIC."** WIC has independent eligibility. SNAP participation just makes you automatically income-eligible; you don't need to be on SNAP.
3. **"WIC and SNAP are the same thing."** WIC provides specific approved foods for a targeted population. SNAP provides broader purchasing power. Many families get both.
4. **"You need to be a citizen."** WIC has no citizenship requirement. All immigration statuses are welcome.
5. **"A family of four earning $55,000 earns too much."** At $61,050 as the 2026 threshold for a family of four, many working families qualify.
6. **"Breastfeeding moms get less from WIC."** The opposite is true — fully breastfeeding women receive enhanced food packages with higher fruit and vegetable benefits.
7. **"The eWIC card is stigmatizing."** It looks like a regular debit card and functions the same way at checkout.
8. **"I was denied before, so I can't apply again."** Circumstances change. If your income dropped, you had a pregnancy, or you have a new child under 5, reapply.

## Frequently asked questions

### What is the income limit for WIC in 2026?

The WIC income limit is 185% of the federal poverty level. For 2026, that's $29,526/year for a single person and $61,050/year for a family of four. If you're already on Medicaid, SNAP, or TANF, you're automatically income-eligible regardless of your income figure.

### Does WIC cover baby formula?

Yes. For infants who are not breastfed or only partially breastfed, WIC provides infant formula through your eWIC card. WIC negotiates contracts with formula manufacturers to control costs. Breastfeeding is encouraged and supported, but formula is available for families who need it.

### Can I get WIC if I'm undocumented?

Yes. WIC has no citizenship or immigration status requirement. Undocumented immigrants, DACA recipients, and all non-citizens can apply and receive benefits if they meet income and categorical requirements. WIC does not share information with immigration enforcement agencies and is not a public charge factor.

### How long does WIC last?

- Pregnant women: receive benefits during pregnancy and through 6 weeks postpartum
- Postpartum (non-breastfeeding): up to 6 months after the end of pregnancy
- Breastfeeding women: up to the infant's first birthday
- Infants: from birth to their first birthday
- Children: from age 1 to their fifth birthday

### What is the $43–$47/month fruit and vegetable benefit?

WIC includes a Cash Value Benefit (CVB) that works like store credit specifically for fruits and vegetables. Fully breastfeeding women receive $47/month; most other adult participants and children receive $43/month. This benefit was permanently increased in FY2025 from the earlier $24–$35 range.

### Can my dad apply for WIC for my baby?

Yes. Fathers, foster parents, grandparents, and other primary caregivers can apply for WIC on behalf of eligible children. The application focuses on the child's eligibility, not the caregiver's relationship.

### Can I use WIC at farmers' markets?

Yes. WIC benefits can be used at WIC-authorized farmers' markets via eWIC. Many states also participate in the WIC Farmers' Market Nutrition Program (FMNP), which provides additional seasonal coupons (typically $20–$30 per season) specifically for fresh produce at farmers' markets.

### Does WIC work if I move to another state?

Yes. You can transfer WIC benefits between states. Contact your current WIC office before moving to get documentation, then contact a WIC office in your new state to re-enroll. There may be a brief gap in benefits during the transition.
