## TL;DR

If you're 65 or older, you're likely eligible for several overlapping federal programs, Medicare, Medicaid, SSI, SNAP, Section 8, and LIHEAP at minimum. The average senior who qualifies for all of them receives benefits worth **$30,000–$50,000 per year** in healthcare coverage, food assistance, housing subsidies, and utility help. This guide covers every major program, the income and asset thresholds that apply specifically to seniors, and how to apply for all of them at once.

## Why seniors have a different benefits picture

Most federal benefit programs have two income tests: one for the general population and a looser one for people 60 and older or receiving disability income. Seniors also get extra deductions in SNAP, automatic enrollment pathways in Medicaid, and preference points in federal housing lotteries. The programs listed here are worth checking even if you've been told you earn "too much", because the elderly-specific thresholds are often 20–40% more generous than the standard ones.

## Social Security: the foundation

Before diving into need-based programs; it's worth separating two different Social Security benefits that often get confused.

**Social Security retirement benefits** aren't means-tested. You've earned them. If you worked and paid FICA taxes for at least 10 years (40 quarters), you get a monthly check starting as early as age 62, or up to age 70 for maximum benefit. The average retirement benefit in 2026 is about **$1,907/month**. This income counts toward the means-test for other programs listed below.

**SSI (Supplemental Security Income)** is completely different, a separate need-based program described in the next section.

## SSI: Supplemental Security Income

SSI is a federal cash assistance program for people 65+ and people with disabilities who have very limited income and assets. Being 65 automatically satisfies the age/disability requirement; you don't need to prove a medical condition.

### 2026 SSI income and asset limits for seniors

| Rule | Individual | Married couple |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Maximum monthly benefit | $967 | $1,450 |
| Countable income limit | $967/month | $1,450/month |
| Asset limit | $2,000 | $3,000 |

SSI is intended to fill the gap between your other income and the federal benefit rate. If you receive $400/month in Social Security, your SSI benefit is approximately $567/month ($967 − $400). The math isn't exact because of additional income exclusions, but that's the basic logic.

**What doesn't count as income:** the first $20/month of any income, the first $65/month of earned income, food assistance, home energy assistance, and irregular gifts under $60/quarter.

**What doesn't count as an asset:** your primary home, one vehicle (regardless of value), household goods, burial funds up to $1,500, and life insurance with face value under $1,500.

SSI also automatically enrolls you in **Medicaid** in most states, one application, two programs.

## Medicare: healthcare for 65+

Medicare is not income-based. If you're 65 or older and a U.S. citizen or 5-year lawful permanent resident, you're eligible. Most people don't pay a premium for Part A (hospital coverage) if they or a spouse worked 40+ quarters. Part B (medical insurance) costs **$185/month** in 2026.

### Medicare parts at a glance

| Part | What it covers | 2026 standard premium |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Part A | Hospital inpatient, skilled nursing, hospice | $0 (if worked 40 quarters) |
| Part B | Doctor visits, outpatient, preventive | $185/month |
| Part C (Medicare Advantage) | Bundles A+B+D through private insurer | Varies, often $0–$50/month |
| Part D | Prescription drugs | Varies by plan |

The Part B deductible is $257 in 2026. After that, Medicare covers 80% of most approved services. A Medigap (supplemental) policy covers the remaining 20%.

### Medicare Extra Help (Low Income Subsidy)

If your income is below roughly **$21,996/year** for an individual (or $29,820 for a couple), you may qualify for Extra Help, a program that pays most of your Part D drug costs. Apply through the Social Security Administration.

## Medicaid: the dual-eligibility opportunity

If you qualify for both Medicare and Medicaid, you're a "dual eligible", and it's one of the most valuable benefit combinations available to any American. Medicaid picks up costs Medicare doesn't, including the Part B premium, the 20% coinsurance, long-term care, dental, vision, and hearing.

### 2026 Medicaid income thresholds for seniors (selected states)

| Program type | Monthly income limit (individual) |
| --- | --- |
| Full dual eligibility (QMB+) | ~138% FPL = $1,732/month |
| Qualified Medicare Beneficiary (QMB) | 100% FPL = $1,255/month |
| Specified Low-Income Medicare Beneficiary (SLMB) | 120% FPL = $1,506/month |
| Qualifying Individual (QI) | 135% FPL = $1,695/month |

QMB pays your Medicare Part A and B premiums, deductibles, and coinsurance. SLMB and QI pay the Part B premium only. Check your state's Medicaid agency, many states have expanded thresholds above these federal minimums.

**Asset limits for Medicaid:** most states have a $2,000 individual asset limit for full Medicaid, but some states (California, Maine, Minnesota) have eliminated the asset test entirely. Your home, one vehicle, and personal belongings typically don't count.

## SNAP: food assistance for seniors

Seniors have the most favorable SNAP rules of any demographic. The key differences:

1. **Skip the gross income test.** Households with someone 60+ only need to pass the net income test (100% FPL).
2. **Medical expense deduction.** Out-of-pocket medical costs over $35/month are fully deductible from net income. A senior spending $300/month on prescriptions deducts $265 from their countable income.
3. **No work requirements.** Nobody 60+ is subject to work registration or the ABAWD rule.
4. **Restaurant Meals Program.** In participating states, elderly recipients can use their EBT card at approved restaurants, helpful for those who can't cook due to physical limitations.

### 2026 SNAP net income limits (100% FPL)

| Household size | Monthly net income limit |
| --- | --- |
| 1 | $1,255 |
| 2 | $1,704 |
| 3 | $2,152 |
| 4 | $2,600 |
| Each additional | +$449 |

The medical expense deduction often brings seniors with moderate income below this threshold. A single senior with $1,400 in Social Security income and $250 in monthly medical expenses may net out to $1,135 in countable income, well below the $1,255 limit.

## Section 8 / Housing Choice Voucher: elderly preference

Section 8 gives you a voucher that covers the difference between 30% of your income and the local fair market rent. For a senior on fixed income; this is life-changing: a $1,200/month apartment in a city where fair market rent is $1,200 costs you $360/month.

**Income limits for seniors (50% AMI, most common Section 8 threshold):**

| Household size | National median area; varies significantly by location |
| --- | --- |
| 1 person | ~$31,000–$55,000/year depending on metro |
| 2 persons | ~$35,000–$63,000/year |

Many public housing authorities (PHAs) give **elderly preference points** that move 62+ applicants higher in the queue. Some PHAs also have dedicated elderly-only buildings with lower wait times.

**The waitlist problem:** most cities have Section 8 waitlists of 2–10 years. Apply as soon as possible, and apply to multiple PHAs. Some PHAs open waitlists for only a few days per year.

See our full guide: [Section 8 eligibility 2026](/blog/section-8-eligibility-2026).

## LIHEAP: utility assistance

The Low Income Home Energy Assistance Program pays heating and cooling bills for low-income households. Most states prioritize elderly and disabled applicants.

### 2026 LIHEAP income limits (typical: states vary)

| Household size | 60% state median income (approximate) |
| --- | --- |
| 1 | $27,000–$36,000/year |
| 2 | $35,000–$46,000/year |
| 3 | $42,000–$55,000/year |

Benefits range from a one-time payment of $200 to several thousand dollars per year depending on your state, climate, and heating costs. Some states run weatherization programs alongside LIHEAP that insulate homes and replace inefficient furnaces, free for eligible seniors.

Apply through your local Community Action Agency. Benefits are often first-come, first-served, so apply early in the heating season.

## Pell Grant: yes, seniors can get it

This surprises most people: the federal Pell Grant is available to any student at any age, with no upper age limit. If you're 65 and want to take college courses, complete a degree you started decades ago, or train for a new skill, you can qualify.

### 2026 Pell Grant eligibility for seniors

| Factor | Details |
| --- | --- |
| Maximum award | $7,395/year |
| Income threshold (rough) | Under ~$60,000/year for partial award |
| Age limit | None |
| Enrollment requirement | At least half-time at a Title IV-eligible school |
| Citizenship | U.S. citizen or eligible non-citizen |

Many community colleges offer free or reduced tuition for seniors 65+ through state senior audit programs, which layered with Pell funding can make higher education essentially free.

Apply via FAFSA at studentaid.gov. See our full guide: [Pell Grant eligibility 2026](/blog/pell-grant-eligibility-2026).

## State-specific programs for seniors

Federal programs are the floor. States often add significant benefits on top.

### PACE (Program of All-Inclusive Care for the Elderly)

PACE provides integrated medical, social, and long-term care services to seniors who need nursing-home-level care but want to stay at home. It's funded jointly by Medicare and Medicaid and has no additional cost to dual-eligible participants. Available in 32+ states. Find a PACE program at npaonline.org.

### Senior centers and transportation

Every county in the United States has a federally-funded Area Agency on Aging (AAA) that coordinates:

- **Senior nutrition programs:** congregate meals at senior centers, and home-delivered Meals on Wheels
- **Transportation services:** free or reduced-cost rides to medical appointments, grocery stores, and pharmacies
- **Case management:** help navigating benefits, caregiver support, legal aid referrals

To find your local AAA, call the Eldercare Locator at 1-800-677-1116 or visit eldercare.acl.gov.

### State Pharmaceutical Assistance Programs (SPAPs)

Many states run programs that pay prescription drug costs for low-income seniors, either as a supplement to Part D or as a standalone program. Examples:

- **New York:** EPIC (Elderly Pharmaceutical Insurance Coverage)
- **Pennsylvania:** PACE and PACENET
- **New Jersey:** PAAD (Pharmaceutical Assistance to the Aged and Disabled)
- **California:** Medi-Cal covers most drugs for dual-eligible seniors at $0

Check your state insurance commissioner's website or your local AAA for state-specific options.

## How to apply for multiple programs at once

Most of these programs have separate applications, but you can streamline the process significantly:

### Step 1: Start with SSI

Applying for SSI at your local Social Security office triggers an automatic Medicaid referral in most states. One interview, two programs.

### Step 2: Apply for SNAP

Once you have your SSI approval letter, bring it to your state SNAP agency. SSI recipients are categorically eligible for SNAP in most states, expedited processing, no separate interview in many cases.

### Step 3: Apply for Section 8

Contact your local Public Housing Authority. Ask specifically about elderly preference and whether they have any elderly-designated buildings with shorter wait times.

### Step 4: Apply for LIHEAP

Contact your local Community Action Agency. They often serve as a one-stop shop for utility assistance and weatherization.

## Frequently asked questions

### Does Social Security income count against Medicaid or SNAP?

Yes, it counts as income for both programs. But elderly-specific deductions (medical expenses for SNAP, spend-down rules for Medicaid) often bring countable income below the threshold even when gross income looks too high.

### Can I have both Medicare and Medicaid?

Yes. This is called dual eligibility. Medicaid wraps around Medicare and covers most of the gaps, including long-term care in nursing homes.

### Can I get SSI if I already receive Social Security?

Yes, if your Social Security benefit is below the SSI federal benefit rate ($967/month in 2026) and you meet the asset test. SSI fills the gap between your Social Security income and the maximum benefit amount.

### What if I'm an immigrant who has just turned 65?

Lawful permanent residents with 5+ years of residency generally qualify for SSI and SNAP. Some immigrants qualify immediately, refugees, asylees, and certain other humanitarian categories. Undocumented seniors can still receive Medicaid-funded emergency care in all states, and full Medicaid in some states.

### Is there an income limit for Medicare?

No. Medicare isn't means-tested for basic eligibility. But higher-income seniors pay more for Part B and Part D through IRMAA (Income-Related Monthly Adjustment Amount) surcharges. Seniors earning under ~$106,000 individually pay the standard $185/month premium.

### What happens to my benefits if I go into a nursing home?

If you're in a Medicaid-funded nursing home, your SNAP benefits stop and most of your income (Social Security, SSI) goes toward the cost of care. Medicaid covers the rest. SSI drops to a $30/month personal needs allowance while you're institutionalized.

A 65-year-old with modest income is potentially eligible for Medicare, Medicaid (if income is low enough), SSI, SNAP, Section 8, LIHEAP, and state senior programs, worth tens of thousands of dollars in benefits annually.
