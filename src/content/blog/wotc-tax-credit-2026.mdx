## TL;DR

The Work Opportunity Tax Credit (WOTC) gives employers a federal tax credit of **$1,200 to $9,600 per qualifying hire** when they bring on workers from 10 specific disadvantaged groups — veterans, SNAP recipients, ex-felons, long-term unemployed workers, and others. The credit expired December 31, 2025, so new hires in 2026 are currently ineligible unless Congress reauthorizes it. Employers should still complete Form 8850 within 28 days of every hire to preserve retroactive eligibility if the program is renewed. This guide covers every target group, credit amounts, the certification timeline, and what to do right now.

## What WOTC actually is

The Work Opportunity Tax Credit (IRC Section 51) is a federal employment tax credit that rewards employers for hiring workers from groups that face significant barriers to employment. Congress created it in 1996 through the Small Business Job Protection Act, and it has been renewed — sometimes retroactively — many times since.

WOTC is a general business credit, which means it reduces your federal income tax liability dollar-for-dollar. Tax-exempt employers can claim a separate version (Form 5884-C) specifically for hiring qualified veterans.

Here is the basic structure:

- **Credit rate:** 40% of qualified first-year wages if the employee works 400+ hours; 25% if they work 120-399 hours
- **No per-employer cap** — you can claim credits for every qualifying hire
- **Flows to Form 3800** (General Business Credit) on your tax return

The credit is administered jointly: the IRS handles the tax claim side, and the Department of Labor certifies employees through State Workforce Agencies (SWAs).

## All 10 target groups explained

### 1. Qualified IV-A (TANF) Recipient
Individuals who are members of a family receiving Temporary Assistance for Needy Families (TANF) benefits for any 9 months during the 18-month period ending on the hire date.

### 2. Qualified Veteran
Four sub-categories carry different credit amounts. A veteran qualifies if they are: (a) a member of a family receiving SNAP benefits for at least 3 months during the 12 months before hire, (b) unemployed for at least 4 weeks but less than 6 months in the 12 months before hire, (c) unemployed for at least 6 months in the 12 months before hire, or (d) entitled to compensation for a service-connected disability and either hired within 1 year of discharge or unemployed for 6+ months in the past year.

### 3. Qualified Ex-Felon
An individual convicted of a felony and hired within 1 year of either the conviction date or release from prison. Note: the conviction, not the nature of the crime, is the qualifying factor.

### 4. Designated Community Resident
Ages 18-39 who live in an Empowerment Zone or a Rural Renewal County. The employee must still live in the designated area after hire.

### 5. Vocational Rehabilitation Referral
Individuals with physical or mental disabilities who have been referred to the employer under a state rehabilitation plan, a Department of Veterans Affairs program, or Ticket to Work and Work Incentives Improvement Act programs.

### 6. Summer Youth Employee
Ages 16-17, hired for summer employment (May 1 through September 15), who live in an Empowerment Zone. The reduced $3,000 wage cap and $1,200 maximum credit apply.

### 7. SNAP (Food Stamp) Recipient
Ages 18-39 who are a member of a family that received SNAP benefits for: (a) 6+ months during the 12 months ending on the hire date, or (b) at least 3 of the 5 months ending on the hire date (for recently approved recipients).

### 8. SSI Recipient
Individuals who received Supplemental Security Income for any month in the 60-day period ending on the hire date.

### 9. Long-term Family Assistance Recipient
Individuals who are members of a family that: (a) received TANF for at least 18 consecutive months ending on the hire date, (b) received TANF for any 18 months beginning after August 5, 1997, and the last TANF month ended within 2 years before hire, or (c) stopped being eligible for TANF within the prior 2 years because of a federal or state time limit.

### 10. Qualified Long-term Unemployment Recipient
Individuals who have been unemployed for at least 27 consecutive weeks before hire and received state or federal unemployment compensation during at least some of that period.

## How to claim WOTC: step-by-step

1. **Pre-screen on or before the date of the job offer.** Have the applicant complete IRS Form 8850, Part I (the employee section). This must happen before you extend a written or verbal offer — you cannot go back and do it after the offer is made.

2. **Complete Form 8850, Part II (employer section).** Fill out the employer portion with your business information and the employee's hire date.

3. **Submit within 28 days of the hire start date.** Send Form 8850 plus either ETA Form 9061 (Individual Characteristics Form) or ETA Form 9062 (Conditional Certification, used when the employee already has pre-certification) to your State Workforce Agency (SWA). Missing the 28-day window is the single most common reason WOTC claims are denied — there are almost no exceptions.

4. **Wait for SWA certification.** The SWA reviews the documentation and issues a certification letter. This can take weeks or months, but the 28-day deadline is yours to meet, not the SWA's.

5. **Track wages and hours.** Once the employee is certified, track their hours and qualified first-year wages carefully. The 120-hour and 400-hour thresholds determine your credit rate.

6. **Claim the credit at tax filing.** File IRS Form 5884 (Work Opportunity Credit) with your annual tax return. Tax-exempt employers hiring qualified veterans use Form 5884-C instead. The credit flows to Form 3800 (General Business Credit).

7. **Maintain documentation for at least 4 years.** Keep the SWA certification letter, Form 8850, payroll records showing wages and hours, and any supporting documentation the employee provided.

## Expiration and reauthorization status (2026)

**WOTC expired December 31, 2025.** Under current law, employers cannot claim WOTC for employees whose start date is January 1, 2026 or later.

Here is what you need to know:

- **Pre-2026 hires still qualify.** Employees hired on or before December 31, 2025, who are SWA-certified, can still generate a credit claimable on your 2025 tax return (or amended returns for prior years).
- **Retroactive renewal is common.** WOTC has lapsed and been renewed retroactively multiple times since 1996. Tax professionals widely recommend continuing to screen and file Form 8850 for every new hire so you are protected if Congress renews the credit retroactively.
- **The "One Big Beautiful Bill Act" (signed July 4, 2025) did not include WOTC reauthorization.** This was a notable omission given the bill's scope.
- **No confirmed reauthorization bill as of April 2026.** The credit remains expired. Watch the IRS website and IRS Notice publications for changes.

**The practical recommendation:** Keep your WOTC screening process running. The paperwork cost is minimal, and retroactive eligibility protection is worth it if renewal passes.

## State-level hiring incentives

Most states administer the federal WOTC through their SWAs and do not offer additional credits on top of the federal program. A few states run complementary hiring incentive programs:

- **California:** New Employment Credit (NEC) for businesses hiring in designated geographic areas
- **New York:** Hire-A-Vet Credit ($5,000-$15,000 for qualifying veterans); Empire State Jobs Retention Program
- **Louisiana:** Enterprise Zone hiring incentive ($2,500 per new job)
- **Georgia:** Job Tax Credit ($1,250-$4,000 per new job in qualifying counties)
- **Maryland:** Job Creation Tax Credit; More Jobs for Marylanders Act

## Frequently asked questions

### Is WOTC available in 2026?

Not for new hires starting January 1, 2026 or later. The credit expired December 31, 2025. Hires made through December 31, 2025 can still generate a credit if properly certified.

### How much is the WOTC per employee?

It depends on the target group and hours worked. For most groups, the maximum is $2,400 per hire (40% of up to $6,000 in wages at 400+ hours). Veterans carry higher caps, up to $9,600 for a disabled veteran who was unemployed for 6+ months. The minimum is $1,200 for a Summer Youth Employee.

### What is the 28-day deadline?

Form 8850 must be submitted to your State Workforce Agency within 28 calendar days of the employee's first day of work. There is almost no grace period. Missing this deadline means losing the credit for that hire.

### Does WOTC apply to part-time employees?

Yes. If a part-time employee works 120-399 hours in their first year, you can claim 25% of qualified wages (instead of the standard 40%). Employees who work fewer than 120 hours do not qualify.

### Does WOTC apply to nonprofits?

Only for one specific group: tax-exempt organizations can claim a credit (Form 5884-C) for hiring qualified veterans. Other target groups are not available to tax-exempt employers.

### Can I claim WOTC for rehires?

Sometimes. A rehired employee may qualify if they were absent long enough that they genuinely fall into a qualifying target group at the time of the new hire. The hire must be treated as a new hire, not a recall.

### What if I miss the 28-day deadline?

You cannot claim WOTC for that employee. There is no appeal or exception process for the 28-day rule. This is why automated WOTC screening in your ATS is worth setting up.

### Should I keep filing Form 8850 while WOTC is expired?

Yes. Tax professionals universally recommend it. Retroactive renewal would typically cover hires back to the expiration date, and having your certifications in process protects you. The cost is minimal; the potential upside is real.

### How long does SWA certification take?

It varies by state from a few weeks to several months. Your obligation is to submit within 28 days; the SWA then works at their own pace. Certifications are generally backlogged in high-volume states.

## The bottom line

WOTC is one of the most accessible employer tax credits when it is active — no competitive grant application, no specialized technology required. The main thing it demands is process: pre-screening every applicant and hitting the 28-day Form 8850 deadline without fail. With credit amounts ranging from $1,200 to $9,600 per hire, a mid-size employer who hires consistently from qualifying groups can easily generate $50,000-$200,000 per year in credits.

The credit is expired as of 2026, but retroactive renewal is the historical norm. Keep screening.
