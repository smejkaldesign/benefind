## TL;DR

The IRS estimates that roughly **$1 billion in tax credits goes unclaimed every single year** — most of it from working families who simply didn't know they qualified. Five credits in particular are chronically missed: the Earned Income Tax Credit, Child Tax Credit, Saver's Credit, American Opportunity Credit, and Child and Dependent Care Credit. Together, they can put thousands of dollars back in your pocket. This guide explains who qualifies for each, how much it's worth, and exactly how to claim it.

## Why so many credits go unclaimed

Tax credits are more valuable than deductions. A deduction reduces how much of your income is taxed. A credit reduces the actual tax you owe, dollar for dollar. Some credits are even "refundable" — meaning if the credit is larger than your tax bill, the IRS sends you the difference as a refund.

The reasons people miss them:

- They assume they earn too much (income limits are often higher than people think)
- They don't know the credit exists
- They made an eligibility mistake the prior year and got discouraged
- Their tax software didn't ask the right questions
- They filed "simple" returns themselves and skipped the relevant forms

Let's fix that.

## Credit 1: Earned Income Tax Credit (EITC)

### Who qualifies

The IRS estimates that **1 in 5 eligible workers doesn't claim the EITC** — about 5 million people per year. It's the single largest missed credit in the country.

To qualify in 2026, you need to:

- Have earned income from a job, self-employment, or farming
- Meet the income limit for your filing status and number of qualifying children
- Be a U.S. citizen or resident alien for the entire year
- Not be claimed as a dependent on someone else's return
- Have a valid Social Security number

You do NOT need to have children to claim it, though the credit is much larger with kids.

### How much it's worth

| Filing status / children | Max credit (2026) |
| ------------------------ | ----------------- |
| No children              | $649              |
| 1 child                  | $4,328            |
| 2 children               | $7,152            |
| 3+ children              | $8,046            |

Income limits for 2026: roughly $18,600 for no children (single filers), up to about $59,900 for three or more children (married filing jointly). Use the [EITC calculator at IRS.gov](https://www.irs.gov/credits-deductions/individuals/earned-income-tax-credit/use-the-eitc-assistant) to get your exact number.

### How to claim it

File Schedule EIC with your federal tax return (Form 1040). Tax software handles this automatically if you answer the questions honestly. If you're filing by hand, complete the worksheet in the Schedule EIC instructions.

If you missed the EITC in prior years, you can amend returns going back three years. The IRS estimates the average unclaimed EITC is around $2,400 — worth the 20 minutes it takes to amend.

### Common mistakes

- **Claiming a child who doesn't meet the qualifying child rules.** The child must live with you for more than half the year and be under 19 (or 24 if a full-time student).
- **Self-employed filers underreporting income.** Counterintuitively, you need *enough* earned income to qualify — reporting too little can disqualify you or reduce your credit.
- **Married couples filing separately.** You can't claim EITC if you file Married Filing Separately.

For a full deep-dive on EITC, see our guide: [EITC eligibility 2026](/blog/eitc-eligibility-2026).

## Credit 2: Child Tax Credit (CTC)

### Who qualifies

The Child Tax Credit applies to parents and guardians with dependent children under age 17. The child must:

- Be your son, daughter, stepchild, foster child, sibling, or a descendant of any of these
- Have lived with you for more than half the year
- Have a valid Social Security number
- Not have provided more than half their own financial support

Income phaseouts begin at $200,000 for single filers and $400,000 for married filing jointly.

### How much it's worth

The full credit is **$2,000 per qualifying child** in 2026. Up to $1,700 of that is refundable (the "Additional Child Tax Credit"), meaning you can receive it even if you owe little or no tax.

The credit phases out above the income thresholds at $50 per $1,000 of income over the limit.

### How to claim it

Complete Schedule 8812 (Credits for Qualifying Children and Other Dependents) with your Form 1040. Tax software will prompt you. If you're filing with a dependent child and you don't see this credit on your return, something went wrong.

### Common mistakes

- **Forgetting older children.** The credit applies through age 16 — some parents stop claiming it once a child hits their teens.
- **Assuming income disqualifies you.** Many middle-income families still get a partial credit even above the full phaseout threshold.
- **Divorced or separated parents claiming the same child.** Only one parent can claim the CTC per child per year. The IRS matches Social Security numbers; duplicate claims trigger audits.

## Credit 3: Saver's Credit (Retirement Savings Contributions Credit)

### Who qualifies

This one is almost embarrassingly unknown. If you contribute to a 401(k), IRA, Roth IRA, SIMPLE IRA, SEP-IRA, or 403(b), and your income is below the threshold, you can get a credit just for saving for retirement.

2026 income limits:

| Filing status       | Income limit to qualify |
| ------------------- | ----------------------- |
| Single              | $36,500                 |
| Head of household   | $54,750                 |
| Married filing jointly | $73,000              |

You also must be 18 or older, not a full-time student, and not claimed as a dependent.

### How much it's worth

The credit is 10%, 20%, or 50% of your contributions, up to $2,000 in contributions per person ($4,000 if married). So the maximum credit is **$1,000 per person**, or **$2,000 for married couples**.

| Adjusted Gross Income (single) | Credit rate |
| ------------------------------ | ----------- |
| Up to $22,000                  | 50%         |
| $22,001 – $24,000              | 20%         |
| $24,001 – $36,500              | 10%         |

### How to claim it

Complete Form 8880 with your federal return. Your tax software should surface this if you indicate you made retirement contributions, but it often gets missed in manual reviews.

### Common mistakes

- **Not making the contribution.** You need to actually contribute to a qualified account. Even a small IRA contribution before April 15 can unlock the credit for the prior tax year.
- **Forgetting rollovers reduce the credit.** Recent retirement plan distributions can reduce or eliminate the credit — it's complicated. Ask your tax preparer or use a CPA if you had distributions this year.
- **Assuming it's only for low-income filers.** A household earning $70,000 filing jointly still qualifies in 2026.

## Credit 4: American Opportunity Tax Credit (AOTC)

### Who qualifies

The AOTC covers the first four years of college. To qualify:

- The student must be pursuing a degree or credential
- Be enrolled at least half-time for at least one academic period during the year
- Have no felony drug conviction
- Have paid qualified education expenses (tuition, fees, required course materials) at an eligible institution

The credit applies whether you're paying for yourself or a dependent child in school. Income phaseout begins at $80,000 for single filers and $160,000 for married filing jointly; it phases out completely at $90,000 (single) or $180,000 (joint).

### How much it's worth

**Up to $2,500 per eligible student per year.** The credit is:

- 100% of the first $2,000 in qualified expenses
- 25% of the next $2,000

Up to $1,000 (40% of the credit) is refundable, meaning you can receive it even with no tax liability.

If your student has been in school for four years and you haven't claimed this, you may be leaving $10,000 on the table. Amend those returns.

### How to claim it

Complete Form 8863 with your tax return. You'll need Form 1098-T from the educational institution, which reports tuition and fees paid.

### Common mistakes

- **Claiming it for graduate school.** The AOTC is for the first four years of undergrad only. For grad students, the Lifetime Learning Credit (worth up to $2,000) applies instead.
- **Counting room and board.** Those don't count as qualified expenses. Only tuition, required fees, and required course materials qualify.
- **The parent and student both claiming it.** You can't double-dip. If the parent claims the student as a dependent, only the parent can claim the credit.
- **Missing the 1098-T.** If the school sends it to your student's dorm address and you never see it, you might skip the form. Check your student's school email or portal.

## Credit 5: Child and Dependent Care Credit

### Who qualifies

If you paid someone to care for a child under 13, a disabled spouse, or a disabled dependent so that you could work or look for work, you may qualify. Both spouses must have earned income (or be full-time students) if filing jointly.

Qualifying expenses include: daycare centers, in-home babysitters, after-school care, and summer day camps. Overnight camps and tutoring don't count.

### How much it's worth

The credit is worth 20% to 35% of your qualifying expenses, depending on your income. The maximum qualifying expenses are:

- **$3,000 for one qualifying person**
- **$6,000 for two or more qualifying persons**

So the maximum credit is **$1,050 for one person**, or **$2,100 for two or more**. Higher earners get 20% (at income above $43,000); lower earners get up to 35%.

This credit is non-refundable, so it can only offset tax you actually owe.

### How to claim it

Complete Form 2441 with your tax return. You'll need the caregiver's name, address, and either their Social Security number or employer identification number (EIN). If the caregiver refuses to provide their SSN, you can still claim the credit — include what information you have and attach a statement.

### Common mistakes

- **Forgetting about employer-sponsored dependent care FSAs.** If your employer offers a Dependent Care FSA and you contributed, you need to coordinate this with the credit. You can't claim the credit on expenses you paid with pre-tax FSA dollars.
- **Skipping it because both spouses work.** That's exactly who it's for.
- **Using it for a 13-year-old.** The child must be under 13 during the year you're claiming.
- **Not keeping records.** The IRS may ask for receipts or documentation of care payments. Keep records for at least 3 years.

## How to claim all of them at once

1. **File a full Form 1040** — not the simplified 1040-SR or 1040-EZ (which no longer exists). All five credits require the full 1040 with supporting schedules.
2. **Use tax software that asks about dependents, education, and retirement contributions.** Free options: IRS Free File (if income is below $84,000), VITA/TCE sites (free in-person help from IRS-trained volunteers), and the IRS Direct File pilot in participating states.
3. **Gather documents before you start:** W-2s, 1099s, 1098-T (education), 1098-E (student loan interest), retirement account contribution records, daycare provider tax ID, Social Security numbers for all dependents.
4. **Don't rush the dependent questions.** This is where most credits are missed. Answer every question about children, education, and care expenses carefully.
5. **Check prior years.** If you missed these credits in 2023 or 2024, you can still amend. File Form 1040-X within 3 years of the original due date.

## Frequently asked questions

### Can I claim multiple credits in the same year?

Yes. These credits are not mutually exclusive. A family with two kids in college, a toddler in daycare, and a 401(k) contribution could potentially claim all five.

### Do tax credits affect my benefits like SNAP or Medicaid?

Tax refunds are generally not counted as income for SNAP, Medicaid, or most other benefit programs for at least 12 months after receipt. The EITC specifically cannot reduce your SNAP benefits. Always check with your benefit caseworker if you have concerns.

### What if I can't afford a tax preparer?

Use VITA (Volunteer Income Tax Assistance) or TCE (Tax Counseling for the Elderly) — free, IRS-sponsored programs that serve people who earn roughly $67,000 or less. Find a site at [irs.gov/vita](https://www.irs.gov/individuals/find-a-location-for-free-tax-prep).

### What's the difference between a tax credit and a tax deduction?

A deduction reduces your taxable income, which indirectly reduces your tax bill. A credit reduces your actual tax bill directly, dollar for dollar. Credits are almost always more valuable. Refundable credits can even pay you money back beyond what you owed.

### I filed already and missed a credit. What do I do?

File an amended return using Form 1040-X. You have three years from the original due date to amend. The IRS processes amended returns in about 16 weeks.
