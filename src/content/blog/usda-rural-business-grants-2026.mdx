## TL;DR

The USDA Rural Business Development Grant (RBDG) program distributes approximately **$30 million per year** to fund economic development in rural America. There's **no matching requirement**, awards typically range from $10,000 to $500,000, and smaller requests score better. The catch: for-profit businesses cannot apply directly. Only **public bodies, Indian tribes, and nonprofit organizations** can be applicants. If you run a rural economic development organization, rural cooperative, or nonprofit serving small businesses, RBDG is one of the best non-competitive federal grant programs available.

## What RBDG is

The Rural Business Development Grant Program (RBDG) is administered by USDA Rural Development's Rural Business-Cooperative Service under authority from the Consolidated Farm and Rural Development Act (7 CFR Part 4280, Subpart E). It's a **competitive grant program** — not a loan — designed to support small and emerging private businesses in rural areas through intermediary organizations that provide technical assistance, training, and capital access.

RBDG has been running in various forms since the 1980s and represents one of USDA's core rural economic development tools. The current program consolidates what were previously two separate programs (the Rural Business Enterprise Grant and Rural Business Opportunity Grant) into a single program with two grant types.

### The two grant types

**Enterprise grants** are the main vehicle. They fund activities that directly benefit small and emerging rural businesses: revolving loan funds, equipment and infrastructure, technical assistance, and training programs.

**Opportunity grants** are more flexible but limited. They fund community economic development planning, technology-based projects, feasibility studies, and leadership training. Opportunity grants are capped at 10% of total RBDG annual funding — roughly $3 million of the $30M annual pool.

### What "rural" means

USDA defines rural as cities and towns with populations of **50,000 or fewer** that are not part of a metropolitan statistical area. This covers a vast swath of the United States, including many county seats and regional hubs that might not think of themselves as "rural."

Geographic eligibility is determined by the project's service area — where the businesses benefiting from the grant are located. Your organization doesn't need to be located in a rural area, but the businesses you serve must be.

## Who can apply

This is the most important eligibility rule, and the most commonly misunderstood:

**Eligible applicants:**
- Public bodies (cities, counties, townships, states, state agencies)
- Indian tribes (federally recognized tribal governments and tribal enterprises)
- Nonprofit organizations (501(c)(3) or other nonprofit classification)

**Not eligible to apply directly:**
- For-profit businesses
- Individuals
- Private developers

For-profit businesses can **benefit** from RBDG-funded programs — a rural economic development organization might use RBDG funds to create a revolving loan fund, provide technical assistance, or run a training program that serves for-profit rural businesses. But the applicant must be a public body, tribe, or nonprofit.

### Beneficiary requirements for the businesses served

Even though for-profits can't apply, the businesses your organization serves need to meet size thresholds to be considered "small and emerging":

- Employ **50 or fewer new employees**
- Have **less than $1 million in projected gross revenues**

## Eligible uses of grant funds

Enterprise grants can fund a broad range of activities that support rural small business development. The eligible uses include:

| Activity | Notes |
|---|---|
| Land acquisition and development | Including easements and rights of way |
| Building construction and renovation | New construction, conversion, or renovation |
| Equipment and machinery | For use in rural business development |
| Access infrastructure | Streets, parking, utilities serving businesses |
| Revolving loan funds | Startup and working capital loans to small businesses |
| Technical assistance | Direct assistance to small businesses |
| Business training | Including distance learning for job training |
| Business incubators | Creating or expanding incubator facilities |
| Cooperative programs | Establishing rural cooperative business development programs |
| Rural transportation | Infrastructure supporting business activity |
| Project planning | Feasibility studies, economic analysis, planning |
| Pollution control | Environmental compliance measures |

**What you cannot fund:**
- Agricultural production (USDA has separate farm programs for this)
- Pass-through grants with no clear rural business benefit
- Projects that primarily benefit urban areas
- Debt refinancing
- Costs incurred before the grant was obligated (don't start spending before award)

## How to apply

### Step 1: Contact your state USDA Rural Development office

This is the most important step. Every state has a USDA Rural Development state office, and most have district offices. They provide **free pre-application technical assistance** and can tell you:
- Current funding availability for your state
- This year's application deadline
- What projects the state office is currently prioritizing
- Whether your project concept meets program requirements

USDA RD offices are genuinely helpful with prospective applicants — this is part of their mission. Don't skip this step.

### Step 2: Check the Federal Register for the annual solicitation

USDA publishes a **Notice of Solicitation of Applications (NOSA)** in the Federal Register each year, typically in the fall for the following fiscal year. The NOSA establishes the application deadline, funding priorities for that cycle, and any program updates. FY 2025 applications were due in early-mid 2025.

Sign up for Federal Register alerts on USDA Rural Development programs to catch the NOSA when it's published.

### Step 3: Prepare your application package

A complete RBDG application typically includes:

| Document | Notes |
|---|---|
| SF-424 (Application for Federal Assistance) | Standard federal form |
| Project narrative | Goals, methods, timeline, beneficiaries (see below) |
| Detailed budget and budget narrative | Line-item costs with justification |
| Organizational capacity statement | Your track record and ability to execute |
| Letters of commitment/support | From partner organizations, beneficiary businesses |
| Environmental compliance documentation | NEPA environmental review |
| Civil rights compliance documentation | Equal opportunity certification |
| Proof of eligibility | Nonprofit determination letter, charter/articles, etc. |

### Step 4: Write a strong project narrative

The project narrative is where competitive applications are won or lost. Address these elements explicitly:

- **Problem statement:** What rural business development challenge are you solving?
- **Project approach:** Specifically how will you use the funds?
- **Beneficiaries:** How many small businesses will benefit, and how?
- **Outcomes:** Jobs created or saved, businesses started, loans made, revenue generated
- **Sustainability:** How will the program continue after the grant period ends?
- **Partnerships:** What organizations are collaborating, and what are they contributing?

### Step 5: Submit and track

Applications are submitted through Grants.gov or in paper to the state RD office, depending on state preferences. After submission, your state RD office reviews for completeness, then forwards to a national scoring process.

**Timeline:** Applications are reviewed on a national competitive basis. Award decisions are typically made 3-6 months after the application deadline.

## Priority scoring: what scores better

RBDG applications are scored on several factors. Understanding these factors helps you design a stronger project.

| Scoring Factor | Notes |
|---|---|
| Rural population served | Projects in communities under 10,000 score higher |
| Jobs created or saved | Clear job metrics improve scores |
| Veteran-owned businesses served | Projects explicitly helping veteran entrepreneurs |
| Persistent poverty counties | Projects in federally designated persistent poverty areas |
| Community need | Evidence of demonstrated economic distress |
| Applicant track record | Prior grant performance, organizational capacity |
| Leveraging other funds | Combining RBDG with other state, local, or private funds |
| Project sustainability | Plans for continuing beyond the grant period |

**Note:** As of early 2025, DEI-related scoring criteria were removed from the RBDG application per Executive Order. Applications should focus on rural economic need and business development outcomes.

## Tips for a competitive application

**Keep the award request modest.** Smaller requests consistently score better in the RBDG program. The research source notes this explicitly — competitive applications tend to be in the $10,000-$200,000 range. A $50,000 request for a highly specific technical assistance program will often outperform a $300,000 request for a vaguer initiative.

**Target the smallest communities.** If your project serves multiple rural communities, emphasize service to populations under 10,000. These communities receive priority in scoring.

**Show a clear multiplier effect.** USDA wants to see that RBDG dollars lead to private business investment, jobs, and economic activity that wouldn't happen without the grant. Revolving loan funds are popular because $200K in RBDG can be deployed as $200K in small business loans, then repaid and redeployed — multiplying the federal investment.

**Line up your partners.** Letters of support from community banks, local governments, community colleges, SBDCs, and the businesses you'll serve strengthen the application and demonstrate community buy-in.

**Document sustainability.** Grants that establish revolving loan funds, endowments, or fee-based services score better than one-time projects. Show how the program continues after year one.

**Don't overlook the environmental review.** NEPA compliance documentation trips up many applicants. Work with your state RD office to understand what level of environmental review your project requires — most small technical assistance projects qualify for a categorical exclusion.

## 2026 program updates

**FY 2025 funding:** Approximately $30 million was available for the FY 2025 cycle.

**FY 2026 budget uncertainty:** The Trump Administration's FY 2026 budget proposal suggested possible termination or reduction of several USDA Rural Development programs, including RBDG. As of April 2026, the program remains active, but organizations should contact their state USDA RD office to confirm current funding availability before investing in application preparation.

**DEI scoring removed:** Priority points previously awarded for projects serving underserved communities and meeting DEI-related criteria were eliminated from scoring in early 2025 per Executive Order.

**Application deadlines vary by state.** Unlike federal programs with a single national deadline, RBDG deadlines are set state by state. The only reliable way to know your deadline is to contact your state USDA Rural Development office.

## Complementary USDA programs

If RBDG isn't quite right for your project, consider these related programs:

| Program | What It Does |
|---|---|
| **USDA REAP** (Rural Energy for America Program) | Grants and loans for rural businesses to install renewable energy or improve energy efficiency |
| **USDA Community Facilities Program** | Grants and loans for community infrastructure supporting rural businesses |
| **Appalachian Regional Commission (ARC)** | Economic development grants for 423 counties across 13 Appalachian states |
| **Delta Regional Authority (DRA)** | Economic development grants for Mississippi Delta region (8 states) |
| **Northern Border Regional Commission (NBRC)** | Grants for economically distressed areas of ME, NH, VT, and NY |
| **USDA Business & Industry (B&I) Loan Guarantees** | Loan guarantees for rural businesses (for-profits can apply for loans) |

**Note on B&I Loan Guarantees:** For-profit rural businesses that can't apply for RBDG directly can often access USDA financing through the B&I Loan Guarantee program, which backs loans from commercial lenders for rural business development.

## Frequently asked questions

### Can a for-profit business apply directly for RBDG?

No. Only public bodies, Indian tribes, and nonprofits can be the applicant on an RBDG grant. However, a for-profit business can partner with a nonprofit or local government on an RBDG application, or benefit from programs that a nonprofit creates using RBDG funds (such as a revolving loan fund or technical assistance program).

### Is there a minimum award amount?

The research source notes awards typically start around $10,000, though there's no official program minimum. USDA discourages very small requests because of the administrative overhead of managing a federal grant.

### My organization is in a city, but serves rural businesses — do we qualify?

It depends on your project's service area, not your organization's location. If the small businesses benefiting from your project are in rural areas (populations under 50,000, not in metro areas), your project may qualify even if your organization is based in a larger city. Discuss this with your state USDA RD office.

### We've never applied for a federal grant before. Is RBDG appropriate for a first-time applicant?

RBDG is manageable for first-time federal grant applicants, especially for smaller award requests. The application is less complex than many federal grant programs. However, you'll need to demonstrate organizational capacity — meaning you have staff and systems to manage grant funds, track outcomes, and meet federal compliance requirements. Working with your state RD office and partnering with an experienced organization can help.

### What if our project is in an area affected by persistent poverty?

Persistent poverty counties (counties with 20%+ poverty rates for 30+ years, per Census data) receive priority scoring. If your service area includes persistent poverty counties, explicitly reference this in your application and demonstrate how your project addresses the economic conditions driving that poverty.

### How do I find out if my state has RBDG funding available for the current cycle?

Call or email your state USDA Rural Development office. The USDA RD website has a state office directory at rd.usda.gov/contact-us/state-offices. This is the most reliable source — federal register notices and USDA.gov updates sometimes lag behind actual state-level availability.

## The bottom line

RBDG isn't the right program for every rural organization, but for those doing economic development work in rural communities — running loan funds, providing technical assistance, training small business owners — it's among the best no-match competitive grants the federal government offers.

The program's constraints (no for-profit applicants, modest preferred award sizes) are by design: they focus the money on intermediary organizations that can stretch each federal dollar across many small businesses, rather than subsidizing a single company.

If your organization supports rural small businesses, contact your state USDA Rural Development office now to get on the radar before the next application window opens.
