## TL;DR

The Clean Energy Investment Tax Credit (ITC) gives businesses a credit worth 6-30% of the cost of qualifying clean energy property, with bonus adders that can push the total to 70% in exceptional cases. Section 48 (the legacy credit) applied to projects beginning construction before January 1, 2025. Section 48E (the new tech-neutral credit) applies to projects placed in service on or after January 1, 2025. Meeting prevailing wage and apprenticeship (PWA) requirements is what unlocks the 30% base rate — without them, you get 6%. The One Big Beautiful Bill Act (OBBBA), signed July 4, 2025, added Foreign Entity of Concern (FEOC) restrictions starting January 1, 2026 and announced wind/solar phase-outs after 2027. This guide covers the credit structure, eligible technologies, bonus adders, the Section 48-to-48E transition, how to claim, and the key 2025-2026 changes.

## What the Investment Tax Credit actually is

The Investment Tax Credit (ITC) is a federal tax credit that reduces your income tax liability based on the cost of qualifying energy property you install and place in service. Unlike the Production Tax Credit (PTC), which rewards energy output over time, the ITC is claimed in the year the system is placed in service based on the upfront capital cost.

The ITC has two current versions:

- **Section 48** (legacy) — applies to projects that began construction before January 1, 2025. Based on the technology-specific credit rates established by the Energy Tax Act of 1978 and updated by the Inflation Reduction Act (IRA) in 2022.
- **Section 48E** (tech-neutral) — created by IRA Section 13702; applies to projects placed in service on or after January 1, 2025. Extends the credit to any qualifying facility with a zero or net-negative greenhouse gas emissions rate, regardless of specific technology type.

The credit is administered by the IRS and Treasury Department. You claim it on your annual tax return — there is no pre-approval process.

## Credit structure: base rate and enhanced rate

The credit rate depends almost entirely on whether you meet the prevailing wage and apprenticeship (PWA) requirements.

| Credit Rate | When It Applies |
|---|---|
| **6% of qualified investment basis** | Default rate — applies if you do NOT meet PWA requirements AND the project is 1 MW or larger |
| **30% of qualified investment basis** | Enhanced rate — applies if you DO meet PWA requirements, OR if the project is under 1 MW |

Most commercial projects will want the 30% rate. That means meeting PWA requirements or keeping the project under 1 MW (alternating current).

### Prevailing Wage and Apprenticeship requirements

**Prevailing wage:** All laborers and mechanics employed in construction, alteration, or repair of the qualifying facility must be paid at least the locally applicable prevailing wage rates published by the Department of Labor. This applies to contractor and subcontractor employees, not just direct employees.

**Apprenticeship:** A minimum percentage of total labor hours must be performed by qualified apprentices from registered apprenticeship programs:
- 2024: 12.5% of total labor hours
- 2025 and later: 15% of total labor hours

There is a **good faith effort exception** if a registered apprenticeship program is not available in your area. There is also a **correction provision** — if you fail to meet PWA requirements, you can cure the deficiency by making back-payments to affected workers plus a $5,000 per-worker penalty for wage violations, or $50 per apprenticeship hour shortfall.

**Documentation:** File Form 7220 (Prevailing Wage and Apprenticeship Verification) to claim the enhanced rate.

## Bonus credit adders

Bonus adders stack on top of the 6% or 30% base rate. Multiple bonuses can be combined, bringing the total credit to as high as 70% for projects that qualify for everything.

| Bonus | Additional Credit | Key Requirements |
|---|---|---|
| Domestic Content | +10 percentage points | Steel and iron: 100% U.S. manufactured. Manufactured products: 40% domestic cost content (2024), 45% (June 2025-Dec 2025), 50% (2026), 55% (2027 and after) |
| Energy Community | +10 percentage points | Project located in: a brownfield site; a census tract in or adjoining a community with significant fossil fuel employment or tax revenues; or a community with a recently retired coal mine or coal-fired power plant |
| Low-Income Community (48E(h)) | +10 percentage points | Facility in a low-income community (as defined in IRC Section 45D) or on Indian land; facility capacity under 5 MW |
| Low-Income Residential / Economic Benefit | +20 percentage points | Facility is part of a qualified low-income residential building project or qualifies as a low-income economic benefit project; facility capacity under 5 MW |

**Maximum total ITC:** 30% base + 10% domestic content + 10% energy community + 20% low-income economic benefit = **70%**. Projects meeting all four conditions are rare but not impossible, particularly community solar serving low-income residents in energy communities with domestic equipment.

### Energy Community bonus in practice

Energy communities include census tracts in metropolitan statistical areas where at least 0.17% of employment is in fossil fuel extraction, processing, transport, or storage — or where at least 25% of local tax revenues come from fossil fuels. IRS maps are updated annually. Many more locations qualify than you would expect.

### Domestic Content escalating thresholds

The domestic content thresholds for manufactured products escalate over time:

| Period | Manufactured Products Domestic Cost Threshold |
|---|---|
| Before June 2025 | 40% |
| June 2025 - December 2025 | 45% |
| 2026 | 50% |
| 2027 and later | 55% |

Steel and iron requirements remain at 100% U.S. manufactured throughout. Meeting domestic content is increasingly challenging — and increasingly valuable — as thresholds rise.

## Eligible technologies

### Section 48E (tech-neutral, post-2024 placement in service)

Any qualified facility with an **anticipated greenhouse gas emissions rate of zero or less**. This includes:

- Solar energy (photovoltaic and thermal)
- Wind energy (onshore and offshore)
- Geothermal energy
- Hydropower
- Marine and hydrokinetic energy
- Nuclear power (including advanced nuclear)
- Energy storage (batteries and other storage technologies)
- Combined heat and power (CHP) with zero-emission fuel
- Other zero-emission technologies meeting the emissions test

The tech-neutral structure means new zero-emission technologies can qualify as they emerge without requiring new legislation.

### Section 48 (legacy, pre-2025 construction start)

Section 48 uses a technology-specific list including: solar energy property, qualified fuel cell property, qualified small wind energy property, geothermal energy property, qualified microturbine property, combined heat and power (CHP), waste energy recovery property, energy storage (batteries and other electrochemical storage), biogas property, offshore wind, and interconnection property for small generators.

If your project **began construction before January 1, 2025**, it remains under Section 48 rules even if it is placed in service after that date.

## Section 48 vs. Section 48E: which applies to your project?

| Factor | Section 48 (Legacy) | Section 48E (Tech-Neutral) |
|---|---|---|
| Applies to | Projects beginning construction before January 1, 2025 | Projects placed in service on or after January 1, 2025 |
| Technology eligibility | Specific list of eligible technologies | Any facility with zero or net-negative GHG emissions rate |
| PWA enhanced rate | Same (30% with PWA, 6% without) | Same |
| Bonus adders | Same structure | Same structure |
| FEOC restrictions | Not directly affected | Applies starting January 1, 2026 |
| Wind/Solar future | No change | Phase-out for wind and solar placed in service after December 31, 2027 (OBBBA) |

The transition matters most if you are planning a new project: it must either begin construction before January 1, 2025 (to stay under Section 48) or qualify under 48E's zero-emissions test.

## 2025-2026 key changes (OBBBA impact)

### Wind and solar phase-out

The OBBBA (signed July 4, 2025) terminates Section 48E ITC eligibility for **wind and solar projects placed in service after December 31, 2027**. However, projects that **begin construction before July 4, 2026** may claim the full credit under the original IRA phase-out schedule (which was more gradual). This creates a meaningful construction-start deadline for wind and solar developers.

### Foreign Entity of Concern (FEOC) restrictions

Beginning January 1, 2026, no energy tax credits may be claimed by:
- Entities that are "specified foreign entities" connected to China, Iran, North Korea, or Russia
- Entities where a foreign entity of concern holds 25% or more of equity interest, voting rights, or board seats
- Projects where FEOC entities have effective control

For domestic clean energy developers, this mainly affects equipment sourcing — manufacturers with Chinese equity stakes may disqualify projects from the credit even if the equipment is physically manufactured in the U.S.

### Domestic content threshold escalation

The manufactured products domestic content requirement increased from 40% to 45% in June 2025, will increase to 50% in 2026, and 55% in 2027. Projects that cleared the 40% threshold now need to ensure their supply chain still meets the higher bar.

## Direct pay: tax-exempt entities can get cash

Tax-exempt organizations — including nonprofits, state and local governments, tribal governments, rural electric cooperatives, and the Tennessee Valley Authority — cannot use a tax credit directly because they owe no income tax. The IRA created **elective payment (direct pay)**, which allows these entities to receive the ITC as a cash payment from the IRS instead.

To claim direct pay:
1. File an annual tax return (even if otherwise exempt)
2. File Form 3468 (Investment Credit) reporting the eligible investment
3. Register on the IRS Energy Credits Online portal before the tax return due date

**Domestic content requirement for direct pay:** Facilities claiming direct pay must meet the applicable domestic content threshold or accept a 10-percentage-point reduction in the credit amount.

## Transferability: selling your credits

For-profit entities can sell all or a portion of their ITC to an unrelated taxpayer for cash. This has created an active market for tax credit transfers, allowing companies that cannot use large credits (due to limited tax liability) to monetize them immediately.

Buyers typically pay $0.88-$0.95 per dollar of credit. Sellers report the transfer on their tax return. Both parties must register with the IRS Energy Credits Online portal.

## How to claim the ITC

1. **No pre-approval process.** Unlike some credits, there is no application to file before placing the project in service.

2. **Place the qualifying property in service** (connected, operational, and ready for its intended use).

3. **File Form 3468** (Investment Credit) with your annual income tax return. Report the eligible basis, the applicable credit rate, and any bonus adders claimed.

4. **File Form 7220** (Prevailing Wage and Apprenticeship Verification) if claiming the enhanced 30% rate based on PWA compliance.

5. **Register on IRS Energy Credits Online** if claiming transferability or elective payment (direct pay). Registration must occur before the tax return due date.

6. **Maintain documentation:**
   - Property acquisition records and cost basis
   - Evidence of placement in service date
   - PWA compliance records (certified payroll records, apprenticeship program documentation)
   - Domestic content documentation (manufacturer certifications, supply chain cost breakdowns)
   - Energy Community eligibility documentation (location, census tract confirmation)

Documentation should be retained for at least 7 years. PWA audit risk is real; maintain certified payroll records throughout the construction period.

## State-level clean energy incentives

Many states offer incentives that stack with the federal ITC:

- **California:** Self-Generation Incentive Program (SGIP) for energy storage; Net Energy Metering (NEM)
- **New York:** NY-Sun solar incentives; NYSERDA programs; Clean Energy Standard
- **Massachusetts:** SMART solar incentive program; Clean Peak Energy Standard
- **New Jersey:** Successor Solar Incentive Program (SuSI); offshore wind tax credits
- **Illinois:** Adjustable Block Program for solar; Illinois Solar for All for low-income projects
- **Colorado:** Community Solar Gardens Act; utility rebate programs
- **Minnesota:** Solar*Rewards program; Made in Minnesota Solar incentive
- **Oregon:** Energy Trust of Oregon incentives; Renewable Energy Development Grants
- **Connecticut:** Clean energy incentives through the Green Bank

State incentives are separate applications through state energy offices or utilities. Most stack with the federal ITC without offset.

## Frequently asked questions

### How much is the clean energy tax credit in 2026?

The base rate is 6% without meeting prevailing wage and apprenticeship requirements, or 30% if you meet PWA requirements (or if the project is under 1 MW). Bonus adders can bring the total to 40%, 50%, or up to 70% for projects that qualify for multiple bonuses.

### Does my project need to pay prevailing wages to get the 30% credit?

Yes, unless the project is under 1 MW (alternating current). Projects 1 MW and above must meet both prevailing wage and apprenticeship requirements to qualify for the 30% enhanced rate. Without PWA compliance, the rate drops to 6%.

### Can nonprofits receive the clean energy tax credit?

Yes, through elective payment (direct pay). Tax-exempt organizations file a tax return and Form 3468, and the IRS issues a refund check equal to the credit amount. Domestic content requirements apply for direct pay claims.

### What qualifies as domestic content for the ITC bonus?

Steel and iron components must be 100% U.S. manufactured. For other manufactured products, the applicable percentage of the total cost must come from domestically manufactured components (40% through May 2025, 45% June-December 2025, 50% in 2026, 55% in 2027+). Manufacturers must provide written certification of domestic content percentages.

### What is the Energy Community bonus?

An additional 10 percentage points for projects located in designated energy communities: brownfield sites, census tracts with significant fossil fuel employment or tax revenues, or communities with recently retired coal infrastructure. The IRS updates the eligible census tract map annually.

### What are FEOC restrictions and how do they affect my project?

Beginning January 1, 2026, projects cannot claim the ITC if they involve entities connected to China, Iran, North Korea, or Russia — either as the project owner, equipment supplier, or controlling investor. This primarily affects equipment sourcing from manufacturers with Chinese equity stakes. Projects placed in service before January 1, 2026 are generally not affected.

### Will solar and wind credits be eliminated?

The OBBBA announced a phase-out of Section 48E ITC for wind and solar projects placed in service after December 31, 2027. Projects that began construction before July 4, 2026 may be eligible for the credit under the pre-OBBBA phase-out schedule. Other zero-emission technologies (geothermal, nuclear, storage, hydropower) are not subject to this specific phase-out.

### Can I sell my tax credits to someone else?

Yes. For-profit entities can transfer (sell) ITC credits to unrelated taxpayers for cash. The credit transfer market is active, with buyers typically paying $0.88-$0.95 per dollar of credit. Both parties register through the IRS Energy Credits Online portal.

### What is the difference between Section 48 and Section 48E?

Section 48 is the legacy credit that applies to projects that began construction before January 1, 2025, using a technology-specific eligibility list. Section 48E is the new tech-neutral credit that applies to projects placed in service on or after January 1, 2025, covering any zero-emission technology. Credit rates, bonus adders, and PWA requirements are the same under both sections.

### How do I know if my project qualifies for the Energy Community bonus?

The IRS publishes maps of eligible energy communities based on census tract data. The DOE also maintains an interactive map. You need to confirm your project's census tract and cross-reference it with the most current IRS list, which is updated annually. Many locations qualify that would not intuitively be considered energy communities.

## The bottom line

The clean energy ITC is one of the largest and most accessible federal business incentives currently available. A well-structured project meeting prevailing wage requirements and qualifying for energy community and domestic content bonuses can receive a credit worth 50%+ of capital costs — dramatically improving project economics and payback periods.

The OBBBA changes create real urgency for wind and solar developers: projects need to begin construction before July 4, 2026 to preserve full eligibility under the pre-OBBBA phase-out schedule. The FEOC restrictions starting January 1, 2026 require careful supply chain vetting. For storage, geothermal, nuclear, and other zero-emission technologies, the credit structure remains largely intact and attractive.
