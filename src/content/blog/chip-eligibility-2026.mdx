## TL;DR

CHIP (the Children's Health Insurance Program) provides free or low-cost comprehensive health coverage to children in families that earn too much for Medicaid but can't afford private insurance. Income limits vary by state, but most states cover children in families earning between **175% and 405% of the federal poverty level** — that's roughly $48,000 to $133,000 for a family of four. There's **no enrollment deadline**: you can apply any time of year. Dental, vision, and mental health care are included.

## What CHIP is

The Children's Health Insurance Program (CHIP) is a federal-state partnership that covers children who fall into the gap between Medicaid and private insurance. Congress created it in 1997 specifically for working families — families who are employed and paying taxes but don't have access to affordable employer-sponsored coverage or can't pay private insurance premiums.

CHIP covers **children under 19** (from birth through age 18). Some states also extend CHIP to pregnant women who don't otherwise qualify for Medicaid.

**CHIP is real insurance.** It covers the same types of services as employer-sponsored plans: doctor visits, hospital stays, prescription drugs, mental health care, and — critically — dental and vision care that many private plans underfund.

Federal funding for CHIP was reauthorized through **FY2029 under the HEALTHY KIDS Act**, so the program is stable for the foreseeable future.

## Income limits — what families can earn and still qualify

Income limits vary significantly by state because each state sets its own upper threshold above the federal minimum of 200% FPL. The national range is **175% to 405% FPL** for children, with a median around 255% FPL.

## What CHIP covers

All CHIP programs must provide comprehensive coverage. Required services include:

- **Routine and preventive care:** Well-child visits, developmental screenings, immunizations
- **Doctor visits:** Primary care and specialist appointments
- **Dental care:** Comprehensive dental coverage, including orthodontia in some states — far more extensive than most adult Medicaid dental benefits
- **Vision care:** Eye exams and eyeglasses
- **Prescription drugs:** Covered medications for acute and chronic conditions
- **Mental and behavioral health:** Therapy, counseling, psychiatric care, and substance use treatment for adolescents
- **Inpatient and outpatient hospital care**
- **Emergency services**
- **Laboratory and X-ray services**

### What CHIP typically costs

CHIP may charge modest premiums and copays — unlike Medicaid, which is generally free for children. However, federal rules cap total out-of-pocket costs at **no more than 5% of family income per year**.

| Family Income | Typical Cost |
|---|---|
| Below 150% FPL | Generally $0 premiums, $0 copays |
| 150–200% FPL | $0–$15/month premiums; $5–$10 copays for some visits |
| Above 200% FPL | $15–$50/month premiums; $5–$25 copays |

No copays are charged for preventive care (well-child visits, immunizations) regardless of family income.

## CHIP vs. Medicaid — what's the difference for kids

| Feature | Medicaid (for children) | CHIP |
|---|---|---|
| Income ceiling | Generally up to 133–200% FPL | Generally 200–405% FPL (varies by state) |
| Premiums | None (for children) | Possible, up to 5% of family income |
| Copays | Generally none | Possible for non-preventive services |
| Dental coverage | Comprehensive (EPSDT mandate) | Comprehensive |
| Vision coverage | Yes | Yes |
| Mental health | Yes | Yes |
| Enrollment | Year-round | Year-round |

In practice, when you apply through your state's website, the system automatically determines whether your children qualify for Medicaid or CHIP and routes them to the right program. You don't need to know the exact threshold — just apply.

### Program structures vary by state

States run CHIP in one of three ways:

1. **Separate CHIP program:** A standalone program distinct from Medicaid, with its own enrollment and administration
2. **Medicaid expansion CHIP:** The state extended Medicaid to cover more children; these children get Medicaid benefits and rules
3. **Combination approach:** The most common — states use both structures for different income ranges or age groups

Approximately 10 states run separate CHIP programs, 9 use Medicaid expansion CHIP, and 32 states plus DC use a combination.

## How to apply — step by step

### Step 1: Check your state's income limits

Visit your state's CHIP or Medicaid website, or go to **InsureKidsNow.gov** (a federal clearinghouse with links to every state program). Income limits update annually and vary significantly.

### Step 2: Gather your documents

You'll need:
- Child's Social Security number and birth certificate
- Proof of household income (pay stubs, tax returns, or employer letters from the past 30 days)
- Proof of residency (utility bill, lease, or state ID)
- Proof of citizenship or immigration status
- Information about any current health insurance

### Step 3: Apply online, by phone, or in person

- **Online:** HealthCare.gov (will route you to your state's CHIP program) or your state's Medicaid/CHIP portal
- **Phone:** Call 1-800-318-2596 (federal marketplace) or 1-877-KIDS-NOW (1-877-543-7669) for state-specific help
- **In person:** At your local Medicaid or social services office
- **At the hospital:** Many hospitals have enrollment specialists who can help apply at birth or during a visit

### Step 4: No enrollment deadline — apply any time

CHIP has **no annual open enrollment period.** You can apply any month of the year. If your child is uninsured right now, apply today. Processing typically takes 30–45 days.

### Step 5: When you apply for Medicaid, CHIP is automatic

When you apply for Medicaid for your children, the state automatically screens them for CHIP if they don't qualify for Medicaid. You don't need to file two separate applications.

## CHIP and employer coverage

Children generally must be **uninsured** to qualify for CHIP. If your child has coverage through your employer's health plan, they typically cannot enroll in CHIP. Some states impose a waiting period before a child can enroll in CHIP after dropping employer coverage — often 90 days.

However, if your employer's plan has very high premiums or out-of-pocket costs, CHIP may still be the better option. Many families with employer access still pay less out-of-pocket under CHIP. Run the numbers before assuming employer coverage is the better deal.

## Common mistakes to avoid

1. **"My kids don't qualify because I work."** CHIP is specifically designed for working families. Many full-time workers with children qualify.
2. **"CHIP isn't real insurance."** It provides comprehensive coverage comparable to or better than many employer-sponsored plans, especially for dental and vision.
3. **Not knowing dental and vision are included.** CHIP's dental coverage is often more comprehensive than what adults get under Medicaid.
4. **Thinking there's a deadline.** There's no annual enrollment period. Apply whenever your child loses coverage or you think they might qualify.
5. **Assuming employer coverage is always better.** Compare premium costs and out-of-pocket maximums before deciding.
6. **Not knowing your state's upper income limit.** Many families who would qualify never apply because they assume they earn too much.
7. **Confusing CHIP with Medicaid.** Related programs, different income thresholds and rules. Applying through the same portal routes your child to whichever program they qualify for.

## Frequently asked questions

### What is the income limit for CHIP in 2026?

Income limits vary by state, from about 175% to 405% of the federal poverty level. For a family of four, that's roughly $57,750 to $133,650 per year. Check your state's specific limits at InsureKidsNow.gov.

### Does CHIP cover dental care?

Yes. All CHIP programs provide comprehensive dental coverage, including preventive care (cleanings, fluoride treatments), basic restorative care (fillings), and in many states, orthodontic treatment for medically necessary cases.

### Is there an open enrollment period for CHIP?

No. You can apply for CHIP any time of year. There is no annual enrollment window. If your child loses health coverage for any reason, you can apply immediately.

### Can I get CHIP for my newborn?

Yes. Children are eligible for CHIP from birth through age 18. If your income qualifies at the time of birth, you can apply for CHIP as soon as the baby is born. The application can often be started before birth.

### Does CHIP cover mental health counseling?

Yes. CHIP programs must cover mental and behavioral health services, including therapy and counseling for children and adolescents. Under the Mental Health Parity and Addiction Equity Act, CHIP mental health benefits cannot be more restrictive than medical/surgical benefits.

### What happens if my child ages out of CHIP at 19?

At age 19, children no longer qualify for CHIP. Depending on your family's income and situation, they may qualify for Medicaid (in expansion states), marketplace plans with premium subsidies through HealthCare.gov, or — if attending college — student health coverage.

### Can I apply for CHIP and Medicaid at the same time?

You only need to submit one application. When you apply, the system automatically determines whether your child qualifies for Medicaid or CHIP based on your income and routes them to the appropriate program.

### Does CHIP cover prescriptions?

Yes. All CHIP programs cover prescription drugs. The specific formulary (list of covered medications) and copay structure vary by state, but prescription coverage is a mandatory benefit.
