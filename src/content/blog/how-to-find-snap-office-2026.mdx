## TL;DR

Finding your SNAP office is step one of applying for food assistance, but most people don't need to visit in person, online and phone applications are available in every state. If you do need to go in person, this guide tells you how to find your local office, what to bring, and what to expect. It also includes direct portal links for the 10 most populous states.

> **Before you go anywhere: check if you qualify first.** Run the [Benefind screener](/screening) in 2 minutes so you know you're applying for the right programs.

## Do you actually need to visit a SNAP office?

Probably not. In 2026, every state offers at least one of these remote options:

- **Online application** (available in all 50 states + DC)
- **Phone application** (available in most states)
- **Mail-in paper application** (available everywhere as a fallback)

The in-person interview that used to be required for most applicants has been replaced by phone interviews in the vast majority of states. You can complete the entire SNAP application process without ever leaving your home.

**When you do need to visit in person:**
- Your state requires an in-person interview for your specific case type
- You need help completing the application (many offices have staff who can assist)
- You're in a crisis situation and need same-day expedited SNAP (you can sometimes request this in person)
- You need to resolve a discrepancy or dispute on your case
- You're providing documents that can't be uploaded (some older offices still require originals)

## How to find your SNAP office: 3 ways

### Option 1: USDA's official locator tool

The easiest starting point is the USDA's national office finder. Go to **fns.usda.gov/snap/state-directory** and click your state. This takes you directly to your state's SNAP agency page, which will have office locations, online application links, and contact numbers.

### Option 2: Search "[your state] SNAP office" or "[your state] DHHS office"

Most states run SNAP through a department called something like:
- Department of Human Services (DHS)
- Department of Health and Human Services (DHHS)
- Department of Social Services (DSS)
- Department of Family and Children Services (DFCS)
- Office of Economic Opportunity

Searching your state name plus any of these terms will get you to the right agency website, which will have an office locator.

### Option 3: Call 211

Dial **211** from any phone; it's a national social services helpline available in all 50 states and most territories. Tell them you're looking for your local SNAP office. The operator can give you the address, hours, and application instructions for your area. Many 211 operators can also help you start the application over the phone.

## What to bring to an in-person SNAP visit

If you do visit in person, come prepared. Bringing everything on this list in one trip saves significant time.

### Identity documents

- Government-issued photo ID (driver's license, state ID, passport)
- For household members who don't have photo ID: birth certificate, Social Security card, or school ID

### Proof of Social Security numbers

- Social Security cards for all household members applying
- If you don't have your card, you can bring a document that shows your SSN (W-2, tax return, pay stub)

### Proof of income

For **employed household members:**
- Last 4 consecutive weeks of pay stubs
- If paid irregularly: bank statements showing deposits, or a letter from your employer

For **self-employed household members:**
- Most recent federal tax return (Schedule C)
- Business records or bank statements from the past 30 days

For **other income types:**
- Unemployment award letter
- SSI/SSDI award letter
- Child support payment records (paid or received)
- Pension or retirement income statements

### Proof of housing costs

- Lease or rental agreement showing monthly rent amount
- Mortgage statement
- If no written lease: a self-declaration of your rent amount (accepted in many states)

### Proof of utility costs

- One recent utility bill (electric, gas, water, phone)
- Most states use a Standard Utility Allowance (SUA), so you may not need to prove the exact amount

### Additional documents (if applicable)

- Child care bills or receipts (for the dependent care deduction)
- Medical bills for household members who are elderly (60+) or disabled
- Proof of disability status (award letter from SSA, doctor's documentation)
- Immigration documents for non-citizen household members

**Tip:** You can apply without all documents and provide missing items later. Submitting an incomplete application is better than delaying.

**Agency:** California Department of Social Services (CDSS)
**Program name:** CalFresh
**Apply online:** benefitscal.com
**Phone:** 1-877-847-3663
**Notes:** BenefitsCal is California's unified benefits portal, you can apply for CalFresh, Medi-Cal (Medicaid), and CalWORKs in one application. Phone interviews required in most counties; video interviews available.

### Texas

**Agency:** Texas Health and Human Services Commission (HHSC)
**Program name:** SNAP (Lone Star Card)
**Apply online:** yourtexasbenefits.com
**Phone:** 2-1-1 (statewide benefits hotline)
**Notes:** Texas has an asset test and stricter eligibility rules than most states. Interview is by phone in most cases.

### Florida

**Agency:** Florida Department of Children and Families (DCF)
**Program name:** SNAP (ACCESS Florida)
**Apply online:** myflorida.com/accessflorida
**Phone:** 1-866-762-2237
**Notes:** Florida requires both an application and a phone interview. Processing time is typically 30 days; expedited within 7 days for eligible households.

### New York

**Agency:** New York Office of Temporary and Disability Assistance (OTDA), administered by counties
**Program name:** SNAP
**Apply online:** mybenefits.ny.gov
**Phone:** Call your county DSS office (listed on mybenefits.ny.gov)
**Notes:** New York City residents can also use the HRA benefits portal at nyc.gov/hra. Phone or video interviews available.

### Pennsylvania

**Agency:** Pennsylvania Department of Human Services (DHS)
**Program name:** SNAP
**Apply online:** compass.state.pa.us
**Phone:** 1-800-692-7462
**Notes:** COMPASS is Pennsylvania's integrated benefits portal. Same application covers SNAP, Medicaid, CHIP, and other programs.

### Illinois

**Agency:** Illinois Department of Human Services (IDHS)
**Program name:** SNAP
**Apply online:** abe.illinois.gov
**Phone:** 1-800-843-6154
**Notes:** ABE (Application for Benefits Eligibility) covers multiple Illinois programs. Interviews are conducted by phone.

### Ohio

**Agency:** Ohio Department of Job and Family Services (ODJFS), administered by counties
**Program name:** SNAP (Ohio Direction Card)
**Apply online:** benefits.ohio.gov
**Phone:** Contact your county Job and Family Services office
**Notes:** Ohio has a county-administered system; online applications route to your county office automatically.

### Georgia

**Agency:** Georgia Department of Human Services (DHS), Division of Family and Children Services (DFCS)
**Program name:** SNAP (Georgia Gateway)
**Apply online:** gateway.ga.gov
**Phone:** 1-877-423-4746
**Notes:** Georgia enforces an asset test. Processing can take the full 30 days.

### North Carolina

**Agency:** North Carolina Department of Health and Human Services (DHHS), administered by counties
**Program name:** SNAP
**Apply online:** epass.nc.gov
**Phone:** Contact your county DSS office (listed at ncdhhs.gov/snap)
**Notes:** ePASS is the online portal. Interviews are by phone in most counties.

### Michigan

**Agency:** Michigan Department of Health and Human Services (MDHHS)
**Program name:** SNAP (Michigan Bridge Card)
**Apply online:** michigan.gov/mibridges
**Phone:** 1-855-275-6424
**Notes:** MI Bridges covers SNAP, Medicaid, and other Michigan programs. Phone interviews available statewide.

## What to expect during the phone interview

Most SNAP applicants complete a phone interview within 2–4 weeks of submitting their application. Here's what happens:

### Who calls whom

Usually the caseworker calls you. Make sure the phone number on your application is correct and that you answer calls from unknown numbers during the processing period. If you miss the call, you typically have 10 days to reschedule.

### What they ask

The interview covers:
- Confirmation of household members and their relationships
- Verification of income sources and amounts
- Housing situation (own or rent, who pays utilities)
- Any expenses that qualify as deductions (child care, medical costs, excess shelter costs)
- Identity verification questions

**Be prepared to explain any discrepancies** between what you reported and what the caseworker sees in their system (tax records, previous benefits history, etc.).

### What to have ready

Have a copy of your application and supporting documents in front of you during the call. The caseworker may ask you to confirm specific numbers.

### How long it takes

Most interviews last 15–30 minutes.

## Emergency SNAP: getting benefits fast

If your household has under $150 in gross monthly income and under $100 in bank accounts (or if combined income and assets are less than your monthly rent/mortgage + utilities), you qualify for **expedited SNAP**. Benefits must be issued within 7 days.

To request expedited processing:
1. Indicate on your application that you are in crisis
2. If applying in person, tell the intake worker
3. If applying online or by phone, look for the "expedited" or "emergency" checkbox

You may still need to complete an interview, but it can be same-day or next-day in crisis situations.

## If you need help applying

These resources can help you complete your SNAP application at no cost:

- **211:** Call or text 211 for local assistance navigating benefits applications
- **Local legal aid offices:** Many provide free SNAP application assistance; find yours at lawhelp.org
- **Community action agencies:** Federally-funded organizations in every state that help with benefit applications; find yours at communityactionpartnership.com
- **Food banks:** Many food banks have staff who assist with SNAP applications; find yours at feedingamerica.org

## Frequently asked questions

### How long does it take after I apply?

Standard applications are processed within 30 calendar days. Expedited applications for households in crisis are processed within 7 days. You'll receive a written notice of approval or denial.

### Can someone else apply on my behalf?

Yes. You can designate an "authorized representative" to apply on your behalf and even use the EBT card on your behalf. They'll need to be named on the application and may need to sign a form.

### What if I move to a different county or state mid-certification?

You need to report the move to your current caseworker. For interstate moves, your benefits don't automatically transfer; you need to close your case and apply in the new state. You can request your new state accept your documentation without a new full interview in some cases.

### What if I can't get to an office and don't have a phone or internet?

Contact 211, they can help connect you with community resources. Some states will accept paper applications mailed in, and many have home visit programs for elderly or disabled applicants.

### Can I check my application status online?

In most states, yes. Log in to your state's online benefits portal (the same place you applied) to check status, upload documents, and see case notes.

## The bottom line

You don't need to visit a SNAP office to apply in most cases, online and phone applications work the same as in-person ones. If you do need to visit, use your state's portal or dial 211 to find the right location and hours.
