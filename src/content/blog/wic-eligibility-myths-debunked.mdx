About **6.5 million people** receive WIC each month, but federal estimates suggest **15-20 million are eligible**. The reason? Myths. "You have to be unemployed." "You can't own a car." ## What WIC actually is

WIC is a federal program run by USDA. It provides **monthly food benefits, nutrition education, breastfeeding support, and referrals to health and social services** for:

- **Pregnant women** (through pregnancy)
- **Postpartum women** (for 6 months after giving birth, or 12 months if breastfeeding)
- **Infants** (up to their 1st birthday)
- **Children** (up to their 5th birthday)

The food benefits come via an **eWIC card** that works like a debit card at authorized grocery stores. Participants get a specific monthly food package — things like milk, eggs, cheese, whole grain bread, cereal, fruits, vegetables, peanut butter, and baby formula — not cash and not SNAP-style flexibility.

Average benefit value: **$44-$112 per person per month**, plus the nutrition services which are worth much more on top.

## Myth 1: "You have to be unemployed"

**False.** WIC has NO work requirement. In fact, most WIC participants are in working households. The program is designed to supplement nutritionally at-risk families, and plenty of them work full-time jobs that don't pay enough to afford a complete diet.

WIC income limits are **185% of the federal poverty level** — about:

| Household size                     | 2026 annual limit | Monthly limit |
| ---------------------------------- | ----------------- | ------------- |
| 1 (pregnant woman, household of 1) | $28,953           | $2,413        |
| 2                                  | $39,128           | $3,261        |
| 3                                  | $49,303           | $4,109        |
| 4                                  | $59,478           | $4,957        |
| 5                                  | $69,653           | $5,805        |

A household of 4 can earn nearly **$60K/year** and still qualify. That's well into middle-class territory in most of the country.

## Myth 2: "You can't own a car or a house"

**False.** WIC has no asset test. You can own a car, a house, a second car, retirement savings, a savings account — none of it counts against you. WIC eligibility is based purely on income and "nutritional risk."

This is different from SNAP, TANF, and some Medicaid categories, where asset limits sometimes still apply. WIC dropped asset tests decades ago.

## Myth 3: "Fathers can't apply"

**False.** Fathers, grandparents, foster parents, and other legal guardians can absolutely apply for infant and child WIC benefits. You just can't apply for the "woman" portion (pregnant/postpartum/breastfeeding) if you're not the woman yourself.

A single father with a 2-year-old and a 4-year-old can walk into a WIC office and sign both kids up. So can a grandmother raising her grandchildren, or two dads who adopted an infant.

## Myth 4: "You have to be a US citizen"

**False.** WIC has NO citizenship requirement. It is explicitly **not** considered a "public charge" benefit for immigration purposes. Undocumented immigrants can enroll their eligible children and themselves (if pregnant/postpartum) without affecting immigration status.

USCIS has explicitly confirmed that using WIC does not count against you in a public charge determination. WIC is also not considered federal means-tested public benefit under the 1996 welfare reform law.

If you or your family member is undocumented, you should still apply. WIC does not share enrollment information with immigration enforcement.

## Myth 5: "Only pregnant women qualify"

**False.** WIC covers four distinct categories:

1. **Pregnant women** — during pregnancy
2. **Postpartum women** — 6 months non-breastfeeding, 12 months breastfeeding
3. **Infants** — birth through first birthday
4. **Children** — 1st through 5th birthday

The **majority of WIC participants are actually children ages 1-5**, not pregnant women. If you have a child under 5, apply — it doesn't matter whether you were ever on WIC during pregnancy.

## Myth 6: "WIC is just for formula"

**False.** WIC covers a broad and nutritionally balanced food package. Depending on your category, you can get:

- Milk (whole milk for kids 1-2, reduced-fat for older kids and adults)
- Eggs
- Whole grain bread, brown rice, or whole wheat tortillas
- Cereal (from an approved list of low-sugar cereals)
- 100% juice (limited amount)
- Cheese, yogurt, tofu
- Peanut butter or beans
- Fruits and vegetables (cash-value benefit, now $25-$50/month)
- Canned fish (tuna, salmon) for fully breastfeeding women
- Baby cereal, baby food fruits and veggies, baby food meats (for infants 6-11 months)
- Infant formula (iron-fortified, approved brands)

The fruit and vegetable benefit was permanently increased in 2024 to **$25/month per child, $47/month per pregnant/postpartum woman, and $52/month per breastfeeding woman**. That alone is a meaningful grocery boost for many families.

## Myth 7: "If I have Medicaid I can't have WIC too"

**False.** Having Medicaid, SNAP, TANF, or any other benefit does NOT affect your WIC eligibility. In fact, if you qualify for any of those, you automatically meet WIC's income rules ("adjunctive eligibility") and don't need to submit separate income documentation.

Most WIC participants are on Medicaid too. These programs are designed to work together.

## Myth 8: "You can't use WIC at most stores"

**False.** WIC is accepted at tens of thousands of grocery stores nationwide — **nearly every major chain**. Walmart, Target, Kroger, Safeway, Publix, Albertsons, HEB, Whole Foods, and most regional chains accept eWIC cards. Farmers markets in many states also accept WIC, and some states have online WIC shopping pilots.

The eWIC card works like a debit card. It only authorizes purchases of approved items in your monthly food package — the register automatically accepts the WIC-eligible items and lets you pay for everything else in another form of payment.

## Myth 9: "You have to go to a welfare office"

**False.** WIC is administered by state health departments (not welfare agencies) and usually operates out of health clinics, community centers, public health offices, and some pediatric providers — not DHS or welfare offices.

Most WIC offices don't ask invasive questions, don't run credit checks, don't interview you about your marriage status, and don't treat you like a suspect. It's a health program, not a means-tested cash program.

## Myth 10: "The application takes weeks and you need a lawyer"

**False.** WIC applications take about **30-45 minutes** in person. You bring:

- Proof of income (paystub, tax return, or proof of Medicaid/SNAP)
- Proof of identity (driver's license, state ID, passport)
- Proof of residence (utility bill, rental agreement, or government letter)
- The child or pregnant woman being enrolled (for the nutrition assessment)

A qualified nutrition professional does a quick nutrition assessment (height, weight, hemoglobin for children, brief diet history) to determine "nutritional risk" — which, in practice, almost every applicant meets because the risk criteria include things like "underweight," "overweight," "iron deficiency," and "dietary risk" (which is met by most American diets).

**You usually walk out with benefits the same day.** The eWIC card is loaded at the appointment or mailed within a few days.

## The real eligibility requirements

To get WIC, you need to meet **all three**:

### 1. Category

You must be pregnant, postpartum, breastfeeding, an infant, or a child under 5.

### 2. Income

You must be at or below **185% FPL** OR automatically qualify through Medicaid, SNAP, TANF, or certain FDPIR (Food Distribution Program on Indian Reservations) enrollment.

### 3. Nutritional risk

You must be determined to be "at nutritional risk" by a health professional. This sounds strict but in practice nearly everyone qualifies — the categories include anemia, underweight, overweight, short stature, inadequate diet, pregnancy complications, breastfeeding complications, or even "homeless" or "victim of abuse."

## How to apply

### 1. Find your local WIC office

Call the national WIC information line at **1-800-942-3678** or use the state-by-state directory at [fns.usda.gov/wic/where-apply](https://www.fns.usda.gov/wic/how-apply).

### 2. Schedule an appointment

Call your local office. Many states allow walk-ins, but scheduling cuts wait time.

### 3. Bring documents

- Photo ID
- Proof of income (last 30 days of paystubs, tax return, or Medicaid/SNAP letter)
- Proof of residence (bill, lease, or letter)
- The person being certified (baby, child, or pregnant woman)

### 4. Nutrition assessment

A WIC staffer measures height, weight, and hemoglobin (for children) and asks a few nutrition questions. Takes 5-10 minutes.

### 5. Enroll and receive card

You walk out with an eWIC card loaded with your first month's benefits, plus a schedule of follow-up appointments (every 3-6 months to keep benefits active).

## What happens after you enroll

Every 3-6 months you'll have a recertification appointment — shorter than the first visit, just to update income and check nutritional status. You keep getting benefits automatically between appointments via the eWIC card.

Breastfeeding support is a major WIC benefit that's often overlooked. Most WIC offices have certified lactation consultants, free breast pumps (including hospital-grade pumps for mothers who need them), peer counselors, and nursing rooms. This alone is worth hundreds of dollars if you have a baby.

## Frequently asked questions

### Can I get WIC if I'm only pregnant and have no other kids?

Yes. Pregnancy is an eligible category by itself. You can enroll as a pregnant woman and start receiving benefits immediately.

### What if I move to another state?

Your WIC certification transfers. Call your new state's WIC office and request a "Verification of Certification" (VOC) from your old office. Most transfers are processed within a day or two.

### Can I get WIC and SNAP at the same time?

Yes, and many families do. SNAP is monthly cash-like grocery benefits (broader food options); WIC is a smaller but more targeted food package plus health services.

### What happens when my child turns 5?

They age out of WIC. Children over 5 are not eligible, but their younger siblings can continue.

### Can I get WIC for twins?

Yes — both babies each get their own full food package, doubling the benefit for your household.

### Is WIC cut in the recent federal budget debates?

WIC is funded annually through the federal appropriations process. There have been proposals to cut WIC, but as of 2026 the program is fully funded and nothing has changed for participants. If funding is ever short, USDA has authority to shift dollars around to keep existing enrollees on the program.

### Does WIC report to ICE?

No. WIC is confidential and not shared with immigration enforcement. USDA has affirmed this repeatedly.

## The bottom line

WIC is the most under-claimed federal benefit program in the country, largely because of myths that have been circulating for decades. If you're pregnant or have a child under 5, and your household earns less than 185% of the federal poverty level, **apply**. The application takes under an hour, there's no asset test, no work requirement, no citizenship requirement, and you walk out with benefits the same day.
