## TL;DR

Medicaid is free or low-cost health insurance for about 83 million Americans. In the 41 states that expanded Medicaid under the ACA, a single adult qualifies with income up to **$22,025/year ($1,835/month)**; a family of four qualifies up to **$45,540/year**. There is no open enrollment period — you can apply any time. Work requirements affecting expansion adults are scheduled to begin January 2027.

## What Medicaid is (and what it isn't)

Medicaid is a joint federal-state health insurance program that provides comprehensive coverage to people with low incomes. The federal government sets minimum rules and shares the cost; each state runs its own program and can add benefits beyond the federal floor.

It is **not** Medicare (the federal health insurance program for people 65+ and the disabled with work history). It is **not** a loan, and it is **not** just for unemployed people — millions of working households qualify.

About **83 million people** are enrolled in Medicaid as of 2026, making it the largest health insurer in the United States. You might know it by a different name depending on your state:

- **Medi-Cal** (California)
- **MassHealth** (Massachusetts)
- **BadgerCare Plus** (Wisconsin)
- **TennCare** (Tennessee)
- **Medicaid** in most other states

## Who qualifies — the income rules

The biggest factor is income. Medicaid uses **Modified Adjusted Gross Income (MAGI)**, which counts most taxable income but excludes non-taxable Social Security, veterans' benefits, child support received, and gifts.

### Expansion states (41 states + DC)

If you live in a state that expanded Medicaid under the ACA, the income limit for adults ages 19–64 is **138% of the federal poverty level (FPL)**.

| Household size | Monthly income limit | Annual income limit |
| -------------- | -------------------- | ------------------- |
| 1              | $1,835               | $22,025             |
| 2              | $2,489               | $29,863             |
| 3              | $3,142               | $37,702             |
| 4              | $3,795               | $45,540             |
| 5              | $4,448               | $53,378             |
| 6              | $5,101               | $61,217             |
| Each additional | +$653               | +$7,838             |

### Non-expansion states (10 states)

The 10 states that have **not** expanded Medicaid are: Alabama, Florida, Georgia, Kansas, Mississippi, South Carolina, Tennessee, Texas, Wisconsin, and Wyoming.

In these states, eligibility for adults is much more restrictive. Childless adults generally have no pathway at all, and parents must earn well below the poverty line. Texas, for example, covers parents only up to about 17% FPL — roughly $3,700/year for a family of three.

| Population | Typical income limit |
| ---------- | -------------------- |
| Childless adults (non-expansion states) | Not eligible in most cases |
| Parents/caretakers (non-expansion states) | Often 17–60% FPL (varies widely by state) |
| Children (all states) | 133–200%+ FPL |
| Pregnant women (all states) | Minimum 133% FPL; many states 185–200%+ |
| Elderly and disabled | SSI-level limits ($994/month individual in most states) |

### Special categories

**Children:** All states must cover children in households up to at least 133% FPL through Medicaid or CHIP. Many states extend coverage much higher — New York covers children up to 400% FPL.

**Pregnant women:** Every state must cover pregnant women up to at least 133% FPL. Most expansion states extend to 185–200% or higher. As of early 2026, 48 states and DC cover postpartum care for 12 months after delivery (up from 60 days).

**Elderly and disabled (age 65+ or receiving disability):** These populations are subject to non-MAGI rules that include asset/resource tests. The income limits are tied to SSI levels.

**Medically needy (spend-down):** 35 states plus DC allow people with higher incomes to qualify after paying medical bills that reduce their income to the state's Medicaid threshold. This is often relevant for nursing home care.

## What Medicaid covers

### Mandatory benefits (every state must cover these)

- Inpatient and outpatient hospital services
- Physician and specialist visits
- Laboratory and X-ray services
- Nursing facility services (age 21+)
- Home health services
- EPSDT (comprehensive preventive and treatment services for children under 21)
- Family planning services and supplies
- Federally qualified health center (FQHC) and rural health clinic services
- Transportation to medical care
- Tobacco cessation for pregnant women

### Common optional benefits (most states cover these)

- **Prescription drugs** — all 50 states currently cover prescription medications
- **Mental health and substance use disorder treatment** — available in nearly all states, often through managed care
- **Dental care** — covered for children (EPSDT) in all states; adult dental coverage varies widely. Only 19 states offer comprehensive adult dental.
- **Vision care** — covered for children; adult coverage varies by state
- **Physical and occupational therapy**
- **Personal care services** (assistance with daily activities)

### Cost sharing

There are **no premiums or copays** for:
- Children under 18
- Pregnant women (for pregnancy-related services)
- Terminally ill patients receiving hospice care
- Most institutionalized individuals

Some states charge small copays ($1–$8) for doctor visits or prescriptions for adult Medicaid members above certain income levels, but these are capped by federal rules.

## How to apply — step by step

### Step 1: Find your state's Medicaid agency

Most states have an online application. Search "Medicaid [your state]" or go to Healthcare.gov, which routes Medicaid and marketplace applications automatically.

### Step 2: Gather documents

You'll typically need:

- Social Security numbers for all household members
- Proof of citizenship or immigration status (birth certificate, passport, green card)
- Proof of income (recent pay stubs, tax returns, self-employment records, unemployment letter)
- Proof of residency (utility bill, lease, state ID)
- Proof of age if not on file
- For non-MAGI populations (elderly, disabled): bank statements and asset documentation

### Step 3: Submit the application

Apply online through Healthcare.gov or your state Medicaid portal, by phone, by mail, or in person at a local Medicaid/social services office or community health center. You can also apply at a hospital — they often have enrollment assistants on staff.

There is **no open enrollment period** for Medicaid. Apply any time of year.

### Step 4: Verification and processing

States must process most applications within 45 days (90 days for disability-based applications). If something is missing, the agency will contact you for additional documents.

### Step 5: Retroactive coverage

If you're approved, Medicaid can cover medical expenses from up to **3 months before your application date** if you were eligible during that period. If you have unpaid bills from recent months, ask about retroactive coverage when you apply.

### Step 6: Receive your coverage

You'll get a Medicaid ID card or enrollment letter. About 72% of Medicaid enrollees are in managed care plans — you may need to select a plan and a primary care doctor.

## Medicaid vs. Medicare — the most common confusion

| Feature | Medicaid | Medicare |
| ------- | -------- | -------- |
| Who it's for | People with low income | Adults 65+; people with certain disabilities |
| Eligibility based on | Income and household | Age (65+) or disability/work history |
| Cost | Usually free or very low | Premiums, deductibles, copays apply |
| Run by | Federal + state government | Federal government |
| Dental/vision | Often included | Generally not included (except Medicare Advantage) |
| Long-term care | Covered (nursing homes, home care) | Limited |

Some people qualify for both. These "dual eligibles" get coverage from both programs, which typically means Medicare pays first and Medicaid covers remaining costs.

## State differences that matter most

### California (Medi-Cal)

California covers all income-eligible adults regardless of immigration status — since 2024, this includes undocumented adults of all ages. Medi-Cal also covers more than 138% FPL through various programs and waivers.

### Texas

One of the most restrictive states. No Medicaid expansion means childless adults have no coverage pathway. Parents must earn below roughly 17% FPL (~$3,700/year for a family of 3). About 4.7 million Texans are in the "coverage gap" — earning too much for Medicaid but too little for marketplace subsidies.

### Wisconsin

Covers adults up to 100% FPL through a waiver (but has not adopted full expansion). Does not receive the enhanced 90% federal match available to expansion states.

### Recent expansion states

North Carolina expanded in 2023 (implemented early 2024). If you live in a state that recently expanded, you may be eligible even if you were denied before.

## Work requirements coming in 2027

The reconciliation law signed in July 2025 requires expansion adults to complete **80 hours/month** of qualifying activities (work, job training, education, community service, or caregiving) starting January 1, 2027. Exemptions include:

- Pregnant women
- Primary caregivers of dependents under 6 or disabled family members
- People with disabilities
- Those in substance use treatment
- Adults 55 or older
- Tribal members

If you receive Medicaid as an expansion adult and you're not in an exempt category, you'll need to document work activities to maintain eligibility starting in 2027.

## Common mistakes to avoid

**"I earn too much for Medicaid."** Many people underestimate the limits. In expansion states, a family of four can earn up to $45,540 and still qualify. Run the numbers.

**"I missed the enrollment period."** Medicaid has no enrollment period. You can apply any time, 365 days a year.

**"Medicaid only covers basic care."** It covers comprehensive care including mental health, substance use, and — in most states — prescription drugs. Many states also cover dental and vision.

**"I'll have to pay it back."** Medicaid is not a loan. The exception: estate recovery rules can apply to individuals 55+ who received long-term care services. This does not affect people under 55 or those who didn't receive nursing home or home-based care.

**"Medicaid is for unemployed people."** Working families make up a huge portion of Medicaid. If you work a low-wage job without employer insurance, Medicaid may be your best option.

**"I have a pre-existing condition, so I might not qualify."** Medicaid has no medical underwriting. Your health status doesn't affect eligibility — only income, household size, and categorical criteria matter.

## Frequently asked questions

### Does Medicaid cover dental care?

For children under 21, dental is covered as part of EPSDT in every state. Adult dental coverage varies significantly — only 19 states offer comprehensive adult dental through Medicaid. Most cover emergency extractions at minimum. Check your state's specific Medicaid dental benefit to know what's included.

### Does Medicaid cover prescriptions?

Yes. All 50 states cover prescription drugs through Medicaid. The specific formulary (list of covered drugs) varies by state and by managed care plan, but coverage is generally comprehensive.

### Can I get Medicaid if I have a job?

Yes. Employment does not disqualify you. Many full-time workers at low wages qualify, especially in expansion states. If your employer offers coverage, you can still apply for Medicaid if your household income is below the limit — you don't have to take your employer's plan.

### What happens if I get a raise?

If your income increases and you exceed the eligibility limit, you may qualify for marketplace health insurance with premium subsidies (the ACA marketplace). Healthcare.gov handles both — if you're no longer Medicaid-eligible, it will route you to marketplace options automatically.

### Does Medicaid cover mental health and therapy?

Yes. Mental health and substance use disorder services are covered in all states. Coverage must meet mental health parity requirements, meaning mental health benefits must be comparable to medical/surgical benefits.

### What's the difference between Medicaid and CHIP?

Medicaid covers eligible adults, children, pregnant women, elderly, and disabled individuals. CHIP (Children's Health Insurance Program) covers children in families that earn too much for Medicaid but still have moderate income. They're related programs that often work together — applying for one usually screens you for the other.

### Can immigrants get Medicaid?

U.S. citizens and most lawful permanent residents (after 5 years) qualify. Refugees, asylees, and survivors of trafficking qualify immediately. Most expansion states extend coverage to lawfully residing children and pregnant women without the 5-year wait. Undocumented immigrants qualify for emergency Medicaid only. California is the exception — it covers all income-eligible adults regardless of immigration status.

### What is the Medicaid work requirement?

Starting January 1, 2027, adults who gained Medicaid through ACA expansion must document 80 hours/month of qualifying activities (employment, education, job training, community service, caregiving). There are broad exemptions. The requirement does not apply to children, pregnant women, elderly individuals, people with disabilities, or those in non-expansion states.
