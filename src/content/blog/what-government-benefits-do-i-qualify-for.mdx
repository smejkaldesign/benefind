## The honest answer: most people qualify for more than they think

If you're working in the United States in 2026 and earning under about $75,000 a year for a family of four, **you almost certainly qualify for something**. The problem isn't eligibility. The problem is that the benefits system was designed agency-by-agency, not person-by-person. You have to know which programs exist, figure out which agency runs each one, learn the rules for each, and apply separately — often in person, often on paper, often in English only.

That's why 20% of people eligible for SNAP never claim it. It's why an estimated $60 billion in tax credits go unclaimed every year. It's why families cycle through emergency rooms because they never discovered they qualify for Medicaid.

This guide is the short version of what a benefits navigator would walk you through.

## The seven categories you should actually check

Here are the benefit categories most households should run through. Each links to a deeper guide where we have one.

### 1. Food

- **SNAP (food stamps)** — federal program, now called "EBT" in most states. Monthly benefit, usable at grocery stores and most farmers' markets. See our [SNAP eligibility guide for 2026](/blog/snap-eligibility-2026) for income limits and work requirements.
- **WIC** — for pregnant people, new mothers, and children under 5. Covers formula, produce, eggs, milk, cereal.
- **School meals** — free or reduced-price breakfast and lunch for kids in participating schools.
- **Summer EBT** — newer program that gives families $120 per child to cover food during summer break.

### 2. Health care

- **Medicaid** — state-run, federally funded health insurance for low-income households. Eligibility rules vary dramatically by state because of Medicaid expansion politics. If you live in one of the 40 expansion states, the income cutoff is about 138% of the federal poverty level.
- **CHIP** — children's health insurance for families that earn too much for Medicaid but still can't afford private coverage. Covers dental and vision in most states.
- **ACA marketplace subsidies** — if you're self-employed or your employer doesn't offer coverage, you probably qualify for premium tax credits on healthcare.gov. Most enrollees pay $0–$50 a month.

### 3. Housing and energy

- **Section 8 / Housing Choice Voucher** — long waitlist in most cities, but worth applying anyway. The voucher covers the portion of rent above 30% of your income.
- **LIHEAP** — federal heating/cooling bill assistance. Most states open enrollment in November. See our [step-by-step LIHEAP application guide](/blog/how-to-apply-for-liheap-heating-assistance).
- **Weatherization Assistance Program** — one-time free upgrades to insulation, windows, and heating systems.

### 4. Families and children

- **Child Tax Credit** — worth up to $2,000 per child under 17 in 2026. Claimed on your tax return.
- **Earned Income Tax Credit (EITC)** — worth up to $7,830 for a family with three kids. Refundable, meaning you get it even if you owe no tax.
- **Child Care and Development Fund (CCDF)** — state-run subsidies that cover some or all of your child care costs while you work or go to school.
- **Head Start** — free preschool for 3- and 4-year-olds from low-income families.

### 5. Cash assistance

- **TANF (Temporary Assistance for Needy Families)** — the modern successor to "welfare." Time-limited monthly cash payments. Rules vary by state.
- **SSI (Supplemental Security Income)** — monthly cash benefit for people who are 65+, blind, or disabled, and have low income.
- **SSDI (Social Security Disability Insurance)** — if you've paid into Social Security and can't work because of a disability.

### 6. Education and workforce

- **Pell Grant** — federal college grant that doesn't have to be repaid. Up to $7,395 per year.
- **WIOA training programs** — free job training, often bundled with stipends for transportation and child care while you train.
- **Workforce reentry and apprenticeship grants** — state-run, typically targeted at specific industries.

### 7. Special situations

- **VA benefits** — if you or a spouse served in the military, there's a separate universe of health, housing, and education benefits you may qualify for.
- **Tribal benefits** — if you're an enrolled member of a federally recognized tribe.
- **State-specific programs** — states fund hundreds of smaller programs that aren't on any federal list. California, New York, and Washington alone run more than 40 programs that don't appear in federal databases.

## The three numbers that determine most of your eligibility

If you only remember three things about how eligibility works, remember these numbers. Almost every program uses some version of them.

### Number 1: Your household income as a % of the Federal Poverty Level (FPL)

The Federal Poverty Level is a number the government sets every year. In 2026, it's **$15,650 for a household of 1** and adds $5,500 per additional person. Most programs set their income cutoff at some multiple of this number.

| Program                     | Income cutoff                          |
| --------------------------- | -------------------------------------- |
| SNAP (gross income)         | 130% of FPL                            |
| Medicaid (expansion states) | 138% of FPL                            |
| CHIP                        | up to 300% of FPL depending on state   |
| LIHEAP                      | 150% of FPL in most states             |
| ACA premium subsidies       | 100–400% of FPL                        |
| Pell Grant (Federal aid)    | Varies, most recipients under 250% FPL |

To calculate your own percentage: take your annual household income, divide it by the FPL for your household size, and multiply by 100.

### Number 2: Your household size

Household size matters a lot. Adding one person usually raises the income cutoff by several thousand dollars. Household rules vary — sometimes a roommate counts, sometimes they don't. A few quick rules:

- **SNAP** counts everyone who buys and prepares food together.
- **Medicaid** uses tax household rules (spouse + dependents on your tax return).
- **Housing programs** count everyone living in the unit.

### Number 3: Your assets (sometimes)

Some programs also check your bank account and other assets. SSI limits assets to $2,000 for an individual ($3,000 for a couple). Most states have dropped asset tests for SNAP, but a few still enforce them. Medicaid for long-term care has a strict asset limit. Most working-age Medicaid programs do not.

## How to find out which programs you qualify for — without doing 80 applications

You have three realistic options.

### Option A: Benefits.gov

Federal one-stop portal. Takes about 15 minutes, asks dozens of questions, returns a long list of programs that "may" apply to you. The problem: the list is generic, doesn't factor in state rules, and doesn't tell you the actual application link. Still a reasonable starting point.

### Option B: Your state's 211 hotline

Dial 211 from any phone. A live navigator can help you understand what's available in your state and give you the actual application links. Quality varies by state. In New York, Washington, and California it's excellent. In other states it's hit or miss.

## Frequently asked questions

### Do I have to be a U.S. citizen to qualify for benefits?

No, but rules vary by program. Lawful permanent residents (green card holders) qualify for most major programs after a waiting period, usually 5 years. Refugees, asylees, and some other statuses qualify immediately. Undocumented individuals are not eligible for most federal programs, but their U.S.-citizen children usually are, and some states (like California, New York, and Illinois) fund state-only programs that include undocumented residents.

### Will applying for benefits hurt my immigration case or chances of becoming a citizen?

Under current rules, using most benefits does **not** count against you for a green card or citizenship. The main exceptions are cash assistance (TANF, SSI) and long-term institutional Medicaid. SNAP, Medicaid, CHIP, housing assistance, and WIC are safe. Consult an immigration attorney if you're unsure.

### Can I apply for more than one program at a time?

Yes. You can and should. There's no rule against stacking benefits, and most of these programs are designed to work together.

### How long does it take to get approved?

It varies. SNAP is usually within 30 days (7 days for expedited in emergencies). Medicaid is usually 45 days. Housing assistance can take years because of waitlists. The Child Tax Credit and EITC happen when you file your taxes, usually 3 weeks after filing.

### What if I get denied?

Always appeal. Denials are frequently wrong, especially for SNAP and Medicaid. The appeal process is free, and success rates on first appeal are 30–50% for most programs.
