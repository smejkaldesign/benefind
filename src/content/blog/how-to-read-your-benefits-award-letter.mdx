## TL;DR

Every benefit program, SNAP, Medicaid, WIC, LIHEAP, housing assistance, TANF, SSI, sends you an **award letter** (sometimes called an "eligibility notice" or "notice of action") after it processes your application. These letters are notoriously confusing. They contain the approval decision, the dollar amount, start and end dates, your appeal rights, reporting obligations, and "certification period" deadlines that determine when you have to re-prove eligibility. Missing a detail can mean losing benefits you qualify for. This guide walks through **every section** of a typical award letter, translates the jargon, and tells you exactly what to do with each piece.

## Why award letters are so confusing

Benefit programs are run by different federal, state, and local agencies, each with its own forms, formats, jargon, and appeals processes. A SNAP letter looks nothing like a LIHEAP letter, which looks nothing like a Social Security letter. Even within the same program, each state formats letters differently.

The common thread: these letters are **legal documents**. They contain the binding determination of what you get, for how long, and what happens next. They also contain your **appeal deadlines**, which are often only 30-90 days, miss the deadline and you lose the right to contest the decision.

Let's walk through what you'll typically find in one.

## Section 1: The decision

Every award letter leads with the decision. Look for language like:

- "Your application has been **approved**"
- "Your application has been **denied**"
- "Your benefits are being **increased / decreased / terminated**"
- "You are **ineligible** as of [date]"

If approved, you'll see a benefit amount and effective date. If denied, you'll see a reason code or brief explanation.

### What to do

- **If approved:** note the start date and benefit amount. If those don't match what you expected, circle them and flag for appeal.
- **If denied:** don't panic. Denials are frequently wrong and are often reversed on appeal. Go straight to the denial reason and the appeal section (see below).

## Section 2: The benefit amount

The heart of the letter: how much you're getting, how often, and in what form.

### SNAP award letters

SNAP letters show a **monthly allotment** (for example, "$341 per month, starting May 1, 2026"). The allotment is calculated from household size, gross income, net income, and the "maximum allotment" for your household size minus 30% of your net income.

Look for:

- Monthly benefit amount
- Start date
- EBT card arrival date (usually 1-2 weeks after approval)
- Recertification date (usually 6-12 months out)

### Medicaid award letters

Medicaid letters usually don't list a dollar amount because Medicaid doesn't pay cash, it pays medical providers directly. Instead, you'll see:

- Coverage start date
- Member ID number
- Plan name (if you're in a managed care plan)
- Covered services (or a link/booklet)
- Renewal date (usually 12 months out)
- Co-pay information (if any)

### LIHEAP letters

LIHEAP letters show a one-time or seasonal benefit:

- Amount paid directly to your utility company
- Date the payment was or will be issued
- Application for the next year (if relevant)
- Crisis assistance information

### WIC letters

WIC "letters" are often just a printed summary at the end of your certification appointment:

- Participant list (who's enrolled)
- eWIC card number
- Monthly food package details
- Next appointment date
- Certification end date

### SSI / Social Security award letters

Social Security letters show:

- Monthly payment amount
- Start date
- Past-due benefits (if any)
- Deposit date
- Medicare eligibility (for SSDI recipients after 24 months)

## Section 3: The certification period

This is the sneakiest section. Most benefits have a **certification period**, a fixed window during which your eligibility is locked in. At the end, you must **recertify** (reapply with updated information) or your benefits stop, even if you're still eligible.

Typical certification periods:

| Program            | Typical cert period            |
| ------------------ | ------------------------------ |
| SNAP               | 6 or 12 months                 |
| Medicaid           | 12 months                      |
| WIC                | 6-12 months                    |
| TANF               | 6-12 months                    |
| LIHEAP             | 1 year or single season        |
| Housing assistance | 12 months                      |
| SSI                | Ongoing, with periodic reviews |

**The letter will tell you the exact end date.** Circle it. Set a calendar reminder for **60 days before** so you have time to gather documents and file the recertification.

### What happens if you miss recertification

Your benefits **stop**. In most programs, you have a short grace period (30-60 days) to submit late paperwork and have benefits reinstated without a new application. After that, you have to reapply from scratch.

## Section 4: Your rights and responsibilities

This is the fine print, but it's important.

### The "reporting obligations"

Most benefit programs require you to **report changes in income, household composition, address, or other factors** within 10 days. Failure to report can result in:

- Overpayment claims (they bill you for benefits you weren't eligible for)
- Disqualification from future benefits
- In extreme cases, fraud charges

Common changes that must be reported:

- New job or loss of job
- Income goes up or down substantially
- Someone moves in or out of the household
- You move to a new address
- Marriage or divorce
- Birth or adoption
- Death of a household member
- Change in immigration status

**Most states have a "simplified reporting" rule** for SNAP where you only need to report changes that push your income above 130% FPL between certifications. Check your state's rules, they vary a lot.

### Your right to appeal

Every denial or adverse action can be appealed. The letter will include:

- **Appeal deadline**, usually 30, 60, or 90 days
- **How to appeal**, phone, online, mail, or in person
- **Right to continue benefits pending appeal**, if you appeal a termination within 10 days, your benefits usually continue until the hearing

### Your right to an interpreter

Federal law requires agencies to provide interpreter services in your language free of charge. If the letter isn't in your language, call the number on it and request a translated version. In most states, you can get letters in Spanish, Vietnamese, Chinese, Arabic, Russian, and Korean at minimum.

## Section 5: The fine print you actually need to read

The bottom of every award letter usually has critical language you'll want to understand.

### Overpayment disclosure

If the agency later decides it paid you too much (due to a reporting error, household change, or their own mistake), they can claim an **overpayment** and either reduce future benefits or bill you for repayment. You have the right to appeal an overpayment claim and to request a waiver if the overpayment was not your fault.

### Case number

Your **case number** is the single most important thing on the letter. Keep it. You'll need it for every phone call, follow-up, or appeal. Write it down in multiple places, in your phone, on a notecard, in a folder with your other benefit documents.

### Worker name and contact info

Most letters list the name and phone number of the **case worker** assigned to your case. These people are overworked but can be enormously helpful if you reach them. Save this contact info.

### The "notice of action" date

Every letter has an effective date, the date it was mailed or issued. Appeal deadlines run from this date, not the date you receive it. If you get a letter 2 weeks after it was dated, you already have 2 weeks less to appeal.

## How to organize your benefits paperwork

If you're enrolled in multiple programs, you need a system. A physical folder or a digital folder with these sections:

1. **Active award letters**, one subfolder per program
2. **Applications submitted**, copies of everything you've filed
3. **Recertification due dates**, a running list with dates
4. **Appeal tracker**, any denials you're fighting
5. **Correspondence log**, phone call notes, email threads, dates and names

If paper, buy a cheap accordion folder. If digital, use whatever you already use, Google Drive, Dropbox, Apple Notes. The key is knowing where each letter lives so you don't lose the certification date.

## What to do if you disagree with your letter

### 1. Read it twice

Most "wrong" letters are actually correct but confusing. Read the letter slowly and look up any unfamiliar terms.

### 2. Call the number on the letter

The case worker can often fix simple errors over the phone, a transposed digit on your income, a wrong household size, a missing document.

### 3. Request a "fair hearing"

If the case worker can't fix it, formally request a **fair hearing** (also called an "administrative hearing" or "appeal"). This is a quasi-judicial proceeding where an administrative law judge reviews your case. You have the right to:

- Present evidence
- Bring a witness
- Have a representative (lawyer, legal aid attorney, or advocate)
- Receive a written decision

Legal aid organizations in every state can help with benefit appeals for free. Find yours at [lsc.gov/about-lsc/what-legal-aid/get-legal-help](https://www.lsc.gov/about-lsc/what-legal-aid/get-legal-help).

### 4. Keep getting benefits during the appeal

Critical: if you're fighting a **termination or reduction**, and you file the appeal **within 10 days** of the notice date, you're entitled to **aid paid pending**, your benefits continue at the old level until the hearing decision. Don't let the 10-day window expire.

### 5. Request a waiver for overpayments

If you're hit with an overpayment claim, you can request a waiver if:

- The overpayment was not your fault, AND
- Repayment would cause financial hardship, OR
- You relied in good faith on the agency's decision

Waivers are granted more often than people assume.

## Frequently asked questions

### How long do I have to keep old award letters?

Keep **active** letters indefinitely (or until the program ends). Keep **prior-year letters for at least 3 years** in case of an audit, overpayment claim, or recertification question.

### What if I lost my award letter?

Call the agency and request a duplicate. Most programs will mail or email a new copy within a few days. Your case number (if you still have it) makes this much faster.

### Can I see my benefit history online?

In most states, yes. SNAP, Medicaid, and TANF usually have state portals (MyBenefits, Access, Compass, etc.) where you can see your case history, current status, and upcoming recertification dates. Social Security has [my.ssa.gov](https://my.ssa.gov).

### What if the letter is in a language I don't read?

Call the number on it and request a translated version. Agencies are required to provide interpreter services under federal civil rights law.

### The letter says I owe an overpayment. Now what?

First, read the overpayment notice carefully to see what time period it covers and why. Second, request a **waiver** or **hearing** within the appeal deadline. Third, if the debt is correct but you can't pay, ask for a **payment plan** (typically $10-$50 per month deducted from your benefits). Never ignore an overpayment notice, unpaid debts can be sent to collections or withheld from tax refunds.

### Do I have to re-apply if my certification ends and I miss the deadline?

Usually yes, but check for a "retroactive reinstatement" window. Many states allow you to submit recertification paperwork up to 30 days late and have benefits restored without gap. After that, it's a fresh application.

## The bottom line

Award letters are legal documents. The decision, the amount, the certification period, the appeal deadline, and your reporting obligations are all in there, buried in jargon, but present. Read every letter the day it arrives. Circle the certification end date. Save the case number. File the letter somewhere you can find it again.
