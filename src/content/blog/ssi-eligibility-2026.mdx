## TL;DR

Supplemental Security Income (SSI) is a federal cash benefit for people who are 65 or older, blind, or disabled and have very limited income and assets. In 2026, the maximum federal SSI payment is **$994/month for an individual** and **$1,491/month for a couple**. The resource limit is **$2,000 for individuals** — unchanged since 1989. About 65% of first-time disability applications are denied, but many are approved on appeal.

## What SSI is (and what it isn't)

Supplemental Security Income is a need-based cash assistance program run by the Social Security Administration (SSA). It provides monthly payments to people who have a qualifying disability, blindness, or are 65 or older — and who have very low income and very few assets.

SSI is **not** Social Security retirement. You can be eligible for SSI even if you've never worked or paid into Social Security. What matters is your current financial situation, not your work history.

SSI is also **not** SSDI. The two programs are frequently confused:

| Feature | SSI | SSDI |
| ------- | --- | ---- |
| Basis | Financial need | Work history (credits paid into Social Security) |
| Work history required? | No | Yes |
| Income/asset limit? | Yes — strict | No |
| Payment amount | Based on federal rate (same for all) | Based on lifetime earnings record |
| Medicare? | No (Medicaid in most states) | Yes, after 24-month waiting period |
| Who administers? | SSA | SSA |

You can receive both SSI and SSDI at the same time if your SSDI payment is low enough — these are called "concurrent benefits."

## Who qualifies — the eligibility rules

SSI has three eligibility gates: categorical requirements, income limits, and resource limits. You must meet all three.

### Categorical requirement: age or disability

You must be one of the following:

- **Age 65 or older** (no disability required)
- **Blind** — visual acuity of 20/200 or less in the better eye with correction, or a visual field of 20 degrees or less
- **Disabled** — a physical or mental condition that prevents you from doing substantial work, has lasted or is expected to last at least 12 months, or is expected to result in death

For children, the disability standard is different: the impairment must severely limit daily activities compared to similarly aged children.

For disability claims, SSA uses a five-step evaluation process:

1. Are you doing Substantial Gainful Activity (SGA)? If you earn over **$1,620/month in 2026** (or $2,700/month if blind), SSA generally considers you not disabled.
2. Is your condition severe — does it significantly limit your ability to work?
3. Does your condition meet or equal one of SSA's listed impairments? (If yes, you're automatically approved.)
4. Can you do your past work?
5. Can you do any other work that exists in the national economy, given your age, education, and experience?

If SSA determines you can do some kind of substantial work, your disability claim is denied.

### Income limits

SSI doesn't have a simple dollar cutoff for income eligibility. Instead, SSA applies exclusions to your income and then determines whether the remaining "countable income" is below the benefit amount. Here's how it works:

**Earned income (from work):**
- First $65/month: excluded
- Remaining work income: reduces SSI by $0.50 for every $1 earned

**Unearned income (from Social Security, pensions, unemployment, etc.):**
- First $20/month: excluded (the "general income exclusion")
- Every dollar above $20: reduces SSI by $1

This means you can work and receive SSI simultaneously. Earned income is treated more generously (only a 50% offset) to encourage employment.

As a rough guide, the approximate **maximum earned income before SSI fully phases out** for an individual in 2026 is around **$2,073/month** — but this varies based on your specific exclusions and circumstances.

**What does NOT count as income:**
- SNAP (food stamps)
- WIC benefits
- Tax refunds
- Loans that must be repaid
- Educational scholarships and grants used for tuition
- First $20/month of any income (general exclusion)
- First $65/month of earned income

### Resource (asset) limits

This is where many people are surprised and often denied. SSI has strict asset limits:

| Category | Limit |
| -------- | ----- |
| Individual | $2,000 |
| Couple (both receiving SSI) | $3,000 |
| If parent applying for a child | +$2,000 per parent |

**The $2,000 limit has not been adjusted for inflation since 1989.** In 1989 dollars, $2,000 had roughly the same purchasing power as $5,000 today. Bipartisan legislation to raise these limits to $10,000/$20,000 has been introduced but not yet passed as of April 2026.

**What counts toward the resource limit:**
- Cash on hand
- Bank accounts (checking, savings, CDs)
- Stocks, bonds, mutual funds
- Other property (not your home)

**What does NOT count:**
- Your primary home (regardless of value)
- One vehicle (the most commonly claimed exemption)
- Household goods and personal effects
- Burial funds up to $1,500
- Life insurance policies with a combined face value under $1,500
- ABLE accounts (up to $100,000 — a critical planning tool for disabled individuals)
- Some educational savings plans

If you have over $2,000 in countable resources, you will be denied SSI until you spend down below the limit.

### Citizenship and residency

- U.S. citizens and nationals qualify
- Certain categories of qualified non-citizens (refugees, asylees, lawful permanent residents with 40+ qualifying work quarters, trafficking victims, certain veterans and military members)
- Most non-citizen categories are limited to 7 years of SSI benefits
- Must reside in the U.S. or Northern Mariana Islands
- Cannot be absent from the U.S. for 30+ consecutive days

## SSI payment amounts in 2026

### Federal SSI rates

| Beneficiary | Monthly payment | Annual total |
| ----------- | --------------- | ------------ |
| Individual  | $994            | $11,928      |
| Couple (both eligible) | $1,491 | $17,892    |

These are the 2026 rates after the annual Cost of Living Adjustment (COLA) of 2.8%, up from $967/month individual and $1,450/month couple in 2025.

### How income reduces your payment

Your countable income is subtracted from the federal SSI rate to get your actual payment:

**Your monthly SSI payment = Federal rate − Countable income**

**Example for a single adult:**
- Federal SSI rate: $994
- Monthly wages: $500
- General exclusion: −$20 (any income)
- Earned income exclusion: −$65 (work income)
- Remaining countable income: $500 − $65 = $435; then $435 × 50% = $217.50
- Monthly SSI payment: $994 − $217.50 = **$776.50**

### Living arrangement affects payment

If you live in someone else's household and don't pay a fair share of food and shelter costs, SSA reduces your payment by one-third of the federal benefit rate. This is called "in-kind support and maintenance" (ISM). In 2026, the one-third reduction is approximately $331/month.

## State supplements

Many states add money on top of the federal SSI payment. If you live in a state with a supplement, your actual monthly check will be higher than the federal figures above.

**States with NO state supplement:**
Arizona, Arkansas, Mississippi, North Dakota, Tennessee, West Virginia

**States with the most generous supplements (SSA-administered):**
California, Delaware, DC, Hawaii, Iowa, Michigan, Montana, Nevada, New Jersey, Pennsylvania, Rhode Island, Vermont

California's supplement is among the highest — typically adding $160–$200+/month depending on living arrangement, bringing California SSI recipients to roughly $1,150–$1,200/month in 2026.

To find your state's exact supplement, contact your local SSA office or search "[your state] SSI supplement 2026."

## SSI vs. SSDI — the full comparison

| Feature | SSI | SSDI |
| ------- | --- | ---- |
| Full name | Supplemental Security Income | Social Security Disability Insurance |
| Who qualifies | Any age with disability (or 65+); based on financial need | Workers who paid Social Security taxes and become disabled |
| Work history required | No | Yes (typically 5–10 years) |
| Payment amount | Up to $994/month federal (2026) | Varies — based on earnings record, averaging ~$1,540/month |
| Resource/asset limit | Yes ($2,000 individual) | No |
| Income limit | Yes | No (once approved) |
| Health coverage | Medicaid (most states automatic) | Medicare (after 24-month waiting period) |
| Back pay | To application date | To 5 months after onset date (12-month max retroactive) |
| Family benefits | No | Yes — dependents can receive auxiliary benefits |

If you have a work history and become disabled, you may qualify for SSDI instead of (or in addition to) SSI. If your SSDI payment is below the SSI rate, you can receive both simultaneously to bring your total income up to the SSI level.

## How to apply for SSI — step by step

### Step 1: Gather your documents

Before starting, collect:
- Social Security card and proof of age (birth certificate)
- Proof of U.S. citizenship or immigration status
- Financial records: bank statements, investment records, property deeds, car titles
- Proof of income (pay stubs, benefit letters, pension statements)
- Medical evidence: records from doctors, hospitals, therapists; names and addresses of all treating providers; names and dosages of all medications
- Employment history (job titles, dates, duties — for disability evaluation)
- For children: school records, IEP documentation

### Step 2: Start the application

**Online:** You can begin the SSI application for adults at ssa.gov/apply/ssi. Note that as of 2026, the online application must be completed in conjunction with a phone or in-person appointment.

**Phone:** Call 1-800-772-1213 (TTY: 1-800-325-0778). SSA field offices are experiencing extended wait times in 2025–2026 due to staffing reductions.

**In person:** Visit your local Social Security office. An appointment is strongly recommended.

### Step 3: Complete the disability determination (if applicable)

If you're applying for SSI based on disability, SSA will send your file to your state's Disability Determination Services (DDS) for medical evaluation. They review your medical records and may schedule a consultative examination with an SSA-contracted doctor.

### Step 4: Receive the decision

**Age-based applications (65+):** Generally processed in 1–3 months.

**Disability-based applications:** Highly variable. The initial determination typically takes 3–7 months. Due to SSA staffing pressures in 2025–2026, waits at some stages are longer.

### Step 5: Know about retroactivity

SSI payments are retroactive to the **date of application** (or the date you became eligible, if later), not the date of approval. If your application takes 6 months to process and is approved, you'll receive 6 months of back pay.

## The appeals process

About **65% of initial SSI disability applications are denied**. This is not unusual — it's the expected rate, and many denials are overturned on appeal. Do not give up after an initial denial.

### The four levels of appeal

| Level | Time to request | What happens |
| ----- | --------------- | ------------ |
| Reconsideration | 60 days from denial | A different SSA examiner reviews your file |
| Hearing before an ALJ | 60 days from reconsideration denial | Administrative Law Judge reviews your case; you can testify, bring witnesses, present evidence |
| Appeals Council review | 60 days from ALJ decision | Appeals Council reviews for legal error |
| Federal district court | After Appeals Council | File a civil suit in federal court |

The ALJ hearing is often the most important stage — approval rates at hearings are significantly higher than at initial determination. Getting legal representation (a disability attorney) is highly advisable at this stage. Disability attorneys typically work on contingency, taking 25% of back pay up to $7,200, meaning you pay nothing unless you win.

### Tips for appeals

- Get comprehensive medical records from all treating providers
- If you don't have a doctor, seek care immediately — lack of medical evidence is a top reason for denial
- Keep all appointments scheduled by SSA
- Never miss an appeal deadline (60 days from the denial date)
- Consider a disability attorney or advocate for the ALJ hearing stage

## Common mistakes to avoid

**Confusing SSI with SSDI.** SSI is need-based (no work history required). SSDI requires sufficient work credits. If you've never worked, you may still qualify for SSI; you just won't qualify for SSDI.

**Having over $2,000 in the bank.** This is the single most common reason for denial and overpayment notices. If you have savings over $2,000, you'll need to spend down before qualifying. Plan carefully — spend on legitimate expenses like rent, medical bills, or items you need.

**Not reporting changes.** SSI recipients must report any change in income, living situation, household composition, or resources to SSA. Failing to report can result in overpayments — and SSA will pursue repayment.

**Assuming the first denial is final.** About 65% of initial disability claims are denied. The system expects many people to appeal. An ALJ hearing is where many people finally win approval. Don't give up.

**Not working because you're afraid of losing benefits.** SSI has work incentive programs specifically designed to let you try working without losing benefits. The Ticket to Work program, ABLE accounts, and earned income exclusions all help working SSI recipients keep more of what they earn.

**Not knowing about ABLE accounts.** ABLE accounts let individuals with disabilities who became disabled before age 26 save up to $100,000 without counting toward the $2,000 resource limit. This is a critical planning tool that many SSI recipients don't know about.

## Frequently asked questions

### What is the SSI payment for 2026?

The federal SSI payment is **$994/month for an individual** and **$1,491/month for a couple** where both are eligible. Many states add a supplement on top of this — California adds approximately $160–$200/month, making the total around $1,150–$1,200 for California recipients.

### Can I work while receiving SSI?

Yes. SSA encourages work. For every $2 you earn above $65/month, your SSI payment decreases by $1. The first $65/month of earned income is completely excluded. You can work and receive partial SSI payments simultaneously. Programs like the Ticket to Work and student earned income exclusions provide additional protections.

### What is the $2,000 resource limit?

If you have more than $2,000 in countable resources (cash, bank accounts, stocks, non-exempt property), you are not eligible for SSI. Your primary home, one vehicle, household goods, and certain other items don't count. ABLE account savings (up to $100,000) also don't count. This limit has not been raised since 1989, despite bipartisan support for increasing it.

### How long does SSI take to get approved?

For age-based (65+) applications: typically 1–3 months. For disability-based applications: initial determinations take 3–7 months on average, often longer in 2025–2026 due to SSA staffing reductions. If you need to appeal to an Administrative Law Judge, the total process can take 1–3 years.

### Does SSI automatically qualify me for Medicaid?

In most states, yes. If you're approved for SSI, you're automatically enrolled in Medicaid without a separate application. However, about 11 states (including Connecticut, Hawaii, Illinois, Indiana, Minnesota, Missouri, New Hampshire, North Dakota, Ohio, Oklahoma, and Virginia) require a separate Medicaid application even for SSI recipients.

### What is the difference between SSI and Social Security?

"Social Security" usually refers to retirement benefits or SSDI — both are based on your work history and the taxes you paid into the Social Security system. SSI is funded differently (general federal revenue) and is based entirely on financial need. You can qualify for SSI with no work history at all.

### Can children receive SSI?

Yes. Children under 18 can receive SSI if they have a qualifying disability and their family's income and resources are below the limits. For children, SSA applies a parental deeming formula that counts a portion of the parents' income and resources toward the child's eligibility.

### What happens to SSI when I turn 18?

When an SSI recipient turns 18, SSA conducts an age-18 redetermination using adult disability criteria (which are different from children's criteria). Some recipients who qualified as children will not qualify as adults. SSA will notify you before your 18th birthday and review your case.
