## TL;DR

SBIR and STTR are the two biggest non-dilutive funding programs in the United States. Both give small businesses **up to $2M in federal research grants** without asking for equity. The short version: **SBIR** is for small businesses doing their own R&D; **STTR** requires formal partnership with a research institution (usually a university or national lab). SBIR has a much larger funding pool ($3.2B/year vs ~$440M for STTR), higher success rates, and less administrative complexity. STTR is the right choice when your core technology originates from academic research. Both are split into Phase I ($50K-$295K), Phase II ($750K-$2M), and an unfunded Phase III commercialization. This guide walks through the decision, agency-by-agency rules, and how to apply.

## What SBIR and STTR actually are

SBIR stands for **Small Business Innovation Research**. STTR stands for **Small Business Technology Transfer**. They're mandated by the Small Business Act, and every federal agency with an extramural R&D budget over $100M has to set aside a slice for them.

- **SBIR set-aside:** 3.2% of qualifying agency R&D budgets
- **STTR set-aside:** 0.45% of qualifying agency R&D budgets

Eleven federal agencies run SBIR programs. Only five run STTR (DoD, HHS/NIH, NSF, DOE, NASA). Together they distribute about **$4 billion per year** to small businesses. It is by far the largest source of non-dilutive tech startup funding in the United States.

Both programs are **non-dilutive**. The government does not take equity, does not take debt, does not take IP (you own what you invent). They pay you to do research they want done, and then help you commercialize it.

## The key difference: do you need a research partner?

This is the decision you need to make first.

### SBIR: no research partner required

With SBIR, you do the work yourself. Your small business performs **at least 67% of the work in Phase I** and **at least 50% in Phase II**. You can subcontract up to 33% (Phase I) or 50% (Phase II) to outside parties, including universities, but you don't have to.

Choose SBIR if:

- You have technical expertise in-house
- You don't need a university lab or specific faculty PI
- You want maximum control and speed
- Your commercialization path is direct (small business to market)

### STTR: formal partnership required

STTR **requires** a formal, written cooperative R&D agreement between your small business and a "research institution" (usually a university, but can be a federally funded R&D center or non-profit research lab). The research institution must perform **at least 30%** of the work in both Phase I and Phase II. Your small business must perform **at least 40%**.

Choose STTR if:

- Your core technology came out of university research
- You need specialized faculty expertise or lab equipment
- A professor invented something and you're spinning it out
- You need access to facilities (cleanrooms, animal models, specialized instruments) you can't affordably buy

**A common pattern:** a grad student invents something, the university licenses it to a spin-out, and the spin-out uses STTR to fund early de-risking with the original lab still involved. STTR was designed for exactly this.

## Funding amounts and phases

Both programs use the same three-phase structure. Award amounts vary by agency, but here are the **2026 hard caps**:

| Phase     | Duration    | SBIR award       | STTR award       |
| --------- | ----------- | ---------------- | ---------------- |
| Phase I   | 6-12 months | Up to $295,924   | Up to $295,924   |
| Phase II  | 24 months   | Up to $1,972,828 | Up to $1,972,828 |
| Phase III | ,           | No federal funds | No federal funds |

Agencies can request **waivers** to exceed these caps (NIH regularly funds Phase II above $2M for expensive biomedical work; DoD does it for certain platforms). In practice, most awards come in at or below the cap.

### What each phase is for

- **Phase I** is a feasibility study. You're proving the technical approach works, that the problem is real, and that your solution is at least plausible.
- **Phase II** is substantial R&D. You're building a prototype, doing clinical trials, hardening the science, getting to a testable demonstration. This is where the real money is.
- **Phase III** is commercialization. The federal government does NOT fund Phase III with SBIR dollars, but agencies can (and do) buy your product via sole-source contracts without re-competing. Phase III is also when you raise private capital, partner with primes, or sell directly to customers.

## Who can apply?

Both programs have strict eligibility rules. Your business must:

1. Be a **US for-profit** small business
2. Have **500 or fewer employees** (including affiliates)
3. Be **at least 50% US-owned** by individuals who are US citizens or permanent residents
4. Be independently owned and operated (not a subsidiary of a large corporation)

### The "venture-backed" twist

Historically, VC-backed companies were locked out of SBIR/STTR. In 2011, the Reauthorization Act allowed majority VC-, hedge fund-, or private equity-owned small businesses to apply to NIH, DOE, NSF, and a few others, but only up to **25% of the NIH's SBIR pool** and **15% at other participating agencies**. DoD and most other SBIR programs still require majority individual US-citizen ownership.

If you're a VC-backed startup, check the specific agency rules before assuming you're eligible.

### Principal Investigator (PI) location

- **SBIR:** The PI must be **primarily employed** by the small business (more than 50% of their time).
- **STTR:** The PI can be from **either** the small business or the research institution. This is a huge flexibility advantage for spin-outs where the inventing professor is still at the university.

## The 11 agencies, where should you apply?

Not every agency is a good fit for every idea. Here's a quick guide.

### DoD (Department of Defense), largest SBIR program

- ~$2B/year across Army, Navy, Air Force, Defense Health Agency, etc.
- Topics are specific and published three times a year as "solicitations"
- Emphasis on defense, cybersecurity, hardware, materials, space, autonomy
- **Phase I is shorter (6 months) and tighter ($250K cap)** but Phase II success rate is higher
- Best for: hardware, dual-use tech, defense-adjacent software

### HHS/NIH, second largest, best for biotech

- ~$1.2B/year
- Three standard receipt dates a year
- Highest Phase II funding caps in practice (NIH frequently waives and goes to $3M+)
- Best for: drugs, medical devices, diagnostics, health IT, biotech

### NSF, best for speculative deep tech

- ~$200M/year
- Very broad topic scope, if it's scientifically interesting and commercializable, NSF will consider it
- Shortest application (15 pages)
- Best for: early-stage deep tech, academic spin-outs, novel materials

### DOE (Energy), best for clean tech and hard sciences

- ~$280M/year
- Strong focus on energy, grid, batteries, nuclear, fusion, carbon capture
- Best for: clean tech, materials science, anything power-related

### NASA, best for space and aerospace

- ~$200M/year
- Specific technical topics published annually
- Strong commercialization support through NASA SBIR ARR and TAP
- Best for: space, aerospace, robotics, sensing, propulsion

### Other agencies

USDA (agriculture and food), ED (education technology), DHS (homeland security), DOC/NOAA (weather, oceans), EPA (environmental), DOT (transportation). Smaller pools but less competition.

## Success rates

Success rates vary wildly by agency and topic. Ballpark numbers for 2024-2025:

| Agency | Phase I award rate |
| ------ | ------------------ |
| DoD    | ~15%               |
| NIH    | ~18%               |
| NSF    | ~10-12%            |
| DOE    | ~12%               |
| NASA   | ~15%               |

**Phase II success rate** is much higher, usually 40-60% of Phase I winners get invited to submit and 50-70% of those get funded. Getting Phase I is the hard part.

## The application process

Both programs use a similar submission pipeline, though each agency has quirks.

### Step 1: Register (allow 6-8 weeks)

Before you can apply, you need:

- **SAM.gov** registration (government contractor database)
- **UEI** (Unique Entity Identifier), replaces the old DUNS number
- **SBA Company Registry**, SBIR-specific registry at [sbir.gov/registration](https://www.sbir.gov/registration)
- Agency-specific accounts (eRA Commons for NIH, SITIS for DoD, research.gov for NSF, etc.)

SAM.gov registration can take **30-45 days**. Start immediately.

### Step 2: Pick a solicitation

Each agency publishes solicitations, lists of topics they'll fund. DoD publishes three a year; NIH has three standard receipt dates; NSF has two. Read the solicitation carefully and match your proposal to a specific topic number.

### Step 3: Write the proposal

A typical SBIR Phase I proposal has:

- **Cover page** (company info, PI, subcontractor info for STTR)
- **Project summary / abstract** (non-confidential)
- **Commercialization plan** (market, competitors, customers, path to revenue)
- **Technical volume**, the meat of it, usually 15-25 pages
- **Budget and budget justification**
- **Letters of support** from customers, mentors, investors
- **Biographical sketches** for PI and key personnel

### Step 4: Submit and wait

Review takes **4-8 months** at most agencies. You get a score, reviewer comments, and either an award or a non-award. If you don't get it, **resubmit with revisions**, many agencies explicitly invite resubmissions, and win rates on the second try are often higher.

## Common mistakes to avoid

1. **Picking the wrong agency.** NIH is the right place for a drug, not DoD. Read the topic list first.
2. **Weak commercialization plan.** Reviewers are looking for real market fit, not a dream. Letters of interest from real customers matter more than a TAM chart.
3. **Treating it like a research grant.** SBIR/STTR is not NIH R01, reviewers want to see a path to a product, not a paper.
4. **Underfunding the budget.** You can ask for the cap. Don't leave money on the table because you think it "looks greedy."
5. **Ignoring the non-federal match.** DoD Phase II often requires or strongly prefers a commercialization matching commitment.

## Frequently asked questions

### Can I apply to SBIR and STTR for the same project?

No, you pick one per submission. You can apply to different agencies simultaneously for different projects, but you can't double-dip on a single concept.

### How long does it take from application to money in the bank?

Assume **9-12 months** from submission to funds for most agencies. A few (DoD Rapid Innovation, NSF America's Seed Fund) are faster.

### Can I do SBIR/STTR while raising a VC round?

Yes, but watch your ownership structure. If VCs own more than 50% of your company, you lose SBIR eligibility at most agencies. NIH, DOE, and NSF have partial carve-outs for VC-owned firms.

### Does SBIR funding affect dilution for later rounds?

Not directly. SBIR money is non-dilutive, so it doesn't change your cap table. But SBIR money signals to investors that a technical agency has validated your work, which often helps valuations.

### Who owns the IP?

The small business. SBIR/STTR includes "data rights" protections and march-in rights are extremely rare. You keep ownership of anything you invent with SBIR dollars.

### Can I use SBIR money to pay salary?

Yes. Labor is usually the largest line item. You can pay yourself, your employees, and any direct subcontractors.

### What's the best way to learn more?

Start at [sbir.gov](https://www.sbir.gov), the official cross-agency portal. Each agency has its own resources (NIH has [sbir.nih.gov](https://sbir.nih.gov), DoD has [dodsbirsttr.mil](https://www.dodsbirsttr.mil)). Attend the free SBIR Road Tour events that run nationally each year.

## The bottom line

**Choose SBIR** if you have in-house technical expertise and want to move fast. **Choose STTR** if your tech originated from a university lab and you need formal research partnership. Either way, you're looking at up to $2M in non-dilutive funding, no equity, no debt, the single best deal in federal small-business support.
