## TL;DR

Every major state runs employer-funded workforce training programs that reimburse businesses for the cost of training new hires and upskilling existing employees. These aren't competitive grant programs with a single annual deadline — most are **open year-round**, with no employer match required in most states. California's ETP tops out at **$850K per company**. Texas's Skills Development Fund goes up to **$500K**. New York's Excelsior Jobs Program pays **6.85% of gross wages per new job for up to 10 years**. Florida reimburses up to **75% of training costs**. Pennsylvania gives **$2,000 per employee trained**. This guide covers the top five states in detail, plus how to find your state's program.

## Why state training grants matter

State workforce training programs are some of the most underused business incentives in the country. Unlike SBIR or 8(a) certification, they don't require niche eligibility or months of preparation. Most are available to any employer with full-time W-2 employees, open year-round, and processed quickly.

The funding source is what makes these programs sustainable: most are funded by state unemployment insurance taxes that employers already pay. States collect these employer contributions and redeploy a portion as training reimbursements — the theory being that a more skilled workforce reduces unemployment claims downstream. This means the programs are relatively stable and not subject to annual congressional appropriations battles.

**Who benefits most:**
- Companies hiring at scale (manufacturing, tech, healthcare)
- Businesses upskilling existing employees for new technology or equipment
- Companies setting up new facilities or production lines in a state
- Employers in high-demand industries where skill gaps are well-documented

**Who often misses out:**
- Small employers who don't know the programs exist
- Professional services firms who assume they don't qualify
- Remote-first companies who don't realize their in-state employees may qualify

The programs profiled below represent the five largest state economies by GDP and cover the programs in enough detail to apply. If your state isn't covered, use the framework at the end of this guide to find your state's equivalent.

## State-by-state guide

### California: Employment Training Panel (ETP)

The ETP is California's flagship workforce training program and one of the largest in the country. It operates as a **performance-based reimbursement contract** — meaning ETP pays you after training is complete and trainees have met a retention requirement.

**Funding authority:** California Unemployment Insurance Code, Section 10200 et seq.
**Administered by:** Employment Training Panel, a division of the California Labor and Workforce Development Agency
**Funded by:** Employment Training Tax (ETT) paid by California employers

#### Who qualifies

Any employer subject to California's Employment Training Tax can apply. The ETT is a subset of the state unemployment insurance tax; if you file California payroll taxes with a CEAN prefix of 699 or lower, you likely pay the ETT. Priority industries include manufacturing, technology, biotech, healthcare, and clean energy. Out-of-state businesses relocating to or expanding in California may also qualify.

#### Funding amounts

| Contract Type | Maximum Award |
|---|---|
| Standard single employer | Up to $600,000 per fiscal year |
| Critical proposals (strategic industries) | Up to $850,000 |
| Multiple employer contracts (MEC) | Up to $850,000 |

Reimbursement is calculated per trainee based on training type and delivery method. Training must be **8-200 hours per trainee**, and a **90-day retention period** is required after training — ETP pays only after the trained employee has been retained for 90 days.

#### Eligible training

ETP funds job-specific skills training only — not basic skills, academic education, or general orientation. Eligible training areas include:

- Manufacturing (machining, quality control, lean operations)
- Information technology and software development
- Healthcare and biotechnology
- Engineering and advanced manufacturing
- Green and clean technology

Delivery methods include classroom/lab, e-learning, computer-based training, and productive lab (on-the-job training with structured curriculum).

#### How to apply

Submit a proposal through ETP's online system (etp.ca.gov). The ETP panel meets regularly — typically monthly — to approve contracts. Applications are accepted year-round, and the typical timeline from application to contract execution is 3-6 months. Larger or more complex proposals may take longer.

**California tip:** Work with a Training Agency (TA) that has experience navigating ETP applications. ETP has a network of approved TAs who can help structure your proposal and deliver the training, often at no upfront cost to the employer.

#### Other California programs

- **High Road Training Partnerships (HRTP):** Industry-focused training collaboratives with emphasis on equity and job quality. $18.6M available for FY 2025. Grants go to industry/labor/education partnerships, not individual employers.
- **Cal Competes Tax Credit:** Up to $180M/year for job-creating businesses, with a training component.

### Texas: Skills Development Fund (SDF)

Texas's Skills Development Fund is one of the most employer-friendly training programs in the country — no employer match, fast approval, year-round applications.

**Funding authority:** Texas Labor Code, Chapter 303
**Administered by:** Texas Workforce Commission (TWC)
**Funded by:** State appropriations

#### Who qualifies

Any business creating or improving jobs in Texas can apply, provided they partner with an eligible training institution — a public community or technical college, the Texas Engineering Extension Service (TEEX), or a community-based organization. There are no industry restrictions. Trainees must be full-time, W-2 employees.

#### Funding amounts

| Feature | Details |
|---|---|
| Maximum per employer | $500,000 per single-employer grant |
| Consortium awards | Higher for multi-employer grants |
| Average per trainee | $2,400 |
| Employer match | Not required |
| Grant period | 12 months |

#### Eligible training

SDF funds customized training for new workers being hired or skills upgrades for existing workers. Training must be delivered by an eligible training provider and focus on:

- Technical and job-specific skills
- Industry certifications
- Safety training and compliance
- Process improvement and operational skills

#### How to apply

1. Partner with an eligible training institution first — community colleges and TEEX are the most common partners
2. Contact a TWC regional business liaison for guidance on your project concept
3. Submit a proposal (accepted year-round)
4. Typical approval timeline: 30-90 days

**Texas tip:** Find your regional TWC business liaison at twc.texas.gov — they'll help you identify the right training partner and structure your proposal before you formally apply.

#### Other Texas programs

| Program | Details |
|---|---|
| **Upskill Texas** | $150K-$500K for upskilling existing employees; up to $3K per trainee; 100% technical training required |
| **High Demand Job Training (HDJT)** | Competitive grants for high-priority workforce needs |
| **Texas Industry Partnership (TIP)** | Matching funds for industry-driven workforce development |

### New York: Excelsior Jobs Program

The Excelsior Jobs Program is New York's primary business attraction and retention incentive. Unlike ETP or SDF (reimbursement programs), Excelsior offers **refundable tax credits** tied to job creation and capital investment.

**Funding authority:** Article 17, New York Economic Development Law
**Administered by:** Empire State Development (ESD)

#### Who qualifies

Businesses in targeted industries can apply for a multi-year credit agreement. Target industries include:

- Financial services and insurance
- Manufacturing
- Software and information technology services
- Scientific R&D and high technology
- Agriculture
- Distribution centers
- Life sciences
- Clean energy

Companies must commit to creating new jobs or retaining existing jobs and making capital investments in New York.

#### Credit amounts (refundable, over up to 10 years)

| Credit Type | Amount |
|---|---|
| **Jobs Tax Credit** | Up to 6.85% of gross wages per net new job per year |
| **Investment Tax Credit** | 2% of qualified capital investments |
| **R&D Tax Credit** | Up to 50% of Federal R&D credit (up to 6% of NY R&D expenditures) |
| **Real Property Tax Credit** | Available in certain circumstances |

The Jobs Tax Credit at 6.85% of wages is the headline benefit. A company creating 50 jobs averaging $80,000/year in wages earns a credit of approximately **$274,000 per year** — for up to 10 years.

**2026 update:** Governor Hochul's 2025-26 Executive Budget extends Excelsior from 2029 to 2039 and creates enhanced benefits for businesses in the semiconductor supply chain.

#### Employee Training Incentive Program (ETIP)

Separate from Excelsior, ETIP is a refundable tax credit for employers making significant capital investment who also invest in employee training:

- Up to **50% of training costs**, capped at **$10,000 per employee**
- Available to employers making qualified capital expenditures
- Must upgrade or improve employee productivity

#### How to apply

Apply through Empire State Development at esd.ny.gov. Excelsior is a competitive program; companies submit a business case and negotiate a credit agreement with ESD. The process typically takes 2-4 months from initial application to approved agreement.

#### Other New York programs

- **START-UP NY:** Tax-free zones near universities. Zero state and local taxes for 10 years for qualifying businesses.
- **Hire-A-Vet Credit:** $5,000 per qualifying veteran hired; $15,000 for disabled veterans.
- **Semiconductor Manufacturing Workforce Training Incentive Program:** New 2026 program for the semiconductor industry.

### Florida: Quick Response Training (QRT) and Incumbent Worker Training (IWT)

Florida runs two complementary training programs: QRT for new hires and IWT for existing employees.

**Administered by:** CareerSource Florida / Florida Department of Economic Opportunity

#### Quick Response Training (QRT)

QRT targets businesses that are **new to Florida or undergoing significant expansion** and creating new permanent full-time jobs.

Priority industries: manufacturing, IT, life sciences, financial services, aviation and aerospace, clean energy.

**How QRT works:** CareerSource Florida provides customized training grants for new hires. Grant amounts vary by project scope and job creation numbers. In FY 2023-24, $6.5 million was awarded to 24 businesses, training over 2,700 new hires. No employer match is required.

#### Incumbent Worker Training (IWT)

IWT reimburses up to **75% of training costs** for existing employees who need skills upgrades.

| Feature | Details |
|---|---|
| Reimbursement rate | Up to 75% of eligible training costs |
| Typical grant range | $10,000-$50,000 per business |
| FY 2023-24 totals | $2.9M to 69 businesses, 2,700+ employees |
| Employer match | 25% (employer pays 25% of training costs) |

**Eligible training for IWT:** Any training that demonstrably closes a skills gap in the existing workforce.

#### How to apply (both programs)

1. Create an account on CareerSource Florida's website (careersourceflorida.com)
2. Complete the "See if you Qualify" wizard
3. Submit online application
4. Approval is typically quick; funds available year-round until exhausted

CareerSource Florida operates through a network of regional workforce boards — contact your regional board directly for the fastest response.

#### Other Florida programs

| Program | Details |
|---|---|
| **Qualified Target Industry (QTI) Tax Refund** | $3,000-$6,000 per new full-time job; up to $12,000 in enterprise zones |
| **High Impact Performance Incentives (HIPI)** | Large-scale incentives for significant job creation in target industries |

### Pennsylvania: WEDnetPA

Pennsylvania's Workforce and Economic Development Network (WEDnetPA) is the state's primary employer training grant — simple application, no match required, open annually.

**Administered by:** PA Department of Community & Economic Development (DCED), delivered through partner colleges and universities
**Funded by:** State appropriation

#### Who qualifies

- Pennsylvania-based companies
- Employees must be PA residents employed in Pennsylvania
- Employees must earn at least **$16.74/hour** (excluding benefits)
- Companies can receive funding in **2 out of 3 consecutive years** (or 3 out of 5 years)

#### Funding amounts

| Feature | Amount |
|---|---|
| Per employee trained | Up to $2,000 |
| Per company per year | Up to $100,000 |
| Employer match | Not required |

#### Eligible training

WEDnetPA funds skill-building for the employee's current job or advancement to a higher role. Eligible training areas include:

- Technical and job-specific skills
- Industry certifications
- Computer and IT skills
- Workplace safety and compliance

Training must be delivered by an approved WEDnet partner institution — community colleges, state universities, and other approved providers.

#### How to apply

Apply starting **August 1, 2025** for FY 2025-2026 at wednetpa.com/Apply. Work with a WEDnet partner institution (they help structure the training plan and submit paperwork). Funds are distributed first-come, first-served, so applying early in the cycle is important.

#### Other Pennsylvania programs

| Program | Details |
|---|---|
| **PA First** | Customized job training; $450-$850 per trainee |
| **PAsmart** | Apprenticeship and workforce development grants |
| **Foundations in Industry Training (FIT)** | Covers formal instruction costs for registered apprenticeship programs |
| **Schools-to-Work (STW) Grants** | Up to $250K; builds programs to fill labor market needs |

## How to find your state's program

If your state isn't covered above, here's how to find the equivalent program:

1. **Search "[your state] workforce training grant employer"** — most states have a program with a clear name
2. **Contact your state workforce development agency** — every state has one, often called "[State] Department of Workforce Development" or "[State] Workforce Commission"
3. **Contact your local Small Business Development Center (SBDC)** — they're federally funded, free to businesses, and maintain up-to-date knowledge of state incentive programs
4. **Check your state's economic development office** — they often have an employer incentive guide or direct contact for training programs

Most states outside the top five that have workforce training programs include: NC (Customized Training Program), GA (Quick Start), OH (Job Ready Ohio), MI (Going PRO Talent Fund), CO (Colorado Workforce Development Council grants), AZ (Arizona Job Training Program), and WA (Opportunity Grant).

## How to apply (general process for any state)

The application process varies by state, but follows a common pattern:

1. **Determine eligibility:** Full-time W-2 employees, operating in the state, industry eligibility (if any)
2. **Identify a training provider:** Many programs require you to partner with an approved training institution (community college, technical school, etc.)
3. **Define the training plan:** Specific skills, training hours, number of employees, delivery method
4. **Apply:** Most states have online applications; some require an initial phone consultation
5. **Get approved before you spend:** These are reimbursement programs — don't start training until you have approval
6. **Execute the training:** Maintain attendance records, completion records, payroll documentation
7. **Submit reimbursement:** After training is complete and retention period (if any) is met, submit documentation for payment

**Documentation to have ready:**
- Proof of business registration in the state
- Payroll records showing employee wage rates and hours
- Employee roster for training participants
- Training provider's credentials/approval status
- Training curriculum outline
- Cost breakdown (training fees, materials, instructor costs)

## Frequently asked questions

### Do I need to create new jobs to qualify?

It depends on the program. Most states have two tracks: (1) new hire training for businesses adding jobs, and (2) incumbent worker training for upskilling existing employees. You can often access both, depending on your situation.

### My company is remote-first. Do my employees qualify?

Generally yes, if your employees are physically located in the state and you pay state payroll taxes there. Remote work has made this more complex — check with your state program administrator and confirm that employees working remotely from the state are covered.

### Can I train employees in multiple states simultaneously?

You would need to apply separately in each state. There's no federal program that coordinates across state workforce training programs. In practice, if you're a multi-state employer, it's worth applying in your top 2-3 states simultaneously.

### How long does reimbursement take after training?

Typically 4-12 weeks depending on the state and program. California's ETP has a 90-day retention requirement after training before they'll process payment, which means the full cycle from training completion to payment can be 4-6 months.

### What if my employees make below the wage threshold (Pennsylvania's $16.74/hour)?

Lower-wage employees are often covered by different programs. Workforce development programs funded through the Workforce Innovation and Opportunity Act (WIOA) specifically target lower-wage workers and are worth exploring through your local American Job Center.

### Is there a limit on how many times I can apply?

Most states allow annual applications. Some (like Pennsylvania) have explicit limits (2 of 3 years). A few states have no limits. Check your state program's rules before assuming you can apply every year indefinitely.

## The bottom line

State workforce training grants represent some of the easiest-to-access business incentives available. The programs are designed to be employer-friendly — low paperwork, fast approval, minimal matching requirements — because states want employers to use them.

The main reason businesses miss out is simply not knowing the programs exist. A manufacturing company training 40 employees in California might be leaving $500,000+ in ETP reimbursements on the table every year. A Texas company partnering with a community college to train 100 new hires might qualify for a $240,000 SDF grant — with no employer match required.

Start by contacting your state's workforce development agency or your regional SBDC. They can tell you in one call whether you qualify, what the current funding availability looks like, and how to get started.
