## TL;DR

Veterans qualify for two separate categories of benefits in 2026: **VA-specific programs** (disability compensation, GI Bill, VA healthcare) and **mainstream programs** that have special rules or expanded eligibility for veterans (SNAP, Medicaid, Section 8, SSI, EITC, Pell Grant). Most veterans are leaving money on the table in at least one of these categories. This guide covers both.

## Two categories, one checklist

The biggest mistake veterans make is thinking "VA benefits" covers everything. The VA runs a parallel system — disability ratings, GI Bill, VA healthcare — that's separate from the mainstream federal benefit system. The two systems don't talk to each other well, and navigating between them is the main reason so many veterans leave money unclaimed.

Here's the short version of what you should check:

**Category 1: VA-specific programs**
- VA disability compensation
- GI Bill (education benefits)
- VA healthcare
- Veterans Pension
- Vocational Rehabilitation and Employment (VR&E)

**Category 2: Mainstream programs with veteran-friendly rules**
- SNAP (food benefits)
- Medicaid / VA healthcare coordination
- Section 8 / Housing Choice Voucher
- SSI (Supplemental Security Income)
- EITC (Earned Income Tax Credit)
- Pell Grant and other education aid

We'll cover both categories below.

## VA-specific programs

### VA disability compensation

If you have a service-connected disability — any physical or mental health condition caused or worsened by your military service — you may be entitled to monthly tax-free compensation.

**How ratings work:** The VA assigns a disability rating between 0% and 100% in 10-point increments based on the severity of your conditions. In 2026, monthly compensation rates range from $171.23 (10%) to $3,737.85 (100%), with additional amounts for dependents.

**Key things veterans miss:**
- Mental health conditions (PTSD, depression, anxiety) are compensable if service-connected
- Secondary conditions (conditions caused by a service-connected condition) can be added
- Many veterans are under-rated; you can file a supplemental claim or appeal
- Camp Lejeune water contamination, Agent Orange exposure, and toxic burn pit exposure now qualify under the PACT Act

**How to apply:** Through VA.gov or a VSO (Veterans Service Organization) like the DAV, VFW, or American Legion. VSO representation is free and significantly improves claim outcomes.

### GI Bill education benefits

The Post-9/11 GI Bill (Chapter 33) is the most valuable education benefit for veterans who served on active duty after September 11, 2001.

**What it covers:** 100% of tuition and fees at public in-state schools, up to $28,937.09/year at private schools, a monthly housing allowance (based on the E-5 with dependents BAH rate for the school's location), and a $1,000/year book stipend.

**Eligibility:** 90+ days of aggregate active duty service after 9/11 (or 30 days if discharged for service-connected disability). Full benefits at 36+ months of service.

**Transfer to dependents:** If you're still on active duty or in the Selected Reserve with 6+ years of service and commit to 4 more years, you can transfer unused entitlement to a spouse or child.

**Montgomery GI Bill (Chapter 30):** An older program for veterans who contributed $1,200 during service. Worth up to $2,199/month in 2026. Less valuable than Post-9/11 for most veterans, but stackable in some situations.

### VA healthcare

Veterans who served on active duty and separated under conditions other than dishonorable are generally eligible for VA healthcare. The scope of coverage depends on your priority group (1–8), which is based on disability rating, income, and other factors.

**Priority 1 (highest):** Veterans with 50%+ service-connected disability rating or receiving VA compensation for any service-connected disability that the VA determined as making the veteran unemployable. They receive care at no cost.

**Other priority groups:** Income-based for non-compensated veterans. The income thresholds are updated annually.

**What to know in 2026:** The PACT Act significantly expanded eligibility. Veterans with toxic exposure (burn pits, Agent Orange, Camp Lejeune water) who were previously ineligible may now qualify. If you applied before and were denied, apply again.

### Veterans Pension

A needs-based benefit for wartime veterans with low income and limited assets. Unlike disability compensation, it's not tied to a service-connected condition.

**Eligibility:** 90+ days active duty with at least one day during a wartime period; income below the Maximum Annual Pension Rate (MAPR); net worth under $155,356 in 2026.

**What it's worth:** The pension pays the difference between your income and the MAPR, which ranges from $16,551 (single veteran, no dependents) to $21,883 (veteran with one dependent) in 2026. Aid and Attendance enhancement adds up to $12,000+ annually for veterans needing assistance with daily activities.

## Mainstream programs with veteran-specific rules

These aren't VA programs — but veterans get expanded eligibility, priority treatment, or higher benefit amounts in several of them.

### SNAP (food benefits)

Veterans generally follow standard SNAP eligibility rules: income under 130% of the Federal Poverty Level and household size/assets within limits.

**Veteran-specific advantages:**
- VA disability compensation counts as income but disability payments do not affect the Standard Utility Allowance deduction
- Certain allowances and reimbursements are excluded from income calculations
- Veterans experiencing housing instability often qualify for expedited 7-day SNAP processing

See the full [SNAP eligibility guide for 2026](/blog/snap-eligibility-2026) for current income limits and how to apply.

### Medicaid

Veterans who don't meet VA priority groups for free care, or who prefer a community provider for primary care, may qualify for state Medicaid.

**Key point:** VA healthcare and Medicaid can coordinate coverage. You can use VA for some care and Medicaid for other services. This is especially common for dental care, which VA covers narrowly for most veterans but Medicaid covers more broadly in many states.

See the [Medicaid eligibility guide for 2026](/blog/medicaid-eligibility-2026) for state-specific income limits.

### Section 8 / Housing Choice Voucher

Low-income veterans may qualify for Section 8 in the same way as non-veterans, but there's an important parallel program: **HUD-VASH (HUD Veterans Affairs Supportive Housing).**

HUD-VASH is a joint VA/HUD program that provides Housing Choice Vouchers specifically for homeless veterans, combined with VA case management services. Unlike standard Section 8, HUD-VASH vouchers have no waitlist in many cities and can be activated faster.

**How to access HUD-VASH:** Contact your local VA Medical Center's homeless veteran programs office. Eligibility requires a level of homelessness (including fleeing domestic violence or staying in temporary housing) and VA healthcare eligibility.

See the full [Section 8 eligibility guide](/blog/section-8-eligibility-2026) for standard program details.

### SSI (Supplemental Security Income)

Veterans with service-connected disabilities that limit their ability to work may qualify for SSI, SSDI, or both, in addition to VA disability compensation.

**Important:** SSI and VA compensation can both be received, but SSI is reduced dollar-for-dollar by VA compensation income (with a $20 general income exclusion). In many cases, veterans with significant VA compensation don't benefit from SSI because the offset eliminates the SSI payment.

**Where SSI often helps:** Veterans with low or no VA disability rating who have non-service-connected disabilities may qualify for SSI without the offset issue. See the [SSI eligibility guide](/blog/ssi-eligibility-2026).

### Earned Income Tax Credit (EITC)

Veterans with earned income (wages, self-employment) and low-to-moderate income levels qualify for the EITC on the same basis as other workers. In 2026, the maximum credit is $7,830 for a family with three or more qualifying children, $6,960 for two children, $4,213 for one child, and $632 with no children.

**Veteran-specific note:** Nontaxable combat pay can be included in earned income for EITC calculation purposes — this is optional but often increases the credit for recently separated veterans.

See the full [EITC guide for 2026](/blog/eitc-eligibility-2026) for income limits and filing instructions.

### Pell Grant and federal student aid

Pell Grant eligibility for veterans follows the same rules as other students, based on expected family contribution (EFC) calculated from the FAFSA. In 2026, the maximum Pell Grant is $7,395.

**Key veteran consideration:** If you're using GI Bill benefits, those may affect your financial aid package — schools are required to count VA educational assistance in their financial aid calculations. The interaction varies by school.

**Post-9/11 GI Bill vs. Pell Grant:** You can receive both, but the GI Bill's housing allowance is not counted as income for Pell Grant purposes. For veterans at community colleges or lower-cost schools, combining both can significantly increase total support.

See the [Pell Grant eligibility guide](/blog/pell-grant-eligibility-2026) for full details.

## Frequently asked questions

### Do I have to prove my service to apply for civilian benefits like SNAP or Medicaid?

No. Veteran status is relevant to some eligibility rules but not required to apply for civilian programs. You'll use standard application processes through your state agency for SNAP, Medicaid, and Section 8.

### I was told VA benefits are "all I need." Is that true?

Rarely. VA healthcare has gaps (dental, vision, certain specialist care). VA disability compensation may be lower than expected. And civilian programs like SNAP, EITC, and Medicaid can add significant value alongside VA benefits without any conflict.

### Can I receive both VA disability compensation and SSDI?

Yes. They're separate programs from separate agencies and there's no offset between them. VA compensation is based on your disability rating; SSDI is based on your work history and the severity of disability as defined by SSA. Many veterans receive both.

### What's a VSO and should I use one?

A Veterans Service Organization (VSO) is a nonprofit that provides free help with VA claims. Organizations like the DAV, VFW, American Legion, and Disabled American Veterans have accredited claims agents who can prepare and submit claims on your behalf at no cost. Research consistently shows that claims filed with VSO representation have higher success rates than self-filed claims. Yes, use one.
