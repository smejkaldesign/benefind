## TL;DR

Medicaid and Medicare are both government health insurance programs, but they work completely differently and serve different populations. Medicaid is income-based and available at any age. Medicare is age-based (65+) and tied to your work history. Some people qualify for both — and if you're in that situation, coordinating them correctly is worth thousands of dollars a year.

> **Shortcut:** run the [Benefind eligibility screener](/screening) to find out which program you qualify for — it handles both Medicaid and Medicare, plus other programs you may be missing.

## The one-sentence version of each

**Medicaid:** Government health insurance for people with low income. Eligibility is based primarily on how much money you make and where you live.

**Medicare:** Government health insurance for people who are 65 or older, or who have certain disabilities. Eligibility is based on age and work history, not income.

If that's all you need, you're done. If you want to understand the details — income limits, what's covered, premiums, how dual eligibility works — keep reading.

## Side-by-side comparison

| | Medicaid | Medicare |
|---|---|---|
| **Who runs it** | State governments, with federal funding | Federal government (CMS) |
| **Primary eligibility trigger** | Income and household size | Age (65+) or disability |
| **Income test** | Yes — typically under 138% FPL in expansion states | No income test for Part A/B |
| **Premiums** | Usually $0 | Part B: $185/month in 2026; Part A: $0 if enough work history |
| **Deductibles and copays** | Usually very low or $0 | Part B deductible: $257/year; Part A hospital deductible: $1,676/spell |
| **Coverage** | Comprehensive, including dental/vision in many states | Hospital and medical; limited dental/vision in standard Medicare |
| **Prescription drugs** | Covered in most states | Part D (separate plan, optional) |
| **Long-term care** | Covered (major difference) | Not covered (major difference) |
| **When does it start** | When approved | Part A: age 65 (can enroll earlier if disabled); enrollment window: 7 months around 65th birthday |
| **How to apply** | State Medicaid agency or healthcare.gov | SSA.gov or your local Social Security office |

## Medicaid in 2026: who qualifies

Medicaid eligibility is set by each state, within federal guidelines. This makes it more complicated than Medicare — the rules genuinely differ depending on where you live.

### The income test

In the 40 states (plus D.C.) that have expanded Medicaid under the ACA, the income limit is **138% of the Federal Poverty Level.** In 2026, that's:

- $20,783 for a single person
- $28,208 for a couple
- $35,633 for a family of three
- $43,058 for a family of four

In the 10 states that have not expanded Medicaid (Alabama, Florida, Georgia, Kansas, Mississippi, South Carolina, Tennessee, Texas, Wisconsin, Wyoming), income limits are much lower — often under 50% FPL for working-age adults without children — and many low-income adults simply don't qualify.

**Pregnant women** qualify at higher income levels in most states (up to 200% FPL in many states, higher in some).

**Children** qualify through CHIP at income levels up to 300% FPL in most states.

### What Medicaid covers

Medicaid must cover a core set of services in every state: doctor visits, hospital care, lab tests, X-rays, nursing facility services, and early childhood services. States can — and most do — cover additional services including:

- **Dental care** (adult dental varies by state; children's dental is mandatory)
- **Vision and eye care**
- **Prescription drugs** (covered in all states with varying formularies)
- **Mental health and substance use treatment**
- **Long-term care** (nursing homes, home health aides) — this is the major coverage Medicaid has that Medicare lacks

See the full [Medicaid eligibility guide for 2026](/blog/medicaid-eligibility-2026) for state-specific income limits and coverage details.

## Medicare in 2026: who qualifies

Medicare has four parts. Understanding which part does what is essential to understanding your coverage.

### Part A: Hospital insurance

Covers inpatient hospital care, skilled nursing facility (SNF) stays following a qualifying hospital admission, home health care, and hospice.

**Eligibility:** You or your spouse paid Medicare taxes for at least 10 years (40 quarters). If you meet this work history requirement, Part A is free. If you don't, you can buy in — the premium is up to $518/month in 2026.

**When it kicks in:** You can enroll at 65. If you're already receiving Social Security benefits when you turn 65, you're automatically enrolled. If not, you must actively enroll through SSA.gov.

### Part B: Medical insurance

Covers outpatient services: doctor visits, preventive care, outpatient surgery, durable medical equipment, and some home health services.

**Premium:** $185/month per person in 2026 for most beneficiaries. Higher-income individuals pay more through Income-Related Monthly Adjustment Amounts (IRMAA).

**Deductible:** $257/year before Medicare starts paying. After that, you pay 20% of Medicare-approved amounts for most services.

### Part C: Medicare Advantage (private alternative)

Private insurance plans that replace traditional Medicare Parts A and B. Often include Part D (prescription) coverage and may add dental, vision, and hearing benefits. Plans vary significantly — comparing them annually is important because networks, formularies, and premiums change.

### Part D: Prescription drug coverage

Separate plans that cover prescription drugs. In 2026, the out-of-pocket maximum for Part D is capped at $2,000 annually under the IRA changes — a significant improvement from prior years.

## The long-term care gap: the most important difference

This is the one thing most people don't know until it's too late.

**Medicare does not cover long-term custodial care.** It covers skilled nursing facility care for up to 100 days after a qualifying hospital stay — but only while you're making measurable medical progress. Once you need ongoing help with activities of daily living (bathing, dressing, eating) without a skilled care component, Medicare stops paying.

**Medicaid does cover long-term care.** It's the primary payer for nursing home costs in the U.S. — paying for approximately 62% of nursing home residents. But to qualify for Medicaid's long-term care coverage, you must spend down your assets to state-defined limits (typically $2,000 in countable assets for an individual).

This is why long-term care planning is primarily a Medicaid question, not a Medicare question.

## Dual eligibility: can you have both?

Yes. People who qualify for both Medicaid and Medicare are called "dual eligible" beneficiaries. Roughly 12 million Americans are dual eligible in 2026.

**Who qualifies:** You must qualify for Medicare based on age or disability AND qualify for Medicaid based on income. The most common situation is elderly individuals (65+) with low income who qualify for Medicare but also meet their state's Medicaid income limits.

**Why dual eligibility matters:** When you have both programs, they coordinate coverage. Medicare pays first as the primary payer. Medicaid pays second, covering some or all of what Medicare doesn't pay — premiums, deductibles, and cost-sharing.

For most dual eligible individuals, **Medicare premiums are paid by Medicaid,** eliminating the $185/month Part B cost. Deductibles and copays may also be covered, depending on the specific Medicaid program.

### Medicare Savings Programs (MSPs)

Even if you don't qualify for full Medicaid, you may qualify for a Medicare Savings Program — a Medicaid program specifically designed to help Medicare beneficiaries with the cost-sharing burdens of Medicare.

There are four MSP levels:

| Program | Income Limit (2026, individual) | What It Pays |
|---|---|---|
| Qualified Medicare Beneficiary (QMB) | ~100% FPL (~$1,304/month) | Part A and B premiums, deductibles, and cost-sharing |
| Specified Low-Income Medicare Beneficiary (SLMB) | ~120% FPL (~$1,565/month) | Part B premium only |
| Qualifying Individual (QI) | ~135% FPL (~$1,760/month) | Part B premium only |
| Qualified Disabled and Working Individual (QDWI) | ~200% FPL | Part A premium only |

If you're on Medicare and earning under 135% of the FPL, you should apply for an MSP. The Part B premium savings alone ($185/month) are worth over $2,200 per year.

**How to apply for an MSP:** Contact your state Medicaid agency. You can also apply through the Extra Help/LIS program for Part D cost assistance.

## Which one should you apply for?

This depends on your situation. Here's a quick decision tree:

- **Under 65, low income, not disabled?** Apply for Medicaid.
- **Under 65, disabled?** Check SSDI first. After 24 months of SSDI, you qualify for Medicare regardless of age. Also check Medicaid while waiting.
- **65 or older, enough work history?** Enroll in Medicare Part A and B. Then check if your income qualifies for Medicaid or an MSP.
- **65 or older, low income?** You likely qualify for both. Apply for Medicaid through your state — they'll automatically screen you for Medicare Savings Programs.

## Frequently asked questions

### I thought Medicare was free. Why do I have to pay premiums?

Part A is free if you or your spouse have enough work history (40 quarters of Medicare-covered employment). Part B is not free — the standard premium is $185/month in 2026. If your income is low enough, a Medicare Savings Program can pay that premium for you.

### Can I have Medicaid and Medicare Advantage at the same time?

Yes. There are also plans specifically designed for dual-eligible individuals called Dual Eligible Special Needs Plans (D-SNPs). These plans coordinate Medicare and Medicaid benefits under one plan and can simplify care significantly.

### Does getting Medicaid affect my ability to enroll in Medicare?

No. Medicaid and Medicare enrollment are independent processes. Getting Medicaid doesn't affect your Medicare eligibility or enrollment timeline.

### My state didn't expand Medicaid and I don't qualify. What are my options?

If your income is above your state's Medicaid limit but below 100% of the FPL, you fall into the "coverage gap" — you don't qualify for Medicaid, and you also don't qualify for ACA marketplace subsidies (which start at 100% FPL). Some states have partial programs that may help. Community health centers provide sliding-scale care regardless of insurance. The [Benefind screener](/screening) will surface the best available options for your specific state.

### How do I apply for Medicaid vs Medicare?

**Medicaid:** Apply through your state Medicaid agency or at healthcare.gov. Processing takes up to 45 days.

**Medicare:** Apply at SSA.gov or your local Social Security office. Enroll during your Initial Enrollment Period (the 7-month window around your 65th birthday). Late enrollment penalties apply if you miss the window and don't have qualifying coverage.

## The bottom line

Medicaid and Medicare are different programs that serve different needs — and millions of people qualify for both. If you're 65 or older with low income, you should be enrolled in Medicare AND applying for Medicaid or a Medicare Savings Program. If you're under 65 with low income, Medicaid is likely your path to coverage.
