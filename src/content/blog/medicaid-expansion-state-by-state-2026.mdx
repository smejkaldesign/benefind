## TL;DR

Medicaid expansion is the single biggest driver of who gets free or nearly-free health insurance in the United States. As of 2026, **41 states plus DC have adopted expansion**, and 9 states have not. If you live in an expansion state and earn up to **138% of the federal poverty level** (about $21,597/year for a single adult or $36,777 for a family of three), you almost certainly qualify. If you live in a non-expansion state, there's a brutal "coverage gap" that leaves millions stranded between Medicaid and Marketplace subsidies. This guide walks through every state, the exact income rules, what expansion actually changed, and how to apply no matter where you live.

## What Medicaid expansion actually is

The Affordable Care Act (2010) gave states the option to expand Medicaid to cover nearly all adults under 65 with incomes up to **138% of the federal poverty level (FPL)**. The federal government pays **90% of the cost** for everyone the expansion covers, a much higher federal match than regular Medicaid.

Before expansion, Medicaid was mostly limited to very low-income children, pregnant women, people with disabilities, and seniors in long-term care. Working-age adults without kids were usually locked out, no matter how poor they were.

Expansion changed that. In states that adopted it, any adult earning up to $21,597/year (single) qualifies, period. No work requirement, no asset test in most states, no "are you a parent?" question.

In states that didn't, the old rules still apply. This is the source of the "coverage gap", the infamous situation where a single adult in Mississippi earning $12,000/year is too poor for Marketplace subsidies but too "rich" for traditional Medicaid.

## The 138% FPL income limit, in dollars

Medicaid expansion uses **MAGI** (Modified Adjusted Gross Income) to determine eligibility, which is your AGI from your tax return plus a few add-backs. For most people, MAGI is roughly the same as your gross income.

The 2026 income limits for expansion Medicaid:

| Household size | 100% FPL (annual) | 138% FPL (annual) | 138% FPL (monthly) |
| -------------- | ----------------- | ----------------- | ------------------ |
| 1              | $15,650           | $21,597           | $1,800             |
| 2              | $21,150           | $29,187           | $2,432             |
| 3              | $26,650           | $36,777           | $3,065             |
| 4              | $32,150           | $44,367           | $3,697             |
| 5              | $37,650           | $51,957           | $4,330             |

Add roughly $7,590 per extra person. Alaska and Hawaii have higher numbers (state-specific FPL calculations).

**Important:** the 138% cap in the law is really 133% plus a 5% "income disregard," so you'll sometimes see the number written as either one. The effective cap is 138% and that's the number you should use.

## The 41 expansion states (and DC)

As of 2026, these states have adopted Medicaid expansion:

Alaska, Arizona, Arkansas, California, Colorado, Connecticut, Delaware, District of Columbia, Hawaii, Idaho, Illinois, Indiana, Iowa, Kentucky, Louisiana, Maine, Maryland, Massachusetts, Michigan, Minnesota, Missouri, Montana, Nebraska, Nevada, New Hampshire, New Jersey, New Mexico, New York, North Carolina, North Dakota, Ohio, Oklahoma, Oregon, Pennsylvania, Rhode Island, South Dakota, Utah, Vermont, Virginia, Washington, West Virginia, Wisconsin.

(Wisconsin is a partial-expansion state, it covers adults up to 100% FPL, not 138%, via an 1115 waiver. Functionally similar for most people, but the income cap is lower.)

If you live in any of these states and your income is below 138% FPL, **you qualify for Medicaid**. Apply through your state agency or healthcare.gov.

## The 9 non-expansion states

These states have not adopted expansion:

Alabama, Florida, Georgia, Kansas, Mississippi, South Carolina, Tennessee, Texas, Wyoming.

In these states, adults under 65 without dependent children are **usually not eligible for Medicaid at any income level**. Parents qualify, but only at very low incomes, often under 50% FPL. Pregnant women and children have their own rules (and are covered much more generously).

## The coverage gap, the part nobody tells you

The coverage gap is the biggest quiet injustice in US health policy. Here's how it works:

1. Before the ACA, Marketplace subsidies (what most people mean when they say "Obamacare") were designed to start at **100% FPL**. The ACA assumed anyone below 100% would be covered by Medicaid expansion.
2. The Supreme Court's 2012 NFIB v. Sebelius decision made expansion optional for states.
3. In non-expansion states, adults earning between 0% and 100% FPL fall into a gap, too "rich" for the old Medicaid rules, too poor for Marketplace subsidies.

If you're in the gap, you can technically buy a Marketplace plan, but you'll pay full price (hundreds of dollars a month) for a plan you probably can't afford to use.

**The workaround:** if you earn between 100% and 138% FPL in a non-expansion state, you qualify for the most generous Marketplace subsidies available. A $0 silver plan is often possible. If your income is below 100% FPL, the only real paths are:

- Find part-time work that bumps you above 100% FPL
- Apply for disability (SSI/SSDI) if you qualify
- Move to an expansion state (this sounds flippant but it is, legitimately, something people do)
- Community health centers and charity care programs

## How to apply, step by step

### 1. Figure out your state's rules

Start at [healthcare.gov](https://healthcare.gov) and enter your ZIP. The site automatically routes you to either your state's Medicaid agency or the federal Marketplace, depending on where you live.

Some states run their own Marketplaces: California (Covered California), New York (NY State of Health), Washington (Washington Healthplanfinder), Massachusetts (MAhealthconnector), Colorado (Connect for Health), and about a dozen others. Google "[your state] health insurance marketplace."

### 2. Gather documents

- Social Security numbers for everyone applying
- Proof of income: recent pay stubs, W-2, or tax return
- Proof of citizenship or immigration status (green card, passport, naturalization certificate)
- Housing info: address, household composition
- Current health insurance info (if any)

### 3. Apply online, by phone, or in person

Most states let you apply online in about 30 minutes. You can also call 1-800-318-2596 (federal Marketplace) or visit an in-person assister for free help.

### 4. Wait for the decision

Medicaid decisions usually come within **45 days** (or 90 days if they need to verify disability). Many states decide within 2 weeks. You'll get a letter by mail or email.

### 5. If approved, pick a plan

Most Medicaid programs are run through **managed care organizations (MCOs)**, think Anthem, UnitedHealthcare, Molina, etc. You'll usually have a choice of 3-5 plans. Pick one that includes your doctors and pharmacy.

## What Medicaid actually covers

Medicaid is the **most comprehensive coverage** available to most Americans. Required benefits under federal law:

- Doctor visits
- Hospital stays
- Emergency room
- Lab tests, X-rays, imaging
- Prescription drugs
- Mental health and substance use treatment
- Preventive care (vaccines, screenings, physicals)
- Family planning
- Maternity and newborn care
- Children's dental and vision

Most states also cover adult dental, vision, and hearing, but this varies. Co-pays, if any, are tiny, usually $0-$4 per visit.

## States that expanded recently (2024-2025)

Expansion is still happening. Recent adopters include:

- **North Carolina** (December 2023), added 600,000+ people to Medicaid rolls in the first year
- **South Dakota** (July 2023), voter-approved ballot initiative
- **Missouri** (October 2021), voter initiative, took a court fight to implement
- **Oklahoma** (July 2021), voter initiative

The pattern is clear: when expansion goes on the ballot, it passes. Ballot initiatives have been the main path in deep-red states.

## What to do if you don't qualify

If you don't qualify for Medicaid, you have options:

- **Marketplace with subsidies**, if your income is above 138% FPL (or above 100% in a non-expansion state), check healthcare.gov for subsidized plans. Enhanced subsidies from the American Rescue Plan / Inflation Reduction Act are in effect through 2025, with legislation pending for extension.
- **Employer insurance**, if available
- **Community health centers**, Federally Qualified Health Centers (FQHCs) offer sliding-scale fees based on income. Find one at [findahealthcenter.hrsa.gov](https://findahealthcenter.hrsa.gov)
- **Medicare**, if you're 65+ or receive SSDI
- **Children's Health Insurance Program (CHIP)**, covers kids in families earning too much for Medicaid but not enough for Marketplace, usually up to 200-400% FPL

## Frequently asked questions

### What happens if my income changes mid-year?

Report it. If you drop below 138% FPL mid-year, you can apply for Medicaid at any time (no open enrollment required). If you rise above the limit, you lose Medicaid but qualify for a special enrollment period on the Marketplace.

### Can undocumented immigrants get Medicaid expansion?

Generally no, federal Medicaid is limited to US citizens and qualified non-citizens (green card holders, refugees, etc.). Some states (California, Washington, Oregon, Illinois, New York, Minnesota) use state-only funds to cover undocumented adults and kids. Check your state's rules.

### Is there a work requirement for Medicaid expansion?

Not under current federal law in most states. Arkansas, Georgia, and a few others have tried to impose work requirements via 1115 waivers, but these have been struck down or paused in court. As of 2026, no state is actively enforcing an expansion work requirement.

### How long does coverage last?

Medicaid is continuous as long as you remain eligible. After the COVID-era continuous enrollment ended in 2023, states began "redetermining" eligibility annually. You'll get a renewal packet once a year, fill it out and return it on time or you'll lose coverage even if you still qualify.

### Can I keep my doctor on Medicaid?

Sometimes. Most states run Medicaid through managed care plans, and each plan has its own network. Check whether your doctor is in-network before picking a plan. If your doctor is out of network, you may still see them, but you'll pay out of pocket.

### What if I'm dual-eligible for Medicare and Medicaid?

You get both. Medicare covers hospital and doctor visits; Medicaid covers what Medicare doesn't (long-term care, dental, vision, prescription cost-sharing) and pays your Medicare premiums. This is the most generous coverage combination in the US system, about 12 million Americans are dual-eligible.

## The bottom line

If you live in an expansion state and your household earns less than 138% FPL, you qualify for Medicaid. If you live in one of the 9 non-expansion states and you're between 100% and 138% FPL, you'll likely get a $0 Marketplace plan instead. If you're below 100% FPL in a non-expansion state, you're in the coverage gap and your only realistic paths are Marketplace subsidies via earning slightly more, community health centers, or moving.
