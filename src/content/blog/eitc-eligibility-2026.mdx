## TL;DR

The Earned Income Tax Credit (EITC) is a refundable federal tax credit worth up to **$8,046 in 2026** for working families with three or more children. You don't need to owe taxes to get it — if the credit exceeds what you owe, you get the rest as a refund. About **1 in 5 eligible workers don't claim it**, leaving $4–7 billion unclaimed every year. Single workers without children can also qualify, up to $649.

## What the EITC is (and why it matters)

The Earned Income Tax Credit is a refundable tax credit in the U.S. tax code designed to support working people with low to moderate incomes. "Refundable" means that if the credit is larger than your tax bill, the IRS pays you the difference as a refund check or direct deposit.

The EITC is the **single largest federal anti-poverty program for working families**, lifting approximately 5.6 million people above the poverty line each year. It specifically rewards work: you must have earned income (wages, tips, self-employment) to qualify. Passive income, Social Security, and most government benefits don't count.

The IRS estimates that roughly **1 in 5 eligible taxpayers don't claim the EITC** each year. That's a lot of money left on the table — often by the people who need it most. The most common reasons people miss it: not knowing it exists, thinking they don't earn enough to file taxes, or assuming it's only for parents.

## Credit amounts for tax year 2025 (filed in 2026)

The EITC amount depends on two things: your income and how many qualifying children you have. Here are the maximum credit amounts and income limits for the 2025 tax year, which you file in early 2026.

| Filing Status | 0 Children | 1 Child | 2 Children | 3+ Children |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Single / Head of Household / Widower | Up to $649 | Up to $4,328 | Up to $7,152 | Up to $8,046 |
| Married Filing Jointly | Up to $649 | Up to $4,328 | Up to $7,152 | Up to $8,046 |

The maximum credit amounts are the same regardless of filing status. What differs is the income limit at which the credit phases out.

### Income limits by filing status

| Filing Status | 0 Children | 1 Child | 2 Children | 3+ Children |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Single / HoH / Widower: Max AGI | $19,104 | $49,084 | $55,768 | $59,899 |
| Married Filing Jointly: Max AGI | $26,214 | $56,194 | $62,878 | $67,009 |

Note: investment income must be **$11,600 or less** for the entire year or you're disqualified from EITC, regardless of earned income.

## Who qualifies

### Basic requirements for all filers

- **You must have earned income.** Wages, salaries, tips, self-employment income, and union strike benefits all count. Social Security, unemployment, alimony, child support, and investment returns do not.
- **You must file a federal tax return** (Form 1040), even if you don't owe anything and aren't otherwise required to file.
- **You need a valid Social Security number** for yourself, your spouse (if filing jointly), and any qualifying children.
- **You cannot file as Married Filing Separately.** Married couples must file jointly to claim EITC.
- **Investment income must be $11,600 or less** for the full year.
- You must be a U.S. citizen or resident alien for the entire tax year.

### For workers without children

If you have no qualifying children, you must also be between **ages 25 and 64** at the end of the tax year. The credit tops out at $649, but it's still money you're owed.

### Qualifying child rules

A "qualifying child" must meet all three of these tests:

1. **Relationship:** Son, daughter, stepchild, foster child, sibling, or a descendant of any of these (grandchild, niece, nephew, etc.)
2. **Age:** Under 19 at the end of the year (under 24 if a full-time student; any age if permanently disabled)
3. **Residency:** Lived with you in the U.S. for more than half the year

The child must also have a valid Social Security number and cannot file a joint return themselves (except to claim a refund).

## How to claim the EITC — step by step

### Step 1: File a federal tax return

File Form 1040, even if you don't owe taxes. Don't skip this — you cannot get the EITC without filing. If you have qualifying children, you'll also complete Schedule EIC.

### Step 2: Use a free filing option

Paid tax preparers who charge a percentage of your refund can eat up a significant chunk of your EITC. There are several fully free options:

- **IRS Free File** (IRS.gov): Free software for filers with income under $84,000
- **IRS Direct File**: The IRS's own free filing tool, now available in more states for the 2026 filing season
- **VITA (Volunteer Income Tax Assistance)**: Free in-person tax help for people earning $67,000 or less, people with disabilities, and limited English speakers
- **TCE (Tax Counseling for the Elderly)**: Free tax prep for people age 60 and older

### Step 3: Provide the required documents

Gather:
- W-2s from all employers
- 1099 forms for any other income
- Records of self-employment income and expenses (if self-employed)
- Social Security numbers for yourself, spouse, and any qualifying children
- Proof of qualifying children's residency if needed (birth certificates, school records, medical records)
- Bank account and routing number for direct deposit

### Step 4: Watch for your refund

If you claim the EITC, the IRS is required by law (the PATH Act) to hold your refund until mid-February. Most EITC refunds arrive in early March if you filed electronically with direct deposit.

### Step 5: Check prior years

You can amend returns for up to **3 prior tax years** to claim EITC you missed. If you didn't know about the EITC in 2022, 2023, or 2024, you may be able to file amended returns and collect money owed to you.

## Self-employed workers and the EITC

Yes, self-employment income counts as earned income. If you do gig work, freelance, or run a small business, you can qualify for the EITC. The catch: you must report your net self-employment income (after business expenses) on Schedule SE and pay self-employment taxes. Once you do, that income counts toward the EITC.

Keep detailed records of business income and expenses. If your business had a loss, it reduces your earned income and could affect your EITC amount.

## State EITCs — extra money on top of the federal credit

31 states, Washington D.C., Guam, and Puerto Rico have their own state-level EITC programs. State EITCs are typically calculated as a percentage of the federal credit and are usually refundable. If you live in one of these states, you claim it on your state tax return.

Alabama, Alaska, Arizona, Arkansas, Florida, Georgia, Idaho, Iowa, Kentucky, Mississippi, Missouri, Nevada, New Hampshire, North Carolina, North Dakota, Pennsylvania, South Dakota, Tennessee, Texas, Utah, West Virginia, and Wyoming do not have state EITC programs.

## EITC for workers without children

The EITC for childless workers has historically been small — just $649 maximum for 2025 — and many people don't know it exists. If you're a single worker between ages 25 and 64 who earns under $19,104 a year, you qualify. It's not a huge amount, but it's yours, and it takes about 15 minutes to claim when you file your taxes.

Advocates have long pushed to expand the childless EITC, arguing it does little to help young low-wage workers or workers over 65. No major expansion was enacted for 2025.

## Common EITC mistakes to avoid

1. **Not filing because you don't owe taxes.** Filing is required to claim the EITC. No return, no credit.
2. **Filing as Married Filing Separately.** This immediately disqualifies you. Married couples must file jointly.
3. **Claiming a child who doesn't pass the residency test.** Your child must live with you for more than 6 months in the year.
4. **Not realizing self-employment counts.** Gig income, freelance, and small business income all count as earned income.
5. **Investment income over $11,600.** Even one dollar over this limit voids the entire EITC for that year.
6. **Paying a preparer a percentage of your refund.** Use VITA or IRS Free File instead.
7. **Forgetting to claim prior years.** You have 3 years to file an amended return for missed EITC.

## Frequently asked questions

### What is the maximum EITC for 2026?

The maximum EITC for tax year 2025 (filed in 2026) is **$8,046**, available to families with three or more qualifying children. The credit scales down to $7,152 for two children, $4,328 for one child, and $649 for workers without children.

### Do I have to owe taxes to get the EITC?

No. The EITC is fully refundable. If the credit exceeds your tax liability, you receive the difference as a cash refund. You do need to file a tax return to claim it, even if you owe zero.

### Can I get the EITC if I'm self-employed?

Yes. Self-employment income from gig work, freelancing, or running a small business counts as earned income. You'll report it on Schedule C and pay self-employment tax, but the net income counts toward EITC eligibility.

### When will I get my EITC refund?

By law, the IRS cannot issue EITC refunds before mid-February. If you file electronically with direct deposit early in the tax season, most EITC refunds arrive in early to mid-March.

### What if I missed the EITC in previous years?

You can file amended returns (Form 1040-X) for the three most recent tax years to claim EITC you were entitled to but didn't claim. For example, in 2026 you could still amend returns for tax years 2023, 2024, and 2025 (once filed).

### Can I get both the federal EITC and a state EITC?

Yes, if you live in one of the 31 states with a state EITC program. You claim the federal EITC on your federal return and the state EITC on your state return. Many state EITCs are fully refundable and can add hundreds or thousands of dollars on top of the federal credit.

### Does EITC affect other benefits like Medicaid or SNAP?

EITC refunds are **not counted as income** for Medicaid, SNAP, SSI, housing assistance, or most other federal benefit programs. The refund is also excluded from asset counts for 12 months after you receive it for most programs.

### Is there an age limit for the EITC?

For workers claiming without qualifying children, you must be between 25 and 64 at the end of the tax year. If you have a qualifying child, there is no age requirement. A parent at any age can claim the EITC if they have an eligible child.
