## TL;DR

Section 8 (officially the Housing Choice Voucher Program) helps low-income renters afford private-market housing by covering the gap between 30% of your income and the local fair market rent. To qualify, your household income must be at or below **50% of your area's median income (AMI)** — but 75% of vouchers go to households at or below 30% AMI. Waitlists run 2–5 years in most cities. About 2,200 local housing authorities run the program independently, so rules and wait times vary by location.

## What Section 8 is (and what it isn't)

The Housing Choice Voucher (HCV) Program — commonly called "Section 8" after the section of the Housing Act that created it — is a federal rental assistance program run by the U.S. Department of Housing and Urban Development (HUD) and administered locally by roughly **2,200 Public Housing Authorities (PHAs)**.

When you receive a voucher, you find your own rental unit in the private market. The PHA pays most of your rent directly to your landlord; you cover the rest (typically 30% of your adjusted monthly income). You keep the voucher even if you move — you're not tied to a specific apartment or building.

Section 8 is **not** public housing. Public housing is government-owned apartment complexes where you live in a unit managed by the PHA. With a Section 8 voucher, you rent from private landlords.

About **5 million households** receive Section 8 vouchers in the U.S., representing roughly 11 million people.

## Who qualifies — the income rules

Eligibility is based on your household income compared to the **Area Median Income (AMI)** for your county or metropolitan area. HUD publishes AMI figures for every county and metro area in the country each year.

### Primary income limit: 50% of AMI

Your household income must be at or below **50% of your local AMI** to qualify. Because AMI varies dramatically by location, so does the dollar threshold for eligibility.

**50% AMI income limits for a family of four — selected markets (2025):**

| Metro area | 50% AMI (family of 4) |
| ---------- | --------------------- |
| San Francisco, CA | ~$78,550 |
| Washington, DC | ~$68,200 |
| New York City, NY | ~$55,250 |
| Chicago, IL | ~$52,450 |
| Atlanta, GA | ~$47,600 |
| Houston, TX | ~$44,400 |
| Phoenix, AZ | ~$40,300 |
| Rural Mississippi | ~$27,750 |

To find your specific area's limits, go to hud.gov and search for the HUD Income Limits tool or ask your local PHA.

### The 30% AMI priority rule

By law, PHAs must admit **at least 75% of new voucher holders from households at or below 30% of AMI** (or the federal poverty level, whichever is higher). In practice, this means the people who actually receive vouchers tend to have extremely low incomes — often $20,000/year or below for a family of four in average-cost areas.

**30% AMI income limits for a family of four — selected markets (2025):**

| Metro area | 30% AMI (family of 4) |
| ---------- | --------------------- |
| San Francisco, CA | ~$47,150 |
| New York City, NY | ~$33,150 |
| Houston, TX | ~$26,650 |
| Rural Mississippi | ~$16,650 |

### Household composition

Any household can apply: families with children, single individuals, elderly adults (62+), people with disabilities, veterans, and non-traditional households. You don't need to be a family with children to qualify.

PHAs often give **preferences** to specific groups, which can move you up the waitlist faster:

- Families with children
- Elderly households (age 62+)
- People with disabilities
- Veterans and active military families
- Victims of domestic violence or natural disasters
- People experiencing homelessness
- Working families (some PHAs have "working family" preferences)

### Citizenship and immigration requirements

At least one household member must be a U.S. citizen or eligible non-citizen (lawful permanent resident, refugee, asylee, etc.). Mixed-status families can receive **prorated assistance** — the subsidy is calculated based on the number of eligible household members, so families aren't disqualified just because some members don't have eligible status.

### Background and rental history screening

PHAs screen applicants for:

- Criminal background (drug-related or violent criminal history can result in denial; lifetime sex offender registrants are permanently barred)
- Eviction history
- Outstanding debts to any PHA

Rules vary by PHA. Some are more restrictive; some have adopted "second chance" policies. If you're concerned about your history, ask the specific PHA about their screening criteria before applying.

## How the waitlist works

This is the hardest part of Section 8 to understand from the outside: the program almost always has far more qualified applicants than available vouchers. **The waitlist is the reality of Section 8** for most people.

### Average wait times

- **National average:** 2–5 years
- **High-demand cities** (New York, San Francisco, Los Angeles, Washington DC): 8–10+ years; some waitlists are effectively closed
- **Lower-cost rural areas:** Sometimes 1–2 years
- **Many PHAs have waitlists that are permanently closed** — they stopped accepting new applications years ago and have no plan to reopen

### How PHAs open waitlists

When a PHA has capacity to accept new applications, they announce the open waitlist period publicly — local newspapers, their website, social service agencies. In high-demand cities, these openings attract thousands of applications in a matter of days. Some PHAs use a lottery system to randomly select from all applicants who apply during the open window.

### Staying on the waitlist

This is critical: once you're on a waitlist, you **must respond to all PHA communications**. PHAs periodically send notices to verify you still want a voucher and still live at the same address. If you don't respond — even once — you can be removed from the list and lose your place. Keep your contact information updated.

### Preferences can move you up

If a PHA offers preferences for veterans, domestic violence survivors, or people experiencing homelessness, qualifying for a preference can significantly shorten your wait. Ask about preferences when you apply.

## How the voucher amount is calculated

When you receive a voucher, it's worth a specific dollar amount based on the PHA's **payment standard** — typically 90–110% of HUD's Fair Market Rent (FMR) for the area.

### The math

**Voucher subsidy = Payment standard minus 30% of your adjusted monthly income**

**Example:**
- Family of four with monthly income of $2,000
- PHA payment standard for a 2-bedroom: $1,800/month
- Your share: 30% × $2,000 = **$600/month**
- Voucher pays: $1,800 − $600 = **$1,200/month**

If you choose an apartment that costs $2,000/month (above the payment standard), you pay $600 + $200 = $800/month. Your payment can go up to 40% of income at initial lease-up if you choose a more expensive unit.

### Income does not eliminate your voucher

As your income rises, your tenant share increases, but the voucher doesn't disappear until your income exceeds the eligibility threshold. Working is encouraged — there's no cliff where getting a raise suddenly takes away all your assistance.

### Bedroom sizes and payment standards

Payment standards are set by bedroom size. You won't be given a 3-bedroom voucher if you're a single person — PHAs determine the appropriate unit size based on household composition (typically 2 people per bedroom, with some flexibility).

### Small Area Fair Market Rents

Some PHAs use **Small Area FMRs** set at the ZIP code level rather than the metro-wide average. This allows higher voucher amounts in expensive neighborhoods, giving voucher holders more ability to rent in better school districts and lower-poverty areas.

## How to apply for Section 8 — step by step

### Step 1: Find your local PHA

There is no national Section 8 application. You apply directly to your local PHA (or multiple PHAs — you can be on more than one waitlist at a time).

Find PHAs at hud.gov/program_offices/public_indian_housing/pha/contacts or call HUD at 1-800-955-2232.

### Step 2: Check if the waitlist is open

Most PHAs have closed waitlists. Before applying anywhere, confirm the waitlist is currently accepting applications. PHAs announce openings on their websites and through local media.

### Step 3: Submit your application

Applications are typically free. You can often apply online; some PHAs still use paper applications. During high-demand openings, applications may only be accepted for a few days.

### Step 4: Gather documents

You'll need for all household members:
- Photo ID
- Social Security card
- Birth certificate
- Proof of income (pay stubs, benefit award letters, tax returns)
- Bank statements
- Proof of citizenship or immigration status
- Documentation of veteran status or disability if claiming a preference
- Current lease or landlord contact information

### Step 5: Respond to all PHA communications

Once on the waitlist, check your mail (and email, if provided) regularly. Update your address any time you move. Respond to every verification notice.

### Step 6: Receive your voucher

When your name comes up, the PHA will schedule a briefing to explain the program. You'll receive a voucher and typically have **60–120 days** to find a qualifying unit. Extensions are often available.

### Step 7: Find housing and pass inspection

Your landlord must agree to participate in the program. The unit must pass HUD's Housing Quality Standards (HQS) inspection — basic habitability and safety requirements. The PHA then signs a Housing Assistance Payment (HAP) contract with the landlord.

## Section 8 vouchers vs. public housing

These are two different programs that often get confused:

| Feature | Section 8 Voucher | Public Housing |
| ------- | ----------------- | -------------- |
| Unit ownership | Private landlord | Government (PHA) |
| Where you live | Any qualifying private unit | PHA-owned development |
| Portability | Yes — can move with voucher | No — tied to specific complex |
| Choice | Renter chooses unit | Limited to available units |
| Wait times | Long in most areas | Also long; separate waitlist |
| Income limit | Up to 50% AMI | Up to 80% AMI (usually 30% in practice) |
| Rent you pay | ~30% of income | ~30% of income |

Some households prefer the stability of public housing; others prefer the flexibility of vouchers. You can apply for both simultaneously through the same PHA in many cases.

## Tips for getting off the waitlist faster

**Apply to multiple PHAs.** You can be on as many waitlists as you want. PHAs in suburban or rural areas often have shorter waits than major city PHAs.

**Qualify for preferences.** Veteran, disabled, elderly, domestic violence survivor, or homeless preferences can significantly move you up. Make sure you document and claim any preference you qualify for when applying.

**Stay current on PHA communications.** People who don't respond to verification notices lose their place. This creates movement on the list — which helps those who do respond.

**Check for Emergency Housing Vouchers.** Some PHAs have emergency or special-purpose vouchers (HUD-VASH for homeless veterans, Emergency Housing Vouchers for domestic violence survivors, etc.) with separate, shorter waitlists.

**Consider nearby markets.** If you have flexibility on location, smaller cities and rural areas often have both shorter waits and lower Fair Market Rents, meaning your voucher goes further.

**Once you get a voucher, act fast.** Most vouchers have a 60–120 day search period. Start looking immediately. In high-cost markets, it can be hard to find landlords who accept vouchers — check with local tenant advocacy organizations for lists of participating landlords.

## Common mistakes to avoid

**"Section 8 means public housing."** Vouchers are for private-market rentals. You choose the unit; the PHA subsidizes the rent.

**"I'll get a voucher quickly if I apply."** In most cities, the wait is measured in years, not weeks or months. Apply as soon as possible, even if you don't need it yet.

**"The government pays all my rent."** You pay approximately 30% of your adjusted income, regardless of what the rent is. More expensive unit = higher out-of-pocket cost.

**"My voucher only works in the city where I applied."** Vouchers are portable. After your initial lease period (typically 12 months), you can move to another city or state. The new PHA either absorbs your voucher or bills the original PHA.

**"I can't work or I'll lose my voucher."** Working increases your tenant share of rent but doesn't eliminate your subsidy. Some PHAs have special incentives for working families.

**"All landlords have to accept Section 8."** Landlord acceptance is required only in jurisdictions with Source of Income (SOI) discrimination protections. About 20 states plus many cities have these protections; most states do not. Landlords can legally refuse Section 8 vouchers in states without SOI laws.

## Frequently asked questions

### What is the Section 8 income limit for 2026?

Income limits are set at 50% of the Area Median Income (AMI) for your specific county or metro area. Because AMI varies by location, there's no single national dollar figure. As a rough guide, the limit for a family of four ranges from about $27,750/year in low-cost rural areas to $78,550/year in the San Francisco Bay Area. Find your specific limit at HUD's income limit tool or by contacting your local PHA.

### How long is the Section 8 waitlist in my area?

It depends entirely on your local PHA. National averages run 2–5 years, but high-demand cities like New York and Los Angeles can have waits of 8–10+ years. Many PHAs have permanently closed waitlists. Check directly with your local PHA — their website usually shows whether the waitlist is open and, if so, the current estimated wait.

### Can I use my Section 8 voucher in a different state?

Yes. After your initial 12-month lease period in the jurisdiction where you received the voucher, you can move it to another city or state. The process is called "portability." The receiving PHA will take over administration of your voucher. Some PHAs have more robust portability processes than others — ask your current PHA for the specifics.

### Do landlords have to accept Section 8?

Only in states and cities that have Source of Income (SOI) discrimination protections. As of 2026, about 20 states (including California, New York, Washington, Massachusetts, Connecticut, and others) prohibit landlords from refusing tenants solely because they use a voucher. In states without these laws, landlords can decline.

### How much of my rent does Section 8 pay?

The voucher covers the difference between your local PHA's payment standard (based on HUD's Fair Market Rent) and 30% of your adjusted monthly income. If you earn $1,500/month and the payment standard is $1,400, you pay $450 (30% of income) and the voucher pays $950. If you choose an apartment priced above the payment standard, you pay the difference plus your 30%.

### What happens if my income goes up?

Your tenant contribution increases proportionally as your income rises. The voucher doesn't disappear unless your income exceeds the program's income limit. PHAs perform annual income recertifications to adjust your rent share accordingly.

### Can single people get Section 8?

Yes. Single adults, including non-disabled working-age adults, can apply and receive vouchers. However, bedroom size allowances are based on household size, so a single person would receive a voucher sized for a studio or one-bedroom unit. Some PHAs give additional preferences to elderly (62+) or disabled individuals regardless of family status.

### What is a Section 8 inspection?

Before you can move in using a voucher, the unit must pass HUD's Housing Quality Standards (HQS) inspection. An inspector checks that the unit is safe and habitable — working heat, no structural hazards, functioning smoke detectors, adequate space, and so on. Most standard rental apartments pass easily. If a unit fails, the landlord has time to make repairs and schedule a re-inspection.
