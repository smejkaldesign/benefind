## TL;DR

Most parents know about the Child and Dependent Care Tax Credit. Far fewer know about **CCDBG** (the federal Child Care and Development Block Grant), the **Head Start** network, state-level preschool assistance, Dependent Care FSAs, military childcare subsidies, employer-provided care, tribal childcare programs, or the refundable state dependent-care credits in 12 states. Between federal, state, tribal, employer, and tax-side programs, the average family with kids can stack **$5,000-$20,000/year** in childcare savings. This guide walks through every program, who qualifies, and exactly how to apply.

## Why childcare help is so hard to find

Childcare is the second-largest expense for most young families — often more than housing. Average full-time center-based care is about **$15,600/year per child** nationally, and over $25,000/year in high-cost metros. Yet federal childcare help is deliberately fragmented across dozens of programs, each run by different agencies, with different eligibility rules, different application processes, and almost no coordination between them.

Most families qualify for more than they realize. The problem is finding them.

## The big federal programs you already know

Let's start with the two you've probably heard of so we can move on to the ones you haven't.

### The Child and Dependent Care Tax Credit (CDCTC)

A nonrefundable federal tax credit worth **20-35% of qualifying childcare expenses**, up to $3,000 per child for one child or $6,000 for two or more. The exact percentage drops as your income rises. For most families, this works out to **$600-$2,100/year** in reduced taxes.

It's nonrefundable, so it can't reduce your tax liability below zero. If you owe $0 in federal income tax, you get nothing.

### The Child Tax Credit (CTC)

Not strictly a childcare credit, but worth mentioning: up to **$2,000 per child under 17**, of which up to $1,700 is refundable (the Additional Child Tax Credit). If you file taxes and have kids, you almost certainly get this.

OK, on to the ones you probably haven't seen.

## The big one you probably haven't heard of: CCDBG

The **Child Care and Development Block Grant (CCDBG)** is the single largest federal childcare subsidy program. It distributes about **$9 billion/year** to states to help low- and moderate-income working families pay for childcare.

### Who qualifies

Eligibility varies by state, but the federal floor is **85% of state median income (SMI)**. Some states cap eligibility lower (around 150-200% FPL); others go to the full 85% SMI limit. For a family of four, that's roughly **$70,000-$95,000/year** depending on where you live.

To qualify, the parents must be:

- Working, in school, or in job training
- Responsible for a child under 13 (or up to 19 if the child has special needs)
- A US citizen or qualified non-citizen

### What you get

A **subsidy paid directly to your childcare provider**. You pay a small co-pay (based on a sliding scale) and the state covers the rest. For many families, the co-pay is $10-$50 per week for full-time care that would otherwise cost $250-$400/week.

### How to apply

CCDBG is administered by each state's child care licensing or human services agency. Google "[your state] child care assistance program" or "[your state] child care subsidy." Common state names:

- California: CalWORKs Stage 1/2/3, Alternative Payment Program
- Texas: Child Care Services (CCS) via workforce boards
- New York: Child Care Subsidy Program
- Florida: School Readiness Program

### The waitlist problem

Most states don't have enough CCDBG funding to serve every eligible family. Waitlists of 6-18 months are common. **Apply anyway** — put your name in early, because you're not eligible until you're on the list.

## Head Start and Early Head Start

**Head Start** serves kids ages 3-5 from low-income families. **Early Head Start** serves infants and toddlers ages 0-3, plus pregnant women.

### What it is

A comprehensive federal program that provides **free** preschool, health screenings, dental care, mental health services, parenting support, and family advocacy. It's not just childcare — it's a wraparound service.

### Who qualifies

- Families at or below the **federal poverty level** (about $32,150/year for a family of 4 in 2026)
- **10% of slots** are reserved for children with disabilities, regardless of income
- Foster children and homeless children automatically qualify regardless of family income
- TANF and SSI recipients automatically qualify

### How to find a program

Use the **Head Start Locator** at [eclkc.ohs.acf.hhs.gov/center-locator](https://eclkc.ohs.acf.hhs.gov/center-locator). Every county in the US has at least one Head Start program.

### Hours are limited

Standard Head Start runs **school-day hours, school-year schedule** — think 8:30 AM to 2:30 PM, September through May. This is a huge limitation for full-time working parents. Some Head Start sites offer "wraparound" care funded through CCDBG or other sources.

## State-funded pre-K

**43 states plus DC** fund their own pre-K programs, serving over **1.4 million 4-year-olds**. Some are universal; most are targeted to low-income or at-risk kids. These are NOT the same as Head Start — they're state-funded and run through public school districts or partner centers.

### Notable state programs

- **Oklahoma, Florida, Vermont, West Virginia, DC** — universal pre-K for all 4-year-olds
- **Georgia** — universal via Bright from the Start
- **California** — Transitional Kindergarten (TK) now covers all 4-year-olds statewide
- **New York** — universal pre-K in NYC, targeted elsewhere
- **Texas** — free pre-K for income-eligible, military, foster, and English learners

Google "[your state] pre-K" or "[your state] universal pre-K." Most programs are **free** regardless of income, if your state offers universal pre-K.

## The Dependent Care FSA — cut your childcare bill by 30%+

A **Dependent Care Flexible Spending Account (DCFSA)** lets you set aside up to **$5,000/year** pre-tax to pay for childcare. For a family in the 22% federal bracket plus 7% state, that's a **29% discount** on $5,000 of childcare — about **$1,450/year in savings**.

### How it works

- You elect an annual amount during your employer's open enrollment
- Money is deducted from your paycheck before federal income tax, Social Security, and most state taxes
- You submit claims for eligible expenses (daycare, after-school, day camps, nanny) and get reimbursed
- **Use-it-or-lose-it** — any unspent balance at year-end is forfeited (most employers offer a 2.5-month grace period or $640 carryover)

### Requirements

- Your employer must offer a DCFSA
- Both parents must be working, looking for work, or in school
- The child must be under 13 (or a dependent of any age who is incapable of self-care)

### DCFSA vs CDCTC — pick carefully

You can't use the same expenses for both the DCFSA and the CDCTC, though you can use them on different expenses. For most middle-income families, **DCFSA is the better deal**, because the pre-tax benefit is bigger than the 20-35% CDCTC. Use a tax prep tool or consult a CPA to optimize.

## Military childcare subsidies

If you're active-duty military or a DoD civilian, you may qualify for:

### Child Development Centers (CDCs)

On-base childcare with **sliding-scale fees** based on household income. For many junior enlisted families, the cost is a fraction of civilian rates.

### Fee Assistance Programs

If no CDC slot is available, the military pays a **subsidy to an off-base, licensed, NACCRRA-approved provider**, reducing your cost to what you would have paid at the CDC.

- Army: Child Care in Your Neighborhood
- Navy: Child Care in Your Home / Neighborhood
- Air Force: Air Force Fee Assistance
- Marines: Military Child Care in Your Neighborhood (MCCYN)
- Space Force and Coast Guard have similar programs

Start at [childcareaware.org/fee-assistance-respite-care/](https://www.childcareaware.org/fee-assistance-respite-care/).

## Tribal childcare programs

The **Tribal Child Care and Development Fund** provides direct federal childcare subsidies to federally recognized tribes and Alaska Native villages, serving over 44,000 children. Eligibility and amounts vary by tribe — contact your tribal government or social services office.

## Refundable state dependent-care credits

Twelve states offer **refundable** dependent-care tax credits — meaning you get cash back even if you owe no state income tax. This is a much better deal than the federal credit for low-income families.

States with refundable credits (as of 2026):

- Colorado
- Hawaii
- Iowa
- Louisiana
- Maine
- Minnesota
- Nebraska
- New Mexico
- New York
- Oregon
- Vermont
- Wisconsin

Some of these stack on top of the federal credit. In Minnesota, a low-income family can get **over $1,000 back** just from the state credit alone.

## Employer-provided childcare

Increasingly, employers offer on-site or near-site childcare as a benefit, often heavily subsidized. Ask HR about:

- **On-site daycare** (Patagonia, Cisco, Campbell Soup, SAS, and many hospitals)
- **Backup care benefits** (Bright Horizons, care.com — typically 10-20 days/year of subsidized emergency care)
- **Childcare stipends** (some tech companies and startups)
- **Employer matches** to DCFSA

Don't assume these don't exist — benefits have expanded rapidly since 2020, and many employers haven't updated their HR materials.

## The Child Care Tax Credit (state-level)

In addition to the federal CDCTC, **24 states plus DC** offer their own version of the dependent care credit. Some are nonrefundable; 12 are refundable (listed above). Rates range from 10% of federal CDCTC (DC) to 100%+ (Minnesota for low incomes).

## How to stack everything

Here's a realistic example: a family of 4 in Minnesota, both parents working, two kids under 5, household income of $60,000/year, childcare costs of $20,000/year.

| Program                                         | Estimated benefit   |
| ----------------------------------------------- | ------------------- |
| DCFSA ($5K pre-tax)                             | ~$1,450             |
| Federal CDCTC (on remaining $1,000 of expenses) | ~$200               |
| MN refundable credit                            | ~$1,000             |
| CCDBG subsidy (if eligible)                     | ~$6,000-$10,000     |
| **Total**                                       | **~$8,600-$12,650** |

That's about **43-63% of their childcare cost** covered — from programs that are free to apply for and mostly stackable.

## Frequently asked questions

### My kid is in school. Do any of these cover after-school care?

Yes. CCDBG and DCFSA both cover before- and after-school programs. Many state pre-K programs include after-school extended-day options.

### Can I use these programs for a nanny?

Yes, for most of them. DCFSA explicitly allows household employees (nannies, au pairs) as long as you pay them on the books (W-2 or 1099). CCDBG in some states allows "family, friend, and neighbor" care. The CDCTC allows nannies. Head Start does not.

### What about summer camp?

DCFSA and the CDCTC both cover day camps (but NOT overnight camps). If your work-at-home summer routine includes day camp, these save you money.

### I'm self-employed. Can I still get DCFSA?

No — DCFSA requires an employer. But you can still claim the CDCTC, and your state credit if applicable.

### Do grandparents providing care count?

If you pay them and they're not your dependent, yes. If it's free babysitting, no. For DCFSA and CDCTC you need an actual paid-care arrangement (SSN or EIN of the caregiver required).

### How fast can I get CCDBG help?

It depends on your state's waitlist. Some states (Mississippi, Alabama) approve within 30 days if funds are available. Others (California, New York) have 12+ month waitlists. Apply anyway — you're not first in line until you apply.

## The bottom line

The US childcare system is fragmented, but the total amount of help available is much larger than most parents realize. Stack **CCDBG + DCFSA + CDCTC + state credit + employer benefits + Head Start or pre-K if eligible**, and a typical middle-income family can cut their childcare costs by 40-70%.
