## TL;DR

The R&D tax credit is one of the most underused small-business tax breaks in the United States. If your company develops software, hardware, biotech, manufacturing processes, or really anything that requires technical experimentation, you probably qualify. Post-2015, qualified small businesses can use the credit to **offset up to $500,000 a year in payroll taxes** even if they owe zero income tax — which means pre-revenue startups can get real cash back. This guide walks through who qualifies, what activities count, how to calculate it, and how to claim it in 2026.

## What the R&D tax credit actually is

The Research and Development Tax Credit (IRC Section 41) has been around since 1981, but Congress made it **permanent** in 2015 with the PATH Act and added the **payroll tax offset** that makes it valuable for startups. There are actually two components:

- **The Regular R&D Credit** — reduces your federal income tax bill
- **The Payroll Tax Offset** — for qualified small businesses, you can take up to $500,000/year of your credit against the employer portion of Social Security and Medicare payroll taxes (raised from $250,000 in 2022)

If you owe no federal income tax, the payroll offset is the one that matters. You get cash back without having to wait until you're profitable.

**Most states also have their own R&D credits.** California, New York, Texas, Massachusetts, Georgia, and about 35 others run state-level versions that can be stacked on top of the federal credit. Some (Arizona, New Jersey, Minnesota) even have refundable state credits.

## Do you qualify? The four-part test

The IRS uses a "four-part test" to decide if an activity counts as qualified research. Every one of your R&D projects has to pass all four to be credit-eligible.

### 1. Permitted purpose

The work has to be aimed at creating a new or improved product, process, software, technique, formula, or invention. It has to result in **improved function, performance, reliability, or quality**. Pure aesthetic changes don't count. Neither does reverse-engineering someone else's product without adding anything new.

### 2. Technological in nature

The work must rely on principles of **hard sciences** — computer science, engineering, physics, chemistry, biology. Social sciences, economics, and humanities research don't qualify.

### 3. Elimination of uncertainty

At the start of the project, you didn't know whether the thing could be built, how to build it, or what design would work best. This is usually the easiest part to meet — almost all genuine development work involves uncertainty.

### 4. Process of experimentation

You had to iterate. Try things. Evaluate alternatives. Prototype, test, measure, adjust. Modeling, simulation, A/B testing, coding and debugging — these all count.

## Activities that clearly qualify

These are the bread-and-butter R&D credit activities. If your company does any of them, you almost certainly have a credit to claim.

- **Software development** — designing new software, developing new features, optimizing performance, building integrations, debugging (if the debugging is resolving genuine technical uncertainty)
- **Hardware design** — PCB design, mechanical engineering, prototyping, firmware development
- **Manufacturing process improvement** — designing new production lines, improving yield, reducing waste, developing new tooling
- **Biotech and pharmaceuticals** — preclinical research, assay development, molecule screening, clinical trial design
- **Food and beverage** — new recipes and formulations, shelf-life improvements, packaging innovations
- **Architecture and engineering** — developing new structural systems, energy-efficient designs, civil engineering simulations (not routine CAD work)
- **Agriculture** — developing new crop varieties, improved irrigation systems, equipment modifications

## Activities that do NOT qualify

A few things are explicitly excluded by the statute or Treasury regulations:

- Research conducted after commercial production begins
- Adaptation of an existing product to a specific customer's need (unless there's real technical uncertainty)
- Duplication of an existing product or process
- Surveys, market research, consumer testing
- Research funded by someone else (a grant or a client paying for it)
- Research conducted outside the United States
- Social sciences, arts, humanities
- Most routine data collection, quality control, and testing

## What expenses count as "qualified research expenditures" (QREs)

Once you've identified qualified activities, you add up the expenses that went into them. There are four categories of QREs.

### Wages

The biggest bucket. Salaries, bonuses, and wages paid to employees who:

- **Performed** qualified research (engineers, developers, scientists)
- **Directly supervised** qualified research (engineering managers, team leads)
- **Directly supported** qualified research (QA testers, lab technicians, sometimes even prototyping assistants)

You can only count the **percentage of each employee's time** they spent on qualified research. If a software engineer spent 80% of 2026 on new feature development and 20% on production support, you can count 80% of their wages as QREs.

### Supplies

Tangible materials consumed in R&D that aren't capital assets. This includes:

- Cloud computing costs for dev/test environments (yes, AWS and Vercel bills count)
- Prototype parts, 3D printing materials, breadboards, sensors
- Lab consumables, chemicals, test samples
- Pre-production tooling

General office supplies, rent, and utilities do NOT count as supplies.

### Contract research

If you outsource R&D work to a third party (a dev shop, a design firm, a CRO), you can claim **65% of those contract costs** as QREs, provided you retain substantial rights to the research and you bear the economic risk.

### Computer rental / cloud services

A 2015 update explicitly allowed cloud hosting costs for development, testing, and experimentation as QREs. This is a big deal for software companies — your AWS, GCP, and Azure bills for dev/staging environments count.

## How to calculate the credit

There are two methods. You can pick whichever gives you more.

### Regular Research Credit

The "regular" method calculates a base amount using historical research spending and pays you 20% of the excess of current QREs over that base. It's complicated and usually not worth it for newer companies without years of R&D history.

### Alternative Simplified Credit (ASC)

This is what most small businesses use. The formula is:

- Calculate your average QREs for the prior **3 years**
- Take **14%** of the amount by which current-year QREs exceed **50% of that 3-year average**
- If you have no prior-year QREs, you get **6%** of current-year QREs

**Example**: Startup with $500,000 in 2026 QREs and no prior R&D spending. The ASC credit is 6% × $500,000 = **$30,000**.

**Example**: Same startup, but they had $200,000 of QREs in 2023–2025 (average $67k/year). Now they're at $500,000 in 2026. Credit = 14% × ($500,000 − $33,333) = **$65,333**.

These numbers grow fast. A Series A company with 15 engineers and $3M of qualified wages often sees credits of $200,000+ per year.

## The payroll tax offset — why this is a big deal for startups

Here's where it gets interesting for pre-revenue companies. Under the PATH Act, a **"qualified small business"** can use up to **$500,000/year** of its R&D credit to offset the **employer portion of FICA payroll taxes** (Social Security and Medicare).

You qualify as a "qualified small business" if:

- You have less than **$5 million** in gross receipts for the current year, AND
- You have **no gross receipts from any year more than 5 years** before the current year

So a 4-year-old startup with $2M ARR qualifies. A bootstrapped 10-year-old company doesn't — even if it's small. The election is made on your timely-filed corporate return (Form 6765), and the offset is then applied quarterly via Form 8974.

**What this means in practice**: a pre-revenue startup with $500k of QREs gets a $30,000 credit. Instead of waiting until profitability, they apply it against next quarter's payroll taxes and get $30,000 of real cash back immediately. That's close to three months of runway for a small team.

## Documentation — the audit-proof version

The IRS doesn't love loose claims. If you claim the R&D credit, you need to be able to show:

- **A list of qualified projects** — ideally one per product or major feature
- **Time tracking** — even approximate, showing what % of each employee's time went to R&D
- **Technical uncertainty documentation** — what problem you were trying to solve and why the solution wasn't obvious at the start
- **Process of experimentation** — the alternatives you considered and tested
- **Supply and contractor invoices** tied to specific projects

Good documentation practice: at the end of every year, have each technical team member spend 30 minutes writing a short memo on what they worked on and roughly what share was R&D. Combine with git commit history for engineering work.

## How to claim it — the 2026 process

1. **Identify QREs** — work with an R&D tax specialist or use software like Neo.Tax, Tri-Merit, or MainStreet to calculate.
2. **File Form 6765** with your corporate income tax return (1120, 1120-S, or 1065).
3. **Elect the payroll offset** on Form 6765 if you're a qualified small business.
4. **File Form 8974** quarterly with your Form 941 payroll tax return to apply the credit.
5. **Keep all documentation** for at least 7 years in case of audit.

Many small businesses work with a specialist firm that charges a flat fee or a percentage (typically 10–25%) of the credit claimed. For simpler cases, software platforms now handle it for under $1,500.

## Amending prior returns

Here's a free bonus: **you can amend returns for the past 3 open tax years** and claim R&D credits you missed. If you've been developing software since 2023 and never claimed the credit, you may be able to go back and get cash back for 2023 and 2024 right now.

## Frequently asked questions

### Does my company need to have a formal R&D department?

No. The activity qualifies, not the department name. A solo founder writing code in their apartment can have qualified research expenditures.

### Can I claim the credit if I take SBIR grants?

Yes, but you can't claim expenses funded by the grant. You can claim the other qualified expenses on the same projects.

### What if my company is an LLC?

LLCs taxed as partnerships or S-corps can still claim the credit, which flows through to the owners' personal returns (or for S-corps, to the company's payroll offset). Single-member LLCs taxed as disregarded entities claim on Schedule C or the owner's return.

### What's the audit risk?

R&D credits are a common IRS focus, but audits are usually manageable if your documentation is solid. The number-one reason claims get denied is insufficient documentation, not ineligible activities.

### Do I need an R&D tax advisor?

For small claims under $50,000, software platforms work well. For larger or more complex claims, or if you're going to amend prior years, a specialist firm is worth the fee.
