## TL;DR

Opportunity Zones let you invest unrealized capital gains into designated low-income communities and defer — or eliminate — the taxes on those gains. The **One Big Beautiful Bill Act (OBBBA)**, signed July 4, 2025, made the program permanent, added a **30% rural bonus** (vs. 10% standard), and set December 31, 2026 as the date when deferred pre-2027 gains become taxable. If you're sitting on capital gains from a stock sale, real estate exit, or business sale, this guide covers everything you need to know to act before that deadline.

## What Opportunity Zones are

Opportunity Zones (OZs) are census tracts designated as low-income or economically distressed communities where the federal government offers substantial tax incentives to attract private investment. The program was created by the Tax Cuts and Jobs Act of 2017 (IRC Sections 1400Z-1 and 1400Z-2) and administered jointly by the IRS, Treasury Department, and CDFI Fund.

There are currently **8,764 designated OZ census tracts** across all 50 states, Washington D.C., and 5 U.S. territories. Current designations remain in effect through the end of 2028.

The mechanics are straightforward: you realize a capital gain, invest that gain into a **Qualified Opportunity Fund (QOF)** within 180 days, and the fund deploys the capital into businesses or real estate within a designated zone. In exchange, you get three layers of tax benefit depending on how long you hold the investment.

### What qualifies as a capital gain?

The 180-day investment window applies to any capital gain — not just real estate. Qualifying sources include:

- Sale of publicly traded stock or securities
- Sale of a privately held business or business interest
- Sale of investment real estate
- Partnership gain pass-throughs
- Installment sale gains (each installment starts its own 180-day clock)

## The three tax benefits

### 1. Capital gains deferral

When you invest qualifying gains into a QOF within 180 days of realizing them, you defer federal tax on those gains. You don't pay the tax when you make the gain — you pay it later.

**For pre-2027 investments:** Deferred gains are recognized on the earlier of the date you sell your QOF investment or **December 31, 2026**. This means if you invested in a QOF in 2021 and are still holding it, you owe tax on that original deferred gain on your 2026 federal return regardless of whether you've sold anything.

**For post-2026 investments (OZ 2.0):** The deferral period is 5 years from the date of investment. After 5 years, the deferred gain is recognized.

### 2. Basis step-up on the deferred gain

When you eventually pay tax on your deferred gain, you may pay less than the original amount because your basis gets stepped up.

| Holding Period | Basis Step-Up | Effect |
|---|---|---|
| Less than 5 years | 0% | Pay tax on full original gain |
| 5 years (standard QOF) | 10% | Pay tax on 90% of original gain |
| 5 years (Rural QOF) | **30%** | Pay tax on 70% of original gain |
| 7 years (pre-2020 only) | 15% total | This benefit has expired for new investments |

The **Rural Qualified Opportunity Fund (QROF)** bonus is a major 2025 change. Rural zones now offer triple the standard basis step-up — 30% vs. 10% — making investments in rural-designated OZs significantly more valuable.

### 3. Permanent exclusion of QOF appreciation

This is the program's biggest benefit. If you hold your QOF investment for **10 or more years**, your basis in the QOF investment is stepped up to fair market value on the date of sale. In practice, this means you pay **zero capital gains tax** on all the appreciation your QOF investment generated during the hold period.

The appreciation exclusion applies only to the QOF investment gains — not to the original deferred gain (which was recognized and taxed at year 5 or December 31, 2026 for pre-2027 investors). But for long-hold investors, this is a powerful benefit: 10 years of investment growth, completely untaxed on exit.

**Post-2026 investments** (OZ 2.0) retain the 10-year exclusion, but with a 30-year cap — the basis steps up to FMV on the 30th anniversary at the latest, rather than indefinitely.

## 2026 updates: what the OBBBA changed

The One Big Beautiful Bill Act (signed July 4, 2025) made sweeping changes to the Opportunity Zone program. Here's what matters for 2026 investors.

### Program made permanent

The OZ program was previously set to sunset for new investments on December 31, 2026. The OBBBA removed that sunset, making the program permanent. New investments made in 2027 and beyond are fully eligible for all OZ benefits.

### New zone designations starting 2027

States can nominate new census tracts in a 90-day window beginning July 1, 2026. New zones take effect January 1, 2027 and run in 10-year cycles. Existing 8,764 zones remain active through end of 2028, so there will be a 2-year overlap period.

New eligibility rules are stricter: median family income cannot exceed 70% of area median (down from 80%), and tracts are disqualified if median income has risen above 125% of area median (anti-gentrification rule). The "contiguous tract" rule that allowed adjacent non-qualifying areas to be included has been repealed.

### Rural bonus (30% step-up)

Qualified Rural Opportunity Funds get a 30% basis step-up after 5 years instead of the standard 10%. The substantial improvement test for existing buildings is also halved — 15 months instead of 30 months to invest an amount equal to the adjusted basis of the building.

### December 31, 2026 recognition date

Pre-2027 investors face a critical deadline: deferred gains become taxable on December 31, 2026 regardless of hold period. If you have a QOF investment with deferred gains, plan your estimated tax payments for Q4 2026 accordingly.

### Puerto Rico blanket designation repealed

Puerto Rico's blanket OZ designation (which made the entire island eligible) ends December 31, 2026. Puerto Rico tracts will be eligible to reapply under the new designation process.

## How to invest through a Qualified Opportunity Fund

Investing in Opportunity Zones requires going through a **Qualified Opportunity Fund (QOF)**. You cannot invest directly in an OZ business or property and claim the benefits — the investment must flow through a QOF.

### Step 1: Realize a capital gain

The clock starts when you realize the gain. You have 180 days to invest in a QOF. For partnership gains, the 180-day clock can start from the end of the partnership's tax year or from the date the gain was realized — you choose whichever gives you more time.

### Step 2: Find and invest in a QOF within 180 days

QOFs are self-certifying entities — any corporation or partnership (including an LLC taxed as a partnership) can become a QOF by filing IRS Form 8996 annually. There are two main paths:

**Invest in an existing QOF:** Numerous real estate developers, private equity funds, and community development organizations have established QOFs. Platforms like Opportunity Zone Database, Novogradac, and CDFI Fund's mapping tools can help you find options.

**Sponsor your own QOF:** If you're a developer or operator investing your own gains into your own OZ project, you can form a QOF directly. This is common for real estate developers with large capital gains reinvesting into their next OZ project.

### Step 3: File Form 8949 to elect deferral

On your tax return for the year you realized the gain, include Form 8949 and check the box to elect OZ gain deferral. This is how you officially notify the IRS you've reinvested into a QOF.

### Step 4: QOF compliance — the 90% test

The QOF you invest in must maintain at least **90% of its assets in Qualified Opportunity Zone Property**, tested semi-annually. There are monthly penalties for non-compliance. Before investing in any QOF, verify they have a compliance monitoring process in place.

### Step 5: Hold and track your investment

The holding period drives your benefits. Five years gets you the basis step-up. Ten years eliminates gains tax on QOF appreciation. Make sure you're tracking both the original deferred gain recognition date (December 31, 2026 for pre-2027 investors) and your QOF anniversary dates.

### QOF investing rules summary

| Rule | Requirement |
|---|---|
| Investment window | Within 180 days of gain realization |
| Entity type | Corporation or partnership (including LLC) |
| Asset test | 90% of assets in QOZ Property (semi-annual) |
| Self-certification | IRS Form 8996 filed annually |
| Ineligible businesses | Liquor stores, golf courses, country clubs, massage parlors, hot tubs, suntan facilities, racetracks, gambling |
| Substantial improvement | Invest equal to adjusted basis of existing buildings within 15 months (rural) or 30 months (standard) |

## State-level supplements

Several states have enacted their own Opportunity Zone incentives on top of the federal benefits:

- **Ohio:** 10% state tax credit on OZ investments, up to $1 million per project
- **Alabama, West Virginia, Mississippi:** State-level OZ investment tax credits
- **Maryland, Virginia, New Jersey:** Additional state incentives for OZ investments in designated areas

Many states conform to the federal OZ treatment for state income tax purposes, meaning you defer the state tax alongside the federal tax. A few states (notably California) do not conform and tax the deferred gain in the year it's realized. Check your state's conformity status with a tax advisor.

## Frequently asked questions

### What's the December 31, 2026 deadline about?

If you invested capital gains in a QOF before 2027, those deferred gains become taxable on December 31, 2026 — regardless of whether you've sold your QOF investment. This is the original statutory recognition date. You'll owe tax on the original (stepped-up) deferred gain on your 2026 return. Plan estimated tax payments accordingly. This does NOT affect the 10-year exclusion on QOF appreciation — you can still hold the QOF investment for 10 years and exclude the appreciation.

### Do I have to invest in real estate?

No. You can invest in OZ businesses, not just real estate. A business qualifies if it's located in a QOZ, derives 50%+ of gross income from within the zone, uses a substantial portion of its tangible property in the zone, and conducts its business there. That said, most QOF capital in practice flows into real estate because of the clear tangible asset requirements.

### Can I invest my own business sale proceeds?

Yes. If you sell a business and realize a capital gain, you can invest those gains in a QOF within 180 days. This is one of the most common OZ investment scenarios — a founder exits and reinvests in OZ real estate or a new OZ-based business.

### What happens if I sell my QOF investment before 5 years?

You lose the basis step-up benefits and pay capital gains tax on the full original deferred gain (minus whatever basis step-up you qualified for). You still pay regular capital gains tax on any QOF appreciation. The 10-year exclusion is also forfeited.

### What's the rural bonus and how do I know if a zone is rural?

Rural QOZs receive a 30% basis step-up (vs. 10% standard) and a shorter substantial improvement window (15 months). Rural zones are census tracts not part of an urbanized area with a population of 50,000 or more. The CDFI Fund's mapping tool identifies which zones are rural. Qualified Rural Opportunity Funds must self-certify as QROFs and hold 90%+ of assets in rural QOZ property.

### What's the difference between the deferred gain and QOF appreciation?

These are two separate buckets. The **deferred gain** is the original capital gain you invested in the QOF — you eventually pay tax on this (at the reduced stepped-up amount) on December 31, 2026 or year 5. The **QOF appreciation** is the growth your QOF investment earns after you invest. If you hold 10+ years, you owe zero tax on the appreciation. They're taxed separately and differently.

### Are there Opportunity Zone funds for small investors?

Yes. Many QOFs have minimum investments starting at $25,000-$50,000. Some community development QOFs have even lower minimums. The 180-day window is the main constraint — you need to identify a QOF before the window closes after realizing your gain.

## The bottom line

Opportunity Zones are one of the few tax programs where the math is genuinely compelling for the right investor. A pre-2027 investor with $500,000 in deferred gains investing in a rural QOF gets a $150,000 basis reduction (30% step-up) — paying tax on $350,000 instead of $500,000. Then 10+ years later, the appreciation on the entire QOF investment exits tax-free.

The December 31, 2026 recognition date is a hard deadline for pre-2027 investors. If you have deferred gains, plan now.
