## TL;DR

Let's be direct: the internet is full of ads for "free government cash grants for single mothers" — and almost all of them are scams. **The federal government does not give personal cash grants to individuals**, and no legitimate program promises money for "anything you want." But real federal and state programs absolutely exist and they can cover **rent, food, child care, health care, cash assistance, and college**. This guide separates the real programs from the scams and shows you exactly how to apply.

## First: what you should know about "grant" scams

If you've searched "free grants for single mothers" you've probably seen websites promising $5,000 to $30,000 in "federal grant money" with no application, no credit check, and no need to pay it back. Some ask for a "processing fee" up front. Some ask for your bank account "to deposit your grant." **All of them are scams.**

Here's how to spot them:

- **They claim Pell Grants are for "single mothers."** Pell Grants are for college students of any household type, not single mothers specifically.
- **They ask for an upfront fee.** Real government grants never charge an application fee.
- **They promise guaranteed money.** No real program guarantees anything without an application.
- **They claim to be "not widely advertised" or "hidden."** Every real federal program is on benefits.gov.
- **They use urgency language.** "Apply today — only 100 grants left!"

The actual federal rules are clear: **Individuals cannot apply for federal grants.** Federal grants go to states, nonprofits, research institutions, and sometimes small businesses — never directly to individuals for personal expenses. Anyone who tells you otherwise is trying to steal your money or identity.

That said, there is real help out there. Lots of it. It just comes through named programs, not "grants."

## The 10 real programs single mothers should actually check

These are the programs that most commonly help single-mom households. We've noted the rough income cutoff for each, but always double-check your state's specific rules.

### 1. TANF (Temporary Assistance for Needy Families)

This is the closest thing to a "grant" for single mothers. It's monthly **cash assistance** for very low-income families with children under 18 (or 19 if the child is still in high school). It's federally funded, state-administered, and benefit amounts vary wildly — from about $200/month in low-benefit states to $900+/month in more generous states. Time limits are typically **60 months lifetime**.

- Income limit: varies by state, usually 50–100% of FPL
- Benefit: $200–$900/month
- Apply: through your state human services office

### 2. SNAP (food stamps)

One of the easiest programs to qualify for and one of the most valuable. A single mother with two kids earning $2,800/month gross probably qualifies for about $500–$700/month in SNAP benefits. See our [complete SNAP eligibility guide for 2026](/blog/snap-eligibility-2026).

- Income limit: 130% of FPL (about $2,888/month for a 3-person household)
- Benefit: up to $768/month for a 3-person household
- Apply: through your state SNAP portal

### 3. WIC (Women, Infants, and Children)

Specifically for pregnant women, breastfeeding mothers, and children under 5. It's not cash — it's nutrition-specific vouchers for formula, milk, eggs, produce, cereal, whole grains, and peanut butter. The benefit is worth roughly $40–$100/month per eligible person.

- Income limit: 185% of FPL
- Benefit: nutrition vouchers valued $40–$100/month per person
- Apply: through your county WIC office; same-day service in most places

### 4. Medicaid and CHIP

Single mothers and their children have the most generous Medicaid eligibility rules of any group. In the 40 Medicaid-expansion states, any adult under 138% of FPL qualifies. For children, eligibility goes up to 200–300% of FPL in most states (CHIP covers kids when parents earn slightly too much for Medicaid).

- Income limit: 138% of FPL for mom, 200–300% for kids (state-dependent)
- Benefit: full health insurance, dental, vision in most states
- Apply: healthcare.gov or your state Medicaid agency

### 5. Child Care and Development Fund (CCDF) subsidies

This is the program that actually helps pay for day care — and it's the one single mothers most often miss because it has a bureaucratic name. States run it under different marketing names: "Working Connections Child Care" in Washington, "CalWORKs Child Care" in California, "Child Care Works" in Pennsylvania.

If you qualify, the state pays your child care provider directly. In most states you have a small co-pay based on income. This is worth **$5,000–$15,000+ per child per year**.

- Income limit: usually 85% of state median income (higher than FPL cutoffs)
- Benefit: covers most or all of day care costs
- Apply: through your state child care subsidy office

### 6. Head Start and Early Head Start

Free, high-quality preschool and infant care for low-income children. Head Start covers ages 3–5; Early Head Start covers pregnant women and children 0–3. Most programs include meals, transportation, and health screenings.

- Income limit: 100% of FPL (priority), with some slots up to 130%
- Benefit: free preschool + wraparound services
- Apply: through your local Head Start agency

### 7. Section 8 Housing Choice Voucher

The largest federal housing program. You pay 30% of your income toward rent and the voucher covers the rest (up to a "payment standard" set by your local housing authority). Waitlists are long — often years in major metros — but the program is worth enormous amounts over time.

- Income limit: 50% of area median income; 75% of new vouchers must go to households under 30% AMI
- Benefit: pays most of your rent
- Apply: through your local Public Housing Agency; join the waitlist as early as possible

### 8. LIHEAP (energy bill assistance)

Helps cover heating and cooling bills. Especially useful in winter. See our [step-by-step LIHEAP application guide](/blog/how-to-apply-for-liheap-heating-assistance).

- Income limit: 150% of FPL
- Benefit: $200–$1,500 per winter, plus crisis assistance
- Apply: through your state LIHEAP agency in October/November

### 9. Pell Grants (for college)

This is one program that actually is called a "grant" — but it's for **college students**, not single mothers as a category. That said, single mothers who enroll in college almost always qualify because income is very commonly below the threshold. Max Pell Grant for 2026 is **$7,395 per year** and it doesn't have to be repaid.

- Income limit: depends on EFC calculated from FAFSA; most recipients' families earn under $60k
- Benefit: up to $7,395/year
- Apply: fill out the FAFSA at fafsa.ed.gov

### 10. Earned Income Tax Credit (EITC)

A refundable tax credit worth up to **$7,830 per year** for a single filer with three qualifying children. "Refundable" means you get the money even if you owe no tax. This is real cash sent to you after you file your return. Millions of eligible people never claim it because they don't file a return.

- Income limit: $56,838 for a single filer with 3 kids
- Benefit: up to $7,830 cash back
- Apply: file your federal tax return (free options at IRS Free File)

## Other real help that's not on benefits.gov

A few more things worth knowing about that single mothers often miss:

### Child support enforcement

The federal Office of Child Support Enforcement helps find non-custodial parents, establish paternity, and collect support. This isn't a benefit, but it's money you may be legally owed. Free to use.

### The Supplemental Security Income (SSI) program

If you or your child has a disability, SSI pays monthly cash benefits. Kids can qualify for SSI based on their own disability even if the parent is working. Max federal SSI is around **$943/month** in 2026, and most states add a supplement on top.

### Diaper banks and formula programs

Diapers are a huge expense that no federal program covers (SNAP doesn't pay for them, WIC only covers formula). Local diaper banks through the National Diaper Bank Network distribute free diapers to families in need — find one at nationaldiaperbanknetwork.org.

### Local emergency assistance

Most counties have an emergency assistance fund for one-time crises (eviction prevention, utility reconnection, car repair needed to keep a job). Usually run through community action agencies. Call 211.

### Nonprofit scholarships for single mothers

These are not government programs, but they are real. Jeannette Rankin Foundation, Patsy Takemoto Mink Education Foundation, and Women's Independence Scholarship Program all specifically fund low-income single mothers going back to school. Applications are competitive but worth it.

## Frequently asked questions

### Are there really no federal cash grants for single mothers?

Not as "grants" in the sense those scam sites describe. There is no application at the federal government that gives you thousands of dollars in cash with no strings attached. TANF is the closest thing — monthly cash assistance administered through your state — and it's what most "free grant" scams are misrepresenting.

### What about programs like "Safelink" or "Lifeline" phone service?

Lifeline is a real FCC program that provides discounted or free phone and internet service to low-income households. Average benefit is $9.25/month ($34.25/month on tribal lands). You can apply at lifelinesupport.org.

### Is there any way to get a lump sum of cash from the government?

The only real path to a lump sum from the federal government is the **EITC** and **Child Tax Credit** at tax time. These are refundable credits — meaning you get cash back even if you owe no tax. For a single mother with 2–3 kids and low earned income, this can total **$8,000–$13,000 every spring**. That's the closest thing to a "government grant" that actually exists.

### How do I avoid scams?

Three rules: (1) never pay an application fee for a government benefit; (2) never give your bank account info before you've received an approval letter; (3) only use .gov sites or your state's official benefits portal to apply.

### Do I qualify for anything if I'm undocumented?

Most federal programs are restricted to citizens, permanent residents, and certain other legal statuses. Your U.S.-citizen children, however, qualify for everything they'd qualify for as citizens — so you can still apply on their behalf. A handful of states (California, Illinois, New York) also run state-funded versions of programs that include undocumented residents.
